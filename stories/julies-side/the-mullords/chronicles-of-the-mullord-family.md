## *The family history created by*

*C H R O N I C L E S*

*Of the*

*MULLORD FAMILY*

*O N E  H U N D R E D  Y E A R S*

*By Henry Mullord of "Heather View", Broadstone, Aged 72,*

*The eldest living member, in this year of 1921.*

*Synopsis*

*1.  Henry Mullord, George Alexander Mullord Senior; and his Son; - George Mullord Junior, came from Scotland to London in 1820 (about).*

*2.  Henry Mullord was, and remained; a Bachelor.*

*3 . George Alexander Mullord Senior, a Widower; married a second wife in London, a Miss Anne Scantlebury.*

*4.  This marriage produced eight children; viz.: -William, Kate, Eliza, Mary, Nancy, Thomas, Henry, Alexander.*

*5.  William had two children: - Molly and William:-  his wife then died, and he married again, a widow    named Mrs. King, who had two girls previously, Named Alice and Eliza.*

*6.   Kate remained a spinster.*

*7.   Mary married a man named Kedge, and had two children: - Mary and William.*

*8.   Eliza married a man named Warner, and had two children Eliza and Willie.*

*9.   Nancy married John Ward, and had no children.*

*10. Thomas married Emma Hoadley, and had ten children:- Emma, Thomas, Rebecca, Rhoda, Alfred, Hannah, Walter and Henry and four that died.*

*11. Henry married Sarah Blackborough, and had nine children: -*

*Henry, Emma, George Christopher, Kate, Alfred, Aleck, Albert and Amy.*

*12 . Alexander married Annie Evans, seven children: - Annie, Alice, Emma,*

*Rhoda, Nellie, Aleck and John.*

*The above category gives us all the children and grandchildren of old George Alexander Mullord, the man who came from Scotland just after the Battle of Waterloo; and who proved indeed a Fruitful Vine Tree.*

*Many of these grandchildren have married, and themselves had children and grandchildren. But the record of all this later progeny I will leave to other Chroniclers, and will proceed to give historical and anecdotal reminiscences of the various people in the above genealogy.*

*The name of "Mullord" is unique, I have never, all though my life, heard or read of any other family in the Kingdom bearing this name. You do not come across it in history. It is not on the famous roll of names at Battle Abbey. It is not in Westminster Abbey; nor at St. Pauls Cathedral. It is rather an "outlandish" name. There are plenty of "Millards"; and there is the famous "Mailard" who led the storming of the Bastille in the French Revolution. But one can hardly imagine any of the Mullords storming the Bastille; they would be much more likely to wait in the crowd, till the other man did it. Personally, and as a member of the clan, I would always prefer to applaud from a discreet distance, than to be the hero of the most glorious and bloody combat since the siege of Troy.*

*Thinking over all the Scotch names I know, I have come to the conclusion that "Mullord" is a corruption from "Macleod". A hundred years ago, people who tramped from Scotland to London, would not be the sort of people to write their names with any frequency. A name was only spoken verbally, by anybody. Now! "Macleod" is often pronounced "Macloud" and "M'loud"; therefore why not "Mulloud", and "Mullord". At the time we speak of, misspent names and words were common enough, every old tombstone has some word spelt wrongly; School Education was not for anyone but the rich. As an instance; I recollect John Harris, who was a great chum of the Mullords, (his portrait, with my Father, Uncle Alick and John Ward, in a group, hangs in my kitchen now.) Harris was a Master Builder employing many men, and he was said to be worth ten thousand pounds. Yet he could not read! And although the Scotch Mullords that came to London, could read; (in fact, were said to be quite "brainy" men), one can understand that the word "M'loud" being rendered "Mullord", it would be allowed to pass, as a matter of no importance.*

*Beginning the history again at No. 1, Henry Mullord, the Bachelor, had been discharged from the Army. Every man had been in the Army or Navy, during the many years of Napoleonic War, and this Henry Mullord had been in the Light Dragoons, and had fought at the Battle of Waterloo! (His light curved sabre was given to my Father, after whom I had it, and I believe it is now in the possession of Henry Mullord at Balham). The war being over, and! Old Bony" as they called Napoleon Buonaparte, safely tucked away on his lonely island in the Pacific Ocean, our soldier Mullord tramped to London; and somehow, History says not how, obtained the post of Beadle, Myrmidon and Guardian of the door; At King's College Hospital. This hospital was then a dignified classical Building in Gower Street, and is now no more, but then it was a part of beautiful old London. It had a Greek facade, and looked like a miniature of the British Museum. It was surrounded by Plane trees and Lilacs, and had spacious grounds around it, and Big heavy iron railings in front, with gold leaf on their spear tops. Here my Father went to see his Uncle Henry, in (about) 1885* ***(should read 1855 by Henry`s age A.J.M. 2000)*** *, and he found him sitting in a big hooded hall chair, which was like a sentry box. He was wearing knee breeches; a cocked hat; and round his shoulders a huge Bear Skin Cape; and he held in his hand, his wand of office; a long black rod with a bright brass tip. This description I listened to as a child of six, when father returned, and we were sitting at dinner in Canal Terrace, Islington.*

*Now let us see what sort of man was this warrior ancestor of ours; who peers at us out of the shadows of time, like a suit of old armour in the garret of an ancient manor house.*

*His portrait is before me now; when I sit at dinner. This portrait came into my Father's possession when the old soldier died. It seems, that he was much liked and esteemed by the many professors and students of the College; of which, to them, he seemed to be a part; so long had he been there, (about 35 years I think). Well! He was so liked, that these Professors and students raised a sum of money to have his portrait lithographed. It was drawn on stone by one of the pupils of the celebrated Zoffany. Unfortunately the pupil had not the ability of the Master, it being rather a feeble bit of Art; but it is a very good portrait, for you can see in it the likeness to all the Mullords. The subscribers each had a copy; which cost them a guinea per head and one copy they gave to Uncle Henry, with suitable speeches. And that copy came to my Father when the old man died. It is now in front of me in my meal room, so that I look at it every day.*

*Now this portrait seems to me to express all the natural characteristics of the Clan.*

*I can see old Uncle William in it, Aunt Kate, Uncle Tom, and my Father. The round apple cheeks, broad forehead, little side-whiskers, and general stockiness of head and neck. He is a comfortable looking man. He smiles at you with a rather fatuous expression, as if he is saying: "I know I am well off! I have plenty to eat and drink my glass of beer and my pipe. I am settled for life; and thank God! I have no wife to bother me with ceaseless chatter about nothing, or to "boss" me, and tell me I ought to try and "get on in the world". I am contented; and! Contentment is a continual feast!" All this is what he felt, if he did not say it. His phlegmatic nature would want the spur of outside circumstance to make him "get up and get" as the Yankees say.*

*Well! There he is, in his little niche of fame in the history of the Mullords, and I must confess to great affection for him, as a good citizen that lived his life serenely. And who was respected, and never did harm to any fellow creature.*

*We will now go back to No. 1 George Alexander Mullord widower, first. For I never saw the Father; He died before I was born, but this son George I knew quite well; and in one thing to my sorrow; for he was a bootmaker, and by force of character insisted on, and compelled, all his brothers and sisters, and most of their children, to wear the boots he made. I was one of his victims. He had certain stock lasts and stock sizes, and if the boot did not fit you, your foot had to grow into the right shape to fit the boot. Hence the big bone below the big toe of each of my feet became enlarged to an enormous projection, causing weird astonishment in the mind of any modern Boot purveyor whom I patronise, and exquisite pain to myself for months after I buy a new pair of boots. Yes! He was a bad bootmaker, but a good Christian! That is, a good Christian according to the standards of that period. He seldom smiled, was somewhat morose, and looked with great suspicion on any worldly pleasures. To go to a Theatre or to read Shakespeare, was shaking hands with the Devil, a pack of cards was called the Devil's play things. He had probably a firm belief that any and all people who held a different view of religion to himself, not only Buddhists, Mohammedans, and followers of Roman Catholicism, but worshippers in English Churches where a cross was exhibited as a Symbol, and where incense or candles were burnt and little boys in night-gowns marched in procession and sang sweet voiced anthems, stood a very good chance of being burnt in Hell for ever and ever.*

*All this was not his intentional fault. It was the Puritan teaching of children by the Scotch Descendants of Jennie Geddes and The Old Covenanters, and the complaisant self satisfaction inherited by most of the Mullord Clan, finding expression in firm belief in one's own judgement and a contempt for that of other people. But George had many good qualities; He was Sober, Industrious, Honest and Frugal. He had found a position as assistant to a Goswell Road bootmaker, when he arrived in London, the bootmaker had died, leaving a daughter; George married the Daughter, (and the business). He had two boys, George and Thomas, and one girl. The shop was at the side of the Court where now is King's, the Account boot makers; Many a time I have been in the old Boot shop, Uncle George vigorously nailing boots by candlelight, (no gas then) while I and the two boys made squibs and crackers by candlelight in the shop parlour; (On one occasion, the whole lot of our "Stuff" "blew up", singeing the red hair and eyebrows of Thomas, and seriously damaging his hand). George junior became my Father's apprentice at Die Sinking, and Thomas became a good carpenter, and a man of gentlemanly tastes and appearances; this may be partly attributed to the refined air of Stoke Newington, where Thomas may be living now, and old man of about 75. His Father had found the competition of "Ready Made" Boots and shoes of elegant shape and infinite variety, exhibited in brilliantly lit up Plate Glass windows; were luring away from him, the "Elite" of his Goswell Road customers, and, at last, disgusted, he retired to the seclusion of De Foe Road, Stoke Newington, where he bought and lived in, a small house, and "carried on" a small "snobbing" business, down in the front kitchen. And incidentally gave to the local Methodist Tabernacle the benefit of his views. For he was quite capable of going in the pulpit, or on the platform, when asked to do so.*

*His wife had died, and he had lived a Widower's life until he was 70, when he, being taken with an illness that kept him in bed for a fortnight, was nursed and looked after by an elderly lady, a member of the chapel. When he got well; he married this lady, with the consequence, that! "Mirabile dictu", in due time a baby was born. He being 70 and the lady being of an age that is looked upon as precluding a family. The baby lived, and may be alive now, in which case she would be about 45 years old.*

*The old man with his second wife continued to reside in De Foe Road until he reached the age of eighty-five, when he died. I went to see him on his deathbed. He was surrounded with friends and was very happy; full of faith and belief that he was entering into a new life. In a dreamy hysterical ecstasy; he said he could see the wings of angels.*

*And so passed away a very strong minded, gaunt featured, but not ill looking, six foot high Scotsman. Who! Taken in all and all, was a credit to the name of Mullord, as a plain straightforward honest hard working man.*

*We must now begin again as it were, and having cleared out of the way two of the original Scotch Trio, namely Henry Mullord the warrior, and George Mullord junior, the bootmaker We come to! The most important of the three, the elder one, On arriving in London, this George Alexander found employment at once with a Clerkenwell jeweller, named Scantlebury.*

*Scantlebury was a Freeman of the City of London, and was a highly respected Tradesman.*

*He made a speciality of Court Shoe Buckles. It had been a wonderfully lucrative business. From Tudor times and before, every gentleman wore handsome buckles in his shoes.      Those who were Courtiers and Princes had real diamonds in their buckles; Swankers and swaggerers had Paste imitation diamonds. The sober commoners and city merchants had faceted steel gems encrusted on their shoes. And a pair of shoe buckles given by Louis Quartoise at Versailles to a "Maintenon", or another pair given by our Prince Regent to a Miss            Clifford at Bath, might easily cost five hundred guineas, while John Gilpin, the Cheapside Merchant, might buy his pair for a guinea.*

*Scantlebury had been for years making these buckles for a fashionable jewellers in Bond Street, and our grandmother; his daughter, used to I tell the tale; that one day her father took her with him, when carrying to this Bond Street shop some special work.    And while she was standing in the shop, The KING (probably George the Fourth) came out of the Jewellers private office, and in passing through, observed her, and chucking her under the chin, said, "You are a pretty little lass! What is your name!"*

*And pretty little lass our George Alexander Mullord thought her too! For a few years after, (when shoe buckles were going out of fashion, and trousers and boots were coming to be the everyday wear, and Scantlebury was getting old and less prosperous,) our Scotch adventurer captured the heart of Anne Scantlebury, and was allowed by the old man to marry her.*

*Our George Alexander did not prosper financially, the buckle business got worse and worse, but he and Anne, between them, managed to have a very large family, as stated in the Pedigree, and then, unfortunately, when there was only just enough money earned to keep things going, he died.*

*It seems; he came home on a wet day, to find something wrong with the yard drainage. He set to work to dig, and put things right but got his clothes soaked in the pouring rain; developed congestion of the lungs and died in a few days.*

*So poor old Grannie was left, to face the world, with very little money and eight children.*

*The two eldest boys, WILLIAM and TOM, were already working and earning a living; MARY and ELIZA were just married. ANNE (Nancy) was working at a Milliners, KATE took a housekeeper's position at Shephards Bush, (then open country); HENRY was already out of his time-and he also got married at once, while ALICK the youngest, had vigorously pushed himself into "Wooleys" the Playing card makers and manufacturing Stationers of Bunhill Row.*

*The old lady moved her modest furniture into three rooms in City Garden Walk, a row of cottages, which ran from Exmouth Street to the House of Detention, (Clerkenwell). To find this City Garden Walk, you went under a stone archway on the left side of Exmouth Street, going west; and found yourself walking at the bottoms of scene long and pretty gardens, at the other end being houses, very small houses, all stuck together, very much like the houses you will find in Poole Street, or facing the Regents Canal.*

*Grandmother Mullord lived on the upper floor, with Anne (Nancy) and Alick.*

*Nancy would bring home some widows caps for her mother to make in between her housework. I recollect as a child of five, being wonderfully attracted by the dummy on which she had to mould these delicate widows cap. The dummy was a ladies head, perfectly bald; nicely modelled and coloured, just like a huge dolls head. And this head was fixed on an iron stand, and in my reverend admiration was a god-like fetish.*

*Grannie was indeed poor then! Nancy's money could not keep three, and Alick was only a boy, earning boy's wages. But in a year or so, John Ward came along and married Nancy. Then Alick was taken apprentice to his Brother Thomas, who was foreman of the wire drawing, at Corneys of Little Britain, and he (Alick) came home at night. His small wages together with the little she earned at cap making kept them going. There was probably also some money help from William, Tom and Henry.*

*When Alick came out of his time, and was earning money as a journeyman, his strenuous character asserted itself, he saved from the beginning, got married, and rented a small house in Brittania Street, (which runs from Murray Street into Nile Street).*

*He took over his Mother, with her little furniture and installed her as lady help.*

*He himself, flew the flag of Independence, turned his back kitchen into a workshop, fixed up drums; and then left Corneys to work for the trade generally.*

*He made good! Such a man was sure to make good. And so the old lady came to be settled finally without financial worry and responsibility, as she deserved; after having brought into the world such a numerous and rather fine family.*

*She remained with her son Alick from about 1850 till about 1880, when her son Henry, whose children had all left the nest, took her over, and cared for her till she died; at the ripe age of 98.*

*She was buried in Abney Park Cemetery. And the hearse had a very numerous following; not only of sons and daughters, but also of grandchildren, and one, or more Great Grandchildren.*

*Because of her great age, and her numerous descendants, she was even more honoured at her death than she had been living. Many were the voices that praised her, and the general view was expressed, I think, in a long verse which I wrote for the memorial cards, and which was carved on her tombstone.*

*The verse began with:*

*"A very monument of Time."*

*and finished with:              "The Mother of a Race."*

*I will try and give a picture of this remarkable ancestress of ours who married the man that came from Scotland, and gave birth to such a large family of Mullords.*

*Although "Scantlebury" is a good old English name, I should think our Anne Scantlebury was descended from a Flemish Family. She was "Flamiache" in, build, and in temperament. About five feet four high, broad and fleshy in head and body, short necked, with pale blue grey eyes, tow coloured hair which turned to white, a small nose with a prominent ridge, and a comfortable placid face.*

*She was rather silent and had no vivacity. In fact! In her fifty years of widowhood, she seemed to have settled down into quiet resignation; waiting for the end, which did not come.*

*She was intensely religious, (a Wesleyan) and was always reading her bible, but read nothing else. She seemed to me to be lacking in intelligence, that is, there was only a simple and childish development of intellectual thought. She had the mentality of a peasant. The rather keen braininess of all her children must have come from the Father; George Alexander Mullord of Scotland, who had died before I could know him, but from all that was told me; was a sharp shrewd man, with much worldly knowledge.*

*I understood that the youngest son, (Alexander) most resembled him. Her only recreation was to go to prayer meetings; she used to amble slowly down from Penn Street to the Wesleyan Chapel in Minterne Street twice a week. When she lived in Clerkenwell, she had been an ardent supporter of "Spa Fields Chapel". A domed building that stood at the back of Emouth Street. She once told me, that this chapel was actually built in the middle of Spa Fields, for John Wesley to preach in, and she, as a child, had heard him there, though afterwards he took up his place, in the house and Tabernacle on Finsbury Pavement. The mention of Spa Fields, which she knew as real fields, reaching from Bagnidge Wells right to Smithfield Cattle Market, is an indication of how London has grown in a century. "Spa Fields", were so named, because of a "Spa", a spring of chalybeate water, which, when I was a child, could still be drank for a fee of a halfpenny, in the Tea Gardens of a Country Inn called "The Merlin's Cave". This Inn stood about a hundred yards north of Exmouth Street. It is now a little dingy beer shop of the same name, in a little dingy square of dingy houses, in the same position, but with all the sordidness of London Poverty; Instead of the simple rustic life which was there when my Father and Mother in their courting days, would take tea in the Gardens, and have their fortune told by Merlin in his cave at the end~of the garden. The cave being made up of cement and oyster shells and Merlin himself being made up with a white wig and beard, and an old patchwork quilt round him. The charge for fortune telling was threepence; But it was a dying industry, so during the day, this same Merlin acted as "Pot Boy"; A Dickensonian character who was attached to every public house at that time, but they are now as a race, extinct as the Pteradaotyl.*

*As we consider the simplicity of, our old grandmother's life, the limitations of her outlook, the quiet Puritan mentality; which made religion the one subject of her thoughts, we must conclude that all her children must have inherited their temperament from their Father, but the religious inclination from the Mother.*

*The Father appears to have been a strong minded forceful man, self-sufficient, self satisfied, and self opinionated, and a very practical man. He could do anything that called to be done. He was keen witted, a man of industrious and sober habits. As I have said his son Alick most resembled him in his mental alertness. But not physically; (Alick was always rather thin, rather short and rather meagre), while the first son George, was supposed to be physically like the Father, and he, whom I knew well, was tall, gaunt featured, muscular, and generally of rather course fibre.*

*I think the Mother gave gentleness and some refinement to the children, for although they all had the self-sufficiency of the Father, they all had a gentle timidity of character; A wish to be respected; A nice open geniality and pleasant amiability; They all wanted to be "on the side of the Angels", and I never knew any of them to use bad language. A simple "damn" was rather shocking to them, though their natural sense of humour and their love of a laugh, made them enjoy the most Rabelaisian jokes; when uttered by John Ward at their numerous festive meetings.*

*Take the family altogether, they were a very creditable lot; Out of the most deplorable poverty, they all raised themselves up to a position of high respectability, and with their characteristic philoprogenitiveness, also managed to raise remarkable families to carry on to posterity, the respected name of MULLORD.*

*I will now take all these sons and daughters, and describe then individually. WILLIAM MULLORD was "A gentleman". I mean both in appearance and temperament. He was always too poor to buy fashionable clothes (Yet his carriage, and the keen gaze that assured one of high mindedness, gave you the impression of decayed nobility. He was the tallest of the family, and was thin and meagre, (probably through his occupation). His hair was originally almost black, with eyebrows, moustache and whiskers to match, but was all turning to grey and white in my boyhood. He had rosy apple cheeks. Big white stony teeth; And keen and very intelligent blue eyes; which turned to steel grey when he was in earnest. And then these eyes seemed to indicate that he could successfully resent any affront put upon him, although a more gentle minded, timidly modest mannered man never lived.*

*WILLIAM had an intellectual cast of mind. The Mullord family taken as a whole were not intellectual, though they were extremely intelligent. Cradled in poverty, their minds were bent on making and saving enough money to live respectably, and climb to-a position above all that poverty means. And a very practical matter of fact honest and honourable kind of life they made of it.*

*But they were never inclined to escape from the prosaic limitations of fulfilling the functions of Physical life. It did not rank among their desires to make excursions into the works of Imagination, Philosophy and Intellectual attainment, which the great thinkers of all ages have endowed the world with.*

*The mental attitude of the Mullords, together I suppose with the bulk of the English people; is that of the gold fish in the water. If the gold fish thinks at all, he knows there is another kind of world above the water, but beyond the fact that sometimes from it comes some gift to him, he has no interest in that upper world. And he says: ~ "My kind of life is good enough for me". Too practical souls of this stage; Speculation as to "The laws regulating Force in connection with matter", has not any interest. A study of "The ethical basis of all religions of all times" is not worth the trouble. And "The evolution of Man from the Amoeba" was, As a proposition; Received with derisive scorn, And with the concerted family opinion that the Mullord family had had something better than Monkeys for ancestors. If at home the writer brought up the oft asked questions of: - What am I? Where did I come from? Where am I going? What purpose do I fulfil? Etc. The remainder of the family circle would look up with blank astonishment; then turning to each other, would tap their foreheads; or make vulgar remarks about "the shortage of buttons".*

*No! The Mullord Family generally do not take an interest in Intellectual studies. But "Uncle William" was an exception. He had a rather fine delicate way of analysing all problems under discussion. And as a boy, I had several intimate talks with him, about such questions as:- "The spiritual aspect of Swedenborg and his works". Or: - "What is Truth", or: - "The political claims of the Chartiste" Or: - Cobden and Peels reform of the corn laws, etc. Uncle William had been in the Chartiste procession of fifty thousand men who met together in Battersea fields, with the avowed intent to march to Westminster and demand manhood suffrage; By violence if need be! West End barricaded its doors and windows with shrieking alarm. The military were called out. The Duke of Wellington personally advising. And the Chartiste processions, with bands and banners, having streamed over all the bridges to the meeting place, with all the "Rag-tak and Bobtail" of London following them, the soldiers took possession of all the bridges, put loaded cannon across them, and waited the return of the "Proletariat".*

*The sight of the cannon, and soldiers with fixed bayonets, across the narrow and only ways, was too much for the immense radical procession. It halted, wavered and finally, (with groans and lamentations) after trying all the bridges, had to find its way back home by Putney, Kew and Richmond, but now in scattered groups, with much sleeping under hedges, and total disorganisation.*

*On these and other reminiscences Uncle William would philosophise, with the meditative mind he was blessed with. And he got much spiritual pabulum from his philosolhy, which was lucky, for he was a poor man. A poor artizan working for poor pay, in a temperature of a hundred and twenty degrees. Baking the lacquer on the "Painted lacquer Tea Trays" which were then the pride of every housewife. These Tea Trays were made of moulded "millboard" (papiere machie) coated with a black shellac, on which was painted gorgeous birds and lovely flowers, with bits of pearl inserted, and the whole varnished again, and baked to develop the mineral colors.*

*Perspiring William; like all the Mullords, loved his work and honoured his empoyer, but at about thirty-five shillings a week. Which provided him and his wife with sufficient nitrogen, hydrogen, and carbon to sustain a healthy mind in a healthy body.*

*He had also a contented heart, so he could smoke his churchwarden and meditate on the phenomena of life, as a Buddhist Yogi attains peace, with a clean body and a minimum of food, and contemplates Nirvana.*

*William married early. But was unfortunate in his wife. His two children, Bill and Molly also were a disappointment, and they eventually dropped away into a low obscurity, and we must leave them at that.*

*After eighteen years as a married man, his wife died; and he went as a widower to live in Corporation Lane, at the house of a Mrs. King, who made him comfortable and happy in a real home.*

*Mrs. King was a most delightful and amiable woman. She too had been unfortunate in her mate, who had left her to fend for herself, in his pursuit of "A short life and happy one" by the worship of Bacchus.*

*Mrs. King was, I think! A happier woman when this husband died. After a time she married Uncle William and a more congenial pair, and a more felicitous match, could not be found. Mrs King had two daughters by her first husband; they were named Alice and Eliza.*

*As a boy of twelve I used often to visit this most happy family at Corporation Lane.*

*I used to think (and think now) that both Alice and Eliza, were very beautiful girls. They both married; the eldest passed out of my ken, but the youngest, Eliza, whom I thought the most beautiful of the two, transmitted her good looks to a daughter named Maud. This Maud is now married to John Mullord (son of Alexander) and, with the help of her husband, is perpetuating those same good looks to posterity, in a numerous progeny of future Mullords.*

*WILLIAM moved over to Lambeth, and with the inherited Puritanical strain in him, gravitated to "Spurgeons Tabernacle". He there became a leading light among the numerous dissenters by whom that establishment was supported.*

*William had the love of making Jingling Rhymes, (a habit that runs through the Clan of Mullords). And it is understood; that more than one set of his verses, written for some special occasion, were sung and appreciated by his co-religionists. I recollect one of his Poems was a long description of "The wreck of the London" which was a very sad disaster, which had stirred the whole nation at the time. This poem was printed. It had about thirty "four line" verses, I had a copy; I wish I had it now, to put in these memoirs. I thought it poor stuff compared with Tennyson or Gray, but as a momento of the dear old man, I should prize it.*

*William lived to a good age; eighty two I think, and ended a clean peaceful life in a gentle fading away.*

*We now come to Eliza Mullord.*

*ELIZA was very good looking. I remember her in a "Coal Scuttle" Bonnet and Crinoline, with long curls hanging round her shoulders, and she looked "A real lady"! She was married at an early age, to WILLIAM WARNER, who was a Clerk at "Pickfords" the Carriers, Canal Bridge, City Road. But Warner's pay at Pickfords was small, and it would not cover necessary expenditure, after all economical measures had been tried. Being a wise, as well as a most intelligent man, he resolved to emigrate, and Canada was his choice.*

*The Warners had two young children, Eliza and Willie, and I recollect all four coming to say a tearful goodbye. It was very sad. They had sold all their home, and were leaving all their friends and launching into the unknown; with only the one certainty, that they would never see their relatives again. While quiet sobbing and talking went on, little Willie and myself had a perfectly beautiful time with a Noah's Ark, which my Mother had bought to solace the little one with on his voyage.*

*How I longed to be allowed to go with him on that great adventure, into the land of promise which every-one was praising.*

*Alas! Poor Willie never reached Canada. There were seven weeks of voyage on a Sailing Vessel, the awful conditions of a cheap steerage passage, together with the frightful storms, (it was winter); The lack of milk and wholesome food, and the lack of a doctor on board. All this settled poor Willie, who was a delicate child always. He was quietly slipped overboard with a scant burial service read by the Captain.*

*Warner took his family to Toronto, and became a "Drummer" and many other things in turn, but eventually getting settled in comfort.*

*Eliza, the wife, wrote over to England periodically, chiefly to her brother Alick, who replied with Budgets of the doings of all her Brothers and Sisters. Many of her letters may be in existence now. His daughter Eliza grew up into a very handsome woman and married a Mr. Harris, then a Manager of works of the firm of Massey, Agricultural Implement makers.*

*The wedding was a great affair. When the happy couple arrived at the settlement in which the works were situated, they were received by four hundred workpeople and rode under Triumphant Arches.*

*HARRIS rose on the great wave of prosperity which had set in for elaborate farming implements. He became Partner, and the Firm is now Massey, Harris Ltd. Harris came to England, and established the present Branch Office in Bunhill Row. Harris did our family the honour of calling. The letters, which the Warners had received from England, had probably given them the idea that the business of A. & A. Mullord was one of considerable importance, and the Mullords Generally were rather important people.*

*Anyhow Harris called at Penn Street Factory one Sunday. We can only imagine the awful disgust and disillusionment that would arise in his mind at seeing the dismal building amid the squalor and sordidness of Hoxton. He, the successful man, straight from the clean beautiful and up to date Factories of his big, modern, highly specialised Canadian Cities. Harris was directed by the Caretaker of the Factory to Henry Road, Finsbury Park, where he was received hospitably and with pleasant welcome by Alexander Mullord and his family. Harris stayed all day, sang hymns with them and generally had a good time. But this prosperous Canadian relative of the family did not turn up again, as far as I know, nor did he invite his host to a dinner at the Ritz; or whatever hotel he was staying at, in return for his hearty welcome. And so he drops out of our chronicle. The loss is his! As he is now a Dollar Millionaire, he no doubt says with the Frenchman; "Heaven gave us our relations, but kindly allows us to choose our own friends.*

*We now come to KATE MULLORD (born about 1824).( 1816 parish records) Aunt Kate as an old maid by nature, and so remained all her life. She had plain amiable features, but a high squeaky voice. Aunt Kate was a housekeeper by profession.           She housekept us when I was a child. There she left us, and went to housekeep a lady at Shephards Bush (then in the Country). I recollect her going, impressed with many instructions from my Mother and Father, as to which train she was to take; and not to forget to eat her sandwiches. She travelled by the new circular line that was just opened; called "The Metropolitan Railway". It took her about five hours to get to her destination. As she explained afterwards, she had asked at almost every station, "if this train was going to Shephard's Bush" and the grinning Porters had always vulgarly answered that she was to "keep in" and she would come to it. At last, arriving at Baker Street, a second time, the platform porter said "Ain't you the lady wot asked me early this morning?" and then gave her more explicit directions, which eventually brought her safely to her destination.*

*I put this little anecdote in, to illustrate the simplicity of the type, of which Aunt Kate was an exponent.*

*She was very placid over it all. I never knew her to exhibit the rather volcanic bursts of ill temper that sometimes! "Breaks out" in the Mullord family. She always wanted to be nice to everyone, and to agree with everybody. In fact! She was a dear, amiable creature. Though at times she was lacking in tact. I can give an instance of this latter.*

*She had come back to housekeep for us for a little while, and we were all sitting round at dinner in the front kitchen of No. 2 Penn Street. I had carefully chosen my seat with my back to the window. Aunt Kate was directly in front of me; and she suddenly said: - "My! 'Enery! You've got a black eye I believe you've been fightin"*

*I turned scarlet, and said sheepishly "No I ain't! I ran up against a lamp post, when I was looking another way."*

*Of course, she looked at me with open-mouthed incredulity. And Father, who up 'till then had been interested chiefly in eating very excellent roast beef we could all get in those days; turned to me and said, in his most solemn and dignified manner, "Don't let me ever ketch you fightin! That's all!"*

*The one and only "Black eye" I ever had the courage to invite. This "Black eye" was given to me by a nice, tall, gentle mannered schoolfellow, named Harrold. He afterwards became a successful Draper in Upper Street, Islington, and lent me at various times, £1,350, during my various speculative House Building Schemes.*

*Moral! Timidity and cowardice is often combined with great audacity. Napoleon the Great said: The only secret of success is "Audace! Audace!. Toujours l'audace".*

*And then mother, (Always full of sympathetic understanding said: "Oh, do let the boy alone; he's got to eat his dinner and get off to School." The conversation being then diverted to the comparative merits of "Plum Duff" and "Treacle Roly", I managed to efface myself quietly.*

*But I always condemned Aunt Kate for want of tact, though I have never liked her any less for it.*

*Soon after this, she obtained the appointment of Housekeeper to the celebrated Mr. Dodd, The dustman. (The "Mr. Boffin!" in Dickens tale of "Our Mutual Friend). Dodd "contracted" with all the Parish Vestries of London, to clear and cart away the refuse in the Dustbins of every house. Hundreds of Mr. Dodd's dustcarts came from all over the town, and dumped their evil smelling contents in his great "dust yards" left and right of Eagle Wharf Road.*

*I used to sneak inside the gates of these mysterious "dust yards", and watch the score or so of dirty women, each with a big basket on either side, picking out the rags for one basket and the, waste paper for the other. They always reminded me of the witches in Macbeth. A score or so of equally dirty men, carried away and emptied the baskets, and other men with other baskets sifted the remaining rubbish; putting old metal in one basket and cinders in another. In the background, two huge volcanic looking mountains of cinders arose. These were worth many hundreds of pounds. And there also arose in warm weather; (although the whole place was kept as orderly and clean as possible), an odour: which fairly eclipsed the ghastly smells emitted by the Canal close by, coated though it was with an oily scum, the surface of which was broken with bubbles of gas; and occasionally with the bodies of cats and dogs in various stages of decay.*

*Aunt Kate stayed for years with Dodd, in the rather nice and very neatly kept house in front of the Yard. They were all alone together; why he did not marry her I do not know; for he was a lonely widower. It may have been his fault, it certainly was not hers.*

*However! Though she had not the arts of fascination, she was a good housekeeper, and nursed him well through a long illness.*

*It was a quiet life; for by his expressed instructions, she could never leave the house, except for Chapel on Sunday evening. She had it is true, a Hen Canary for a companion, and she seemed fairly contented for twenty-five years. Then the old man died! And an urbane, but peremptory lawyer sealed up all the rooms, and turned Aunt Kate out with a quarter's salary in lieu of notice; She went to the funeral, which was a grand one. It was attended by a host of various relatives, all with expectations, for Dodd had amassed a large fortune, not a farthing*

*Of which came to Aunt Kate. As King Lear says: - "Blow! Blow! Thou wintry wind, thy breath is not so keen as man's (especially a Dustman's) ingratitude.*

*Aunt Kate was now getting old, she had saved some money, for she was of frugal habit; her only dissipation had been the frequently going to the Minterne Street Chapel. There she had put 6d in the plate and ld in the Missionary Box, every Sunday. She had also spent twopence per month for seed for the Hen Canary. This had been the total of her expenditure. She may have bought some clothes. It is*

*A well known Psycological fact that the more "plain featured" women have a more highly developed instinct for personal decoration. And they may explain why Aunt Kate, when she paid us a visit, generally had a new "cap", a glorious "creation", composed of black lace and a great number of loops of satin ribbon of blue, green, white and purple; the delightful colour scheme of a Jay.*

*So now! She joined forces with Aunt Ward, (whom we shall speak of later) and both went to live in retirement at Englefield Road, next door from Essex Road. They lived there for some years, quarrelling and making it up, and affectionately tolerating each other.*

*And at last, gentle Aunt Kate died of Senile Decay at the age of 77*

*We now come to MARY MULLORD (known as Aunt Mary).*

*Aunt Mary was the best looking of the family. A really handsome woman.*

*Her good looks captured the affections of a gentleman with the euphonious name of KEDGE.*

*Kedge was a man of elusive character. I never saw him. He never seemed to "cotton on" to the Mullord family; He belonged to another world. I fancy he "Wasted his substance in riotous living". And, he did not do the riotous living with his wife, but elsewhere, largely in a liquid form.*

*Anyhow! Kedge disappears from our ken, and vanishes into "der ewiegkeit". Some years after, her husband presumably dead, when we come across Aunt Mary again, she has become a Mrs. Smith.*

*As a commercial traveller at the age of thirteen, when l was beating up the family circle for orders in household groceries, (my Father having bought the then grocer-shop at the corner of Benyon Road), I ferreted out Aunt Mary. I found her in a large roomy outbuilding in Sekford Street, Clerkenwell. She was hard at work "getting up" "ladies underwear" for a West End business house.*

*It all smelt nice and clean. The warm ironing stoves and the dainty "lingerie" damp with blue starch and perfumed soap.*

*Aunt Mary was happy in her work, (as all Mullords are). She was reticent about her affairs, though it plainly appeared that SMITH the husband did not earn enough to dispense with her earnings.*

*SMITH was "something in the Coal Trade". And we found afterwards that he was a very kindly disposed man.*

*He came one day, to my Father, and said Aunt Mary was ill. Congestion! Of the lungs! Father and Uncle Alick went to see her, but found she was past all helps And they could only bid her a sad farewell.*

*So died, at about the age of fifty, the handsomest of all the Mullords.*

*When first married to Kedge, Aunt Mary had two children, whom I knew as "Bill" and "Dolly". They both of them, like their Father, drifted away from the puritanic formalism of the Mullords. I believe they preferred the far more exciting and pleasurable existence of another plane of life, and I think the "West End of London" took them into its maw and devoured them. I saw Dolly once, as a woman of twenty, and a very pretty woman she was.*

*The next in the family was NANCY MULLORD, (born about 1838).*

*We all knew her as Aunt Ward.*

*NANCY was rather tall and slender, had fine grey eyes, and a rather long pale face. She had also much dignity and grace of carriage, dressed well for her position, and altogether was the most stylish and well mannered of all the family.*

*Her highly bred and "lady like" appearance, caught the attention of JOHN WARD, whom she married after a short courtship.*

*JOHN WARD was a wonderful man.*

*Arnold Bennett would have called him "A card"! Byron would have called him a gigantic "Don Juan". I prefer to think of him, as "Robert Burns" with all "Rabbie's" weaknesses. John Ward lacking only the genius to write "Tam 0'shanter", but having all the sense of humour of, and being a living example of: "A man's a man for a' that".*

*John Ward had the instincts of a gentleman. There was nothing mean about him, physically or mentally. He had a fine taste in dress, and carried his six feet two inches of height, and his massive body, with grave dignity.*

*My earliest recollection of him, is of a very important looking man, with black hair, eyes and eyebrows; a long spreading beard turning grey, and a grave solemn countenance. He wore a fine black cloth coat and trousers of the best quality, and a superb black satin "Ascot" tie round a large open "Gladstone" collar, and a black silk "Moire antique" waistcoat, across which was a fine gold "Albert". And in the right pocket of which was the omnipresent snuffbox. Every man took snuff at that time, and I used to gaze as a child, spellbound; at Uncle John taking snuff. Pushing it up into his big nostrils, with big thumb and finger; then drawing out an enormous Indian silk handkerchief of gorgeous yellows and rich browns, he would blow his nose with a vigorous blast that shook the room. And after that, lightly flicking off some of the snuff grains, that his beard and waistcoat were always bestrewed with, he would solemnly deliver himself of his latest store of bold and wicked jokes. Jokes, which he had been accumulating, all the week for this, his regular Sunday morning visit.*

*Uncle Ward, Uncle Alick and my Father, were great chums, and our house was the starting point for their Sunday morning walk, that is after a preliminary glass of gin or brandy from one of the four Decanters in a silver stand, which made the centre decoration of our "Drawing Room" table (and that of every other drawing room table in those times).*

*They would; all three dressed in their smartest tall silk hats, walk to the Manor House, Green Lanes, then really green lanes, and drink a glass of bitter beer, and return home to consume the big Sunday dinner awaiting them.*

*This they did for years on Sundays, and they would also meet in the evenings every weekday, at what was known as "A Parlour". It was customary for every Public House to have "A Parlour". A place where, after the day's occupation, sober minded, quiet reasonable good citizens would sit in nice comfortable arm chairs, in front of long mahogany tables. Drinking gin and water, smoking long church wardens, talking of the interminable American Civil War or they would*

*"Pooh, pooh" Keenans chances in fighting Tom Sayers, or perhaps bewail the fact of a French horse, "Gladiateur" winning the Derby.*

*The "Trio"! Always went! to the Derby together. It was a regular function while Uncle Ward was "One of us". Sometimes, they would all take their wives. They would hire a small and gaily decorated van from a local greengrocer, friends would join in, all shared the expense, and also brought pies, tarts, tea kettles and tea cups, Big brown jars of beer were not forgotten; and given a fine day, much rollicking fun, and dusty, perspiring, sunburnt thirstiness, made it a day to remembered till the next occasion.*

*These excursions were not limited to Derby Days; an impromptu "days outing" would be arranged more than once during the summer, always at John Ward's initiative. Trips to Riddlesdown, Hampton Court, Epping Forest, are among my most cherished memories; they being  trips on which children were allowed.*

*In winter, each of the Trio, with another "chum" named Hollebone,           gave a Christmas Party.     What glorious affairs these Christmas Parties were; what quantities of food and drink were consumed. And John Ward would tell such tales, and utter such jokes, and Aunt Ward would say "John! I'm ashamed of you! Look at that child, he's all ears" (me). And then John Ward would open the evening's concert, (he could no more sing than a convivial bullock) with: -*

*"'Tis forty years my old Fren! John, Sin we were*

*boys together; Merry, Merry Boys! Merry, Merry*

*boys!. When we were boys together," etc.*

*After that, Uncle Alick and my Father would sing one of their exquisite duets, and then Aunt Ward would pipe out, like a sweet voiced canary, with "The Blind Boy's been at play, Mother", or: "A Father's Love".*

*And so, in this way, with drinking and smoking more freely than is now customary; the party would go on, till two in the morning. Well, there was no great harm in those parties, although I know now, there was much too great a consumption of alcohol.*

*"Drinking" was the curse of those mid-Victorian times, and John Ward was only. One of the victims to the custom, He could hardly help it, his distinguished appearance and manners, attracted men and women of all grades, (so like Rabbie Burns). The men all wanted him to drink with them, and there were women who invited his attentions in more ways than one. As a young man, at Wolverhampton, where he was born, and at Coventry, where he learnt his trade of watchmaking, his life was a round of pleasure. Indeed at Coventry, a city that never lacks many candidates for the annual character of Lady Godiva, it was understood, that more than one confiding damsel persuaded him to play "Romeo" to her "Juliet" with such success, that he concluded it would be wise, as well as pleasant, to make his "Wander jahr. That is to leave Coventry, and make his fortune in London, and to do it, not by prosaic Coach or Railway, but romantically on foot, journeying from place to place, and working for a few days at each city he came to.*

*Surely this was the happiest year of his life. John was always a lover of nature, and we used to get little glimpses from him of this geat adventure. It was like reading George Borrow`s "Lavengro". Elated as the larks which he heard triliing their immortal songs above his head, flushed with the physical exercise his great limbs revelled in, as he trudged the long spaces of dusty high road (quiet then, and not motor cursed). Bare footed, his boots slung over his shoulders by the laces. Welcomed at every wayside Inn. (By the men for his ponderous dry humour, by the women for his "glad eye", and splendid good looks,) life indeed gave him all he desired, and was indeed splendid.*

*John Ward arrived at last in London, and when working at watchmaking in Clerkenwell and met NANCY MULLORD. Her fine grey eyes and puritan-minded manner and dress appealed to his nature. As the "unknown" in woman always calls to a man of his temperament.*

*He courted and married her quickly; for she, no more than others could resist him.*

*John and Nancy took apartments over the shop of Ellis the bootmaker in Shakespeare Walk. And John fitted up a bench in the back room, and worked at home.*

*They never had children, nor do I think their married life was perfect. For one thing; John found that Nance had a temper: and could give vent to it with words that cut like the lash of a whip. John, his day's work done, would "spruce himself up" and depart for an evening among his convivial cronies, leaving Nance to do "Plain hemming" or mend stockings, and work finished, to sadly retire to bed. Then John would return about midnight, in a condition that showed "he had looked upon- the wine when it was red." Next morning; he would be gloomy, morose, and silent, at his work bench, while she snapped out such remarks as would indicate her disapproval of the world in general and John Ward in particular. It was not every night, but much too frequently. For one thing, they were living too near "The Grecian" which was at that time a Classically brilliant haunt of all the gay roysterers of the town. And more than once, has Nance sat in the darkfront room, looking out of the window: with slow tears gathering, waiting the return of her belated partner. And would, when the Grecian-had closed at one hour past midnight, see his tall figure come along, amidst the merry crowd of fellow Bacchanalians of both sexes.*

*He would then, sick of everything, creep into bed, before he wondered in. It was on the morning after two of these occasions, that Uncle John was reduced to an impotent but aggravating silence, by the reproachful invectives from his "better half". By sentences that scorched like burning lava issuing from a volcanic irruption. He at last, ponderously arose from the breakfast table, dressed himself all in his best, (like "Sally in our Alley") and without a word, departed! Closing the street door quietly behind him.*

*Aunt Nancy left victorious on the stricken! field, waited alone in the silent rooms, and at last, past the night in mournful solitude. Next morning she came round to my Father and Uncle Alick, at Penn Street, and poured out her grievous tale.*

*Uncle Alick, always practical, sent for a Cab, (there was a cab rank in Baring Street in those smiling mi&~VictOriaI1 times) and all three journeyed to Kings Cross-, and from there to Coventry.*

*Arriving at Coventry, and making the main street their first hunting ground, they by happy chance met John and two boon companions "face to face".*

*"Hullo Nance" shouted good-tempered John! All resentment having been washed away, (in good. Coventry beer) and coming up to her kissed her loudly.*

*To-the delight of all onlookers. he then tucked her arm in his, and said: "Come and see Mrs. Bishop" (his sister). "She was wondering why you had not come" etc. etc.*

*Mrs. Bishop (a dear woman) made much of her, and gave them all a scrumptious tea.*

*And they all made merry. John and Nance slept at the Bishops! but Father and Uncle Alick slept at the Blue Boar, and returned next morning; Leaving the pair as happy as "two innocent Turtle doves", who couldn't say anything worse than "coo".*

*On the whole, Aunt Nance and Uncle John got on very much better together as the years passed. They both had very fine qualities, although they both had faults, and age mellowed them.*

*I don't think Uncle John was ever unfaithful to his wife; on the contrary, he, I know, respected, and was really fond of her. And Aunt Nancy, although she had that nasty temper, which smouldering in the breasts of all the Mullord Family, bursts out occasionally in an irritable, sour, and cantankerous obsession, was otherwise, a very pleasant woman to live with and was a dutiful and affectionate help-mate.*

*In middle age, John gave up his independence, and took service as watch repairer with "Frodshams" of Piccadilly. They were the most fashionable watchmakers in London.*

*John was a splendid workman. A master of, his art, and one day the great Mr. Frodsham came to him and said "John, I've something for you now! I've just brought from Geneva a watch that winds itself, without a key. There it is! Now you make one like it, and spare no expense".*

*John Ward admired it, and said, "Will you let me make four. The first for you, and the other three I will buy for myself and my two brothers."*

*John made the four, and sold one each to my Father and Uncle Alick, at trade price (which was £30.) I now have the one my Father had; it goes as well as ever. And although fifty years old, is probably worth £40. To day. I expect Uncle Alick's duplicate is still kept in the family, as an interesting relic, or is worn and keeping good time.*

*John Ward, getting old and his eyes getting feeble, gave up I watchmaking at last, and retired with Aunt Nancy to a Wayside Inn, "The Old Mile House", St. Albans.*

*My wife and myself, then only sweethearts went to see them there. It was a lonely place. A mile from town, with a big garden, stables many fowls and some pigs. I thought it an ideal life, but they found it monotonous. Besides! It was bad for Uncle John; to sit idle in the little bar, facing a pretty brass ringed barrel on the shelf in front of him. This barrel was neatly labelled in red letters with the single word "GIN" and it had a dinky little brass tap, which dripped now and again into a neat little glass, which stood beneath it.*

*After two years, they sold the "Good will" of the Inn, and retired to a nice house in the Mile End Road, Whitechapel. Here they lived, in the quietude of coming old age, till one night! Poor John returning late from one of his less frequent shopping jaunts, mounted the stone steps unsteadily, and in some way in the dark, fell over the stone coping into the area.*

*Aunt Nancy hearing the noise, rushed down; and shrieking for help till neighbours came, managed to get him on to a couch, where she waited in grief over her insensible and stertorous husband; till a Doctor came and perfunctorily said: "He would be dead in the morning".*

*So ended a gallant gentleman, who with his many faults, was honoured, and indeed loved, by all the Mullord Family.*

*John Ward died intestate. He lived as a butterfly; taking gladly the gifts each day of sunshine brought him, and flitting from flower to flower drinking the nectar of life. A true "Epicurean"; ignoring the winter of the future.*

*Aunt Nancy, helpless, appealed as usual to her brother Alick; who, practical as usual, invested the small capital, which was in the bank. He bought the leasehold house in Englefield Road, N. (Next to the Butchers shop at the corner.)*

*This house was given to Aunt Ward while she lived, to revert to Mrs. Bishop. (Uncle Ward's sister). And a good woman Mrs. Bishop was to agree to this arrangement of her legal share of the estate.*

*Aunt Nancy took her very nice furniture into the first floor rooms of this house, and let off the top floor; the ground floor; and the kitchens. She also took to live with her, her sister, Aunt Kate, with her savings. And there the two lived together in a more or less congenial manner, (with a little breeze now and then springing up in the usual Mullord manner.)*

*They both were religious, by early training, and in the primitive Wesleyan kind of way. And no doubt! Gave all they could spare, to the little Chapel round the corner.*

*Aunt Nancy's peaceful life was suddenly altered; getting out of bed one morning, she slipped and fell! And falling on the fender broke her thighbone. Fractured it in splinters, and she was too old for it to mend properly. So the poor dear woman was condemned to lie on the couch most of her time, or just hobble about with a stick.*

*Aunt Kate did the best she could to keep things going. But she was old and faded away first; dying quietly and peacefully.*

*Then! Uncle Alick got a poor chapel friend to look after Aunt Nancy in such ways as were necessary, and with his very great help, and small money help from one or two other relatives, she passed along in a rather lonely but resigned way to her end.*

*That "Resignation" was a marvellous feature of her misfortune. She lost all her bad temper and discontent of life, and she sang hymns, and blessed her God! And also her Brother Alick, with humble thankfulness.*

*And Brother Alick deserved it, for he was with her almost daily; While all other friends visited only at rare intervals. She died at about seventy-six.*

*She was a nice lady-like woman of many virtues, and with steady affection bubbling up for anyone she was in touch with.*

*Our next is THOMAS HODGES MULLORD (Born about 1824 ad.)*

*Thomas Hodges Mullord, (hereinafter called "Uncle Tom"), was a bluff hearty open-minded simple strong voiced Englishman. And he used, at friendly gatherings to sing: "The Four Jolly Smiths", (With a bang! and a clang! and a "Ding Ding Dong", the work it goes Merrily, Merrily along!) And when he sang this song in a loud strident voice, he looked just "The Village Blacksmith." Uncle Tom was the biggest man of all Grandmothers' sons. He was of middle height, broad in the shoulders, had stout limbs, blue grey eyes, and an open countenance.*

*Also! He always wanted to be on terms of hearty smiling friendliness with most people he came in contact with. In temperament I think he inherited a certain amount of Grandmothers Flemish contentment with its lack of enterprise. All he wanted to do was a week's good honourable work, and do it well. And to earn and have the fullest respect and approval from his employers. (This he got all his life.) If the difficulties of keeping a large family on small money, made him sometimes dream of launching out into business venture; his highly developed bump of caution made him say: No! To others the adventures, let me have a good situation, and a certain salary, if small. I will be content without 1uxury, if I can have the necessities of life and a peaceful mind.*

*He was offered the management of a Gunpowder Factory. It was that most picturesque white walled mill on the Hoggs Mill River, which you see just as you are getting into Ewell.*

*The post was offered him at a much bigger salary than he was getting.*

*"What," he said. "A gunpowder Mill! Good Heavens! Everything would go on for a year or two; and then some silly ass would light his pipe and throw the match on the ground; and Bang! Me and the Mill, and the big Salary, would all go up into the air together."*

*"No! A thousand times better to be in a warm workshop at "Ash's", in perfect safety. !'Tis better to beer the ills you have, than fly up to others that you know not of."*

*So much for temperament. I (the writer) should have jumped at the chance in his place, and perhaps should have gone to Heaven sooner than I wanted to in consequence; For I have always been fond of smoking. Uncle Tom married EMMA HOADLEY (hereinafter called Aunt Emma).*

*Aunt Emma was a big Buxom Vigorous Country Lass. Her mother was a Farmer's daughter, who had been married, for her good looks, by a gentleman's son named HOADLEY. Hoadley was educated for the Church and had been Ordained. But his people looked upon his marriage as a "mesalliance", when he wedded a pretty little girl, who was only a daughter of a small Ticehurst Farmer.*

*In consequence; Hoadley had a violent quarrel with his Father, who "chucked" him out. Sent him adrift!*

*Hoadley, being the usual University young man, had no chance now of getting "a living" in any sense, so his new Farmer father-in-law (being a violent Dissenter), salved his outraged Yeoman pride, by building a small Methodist Chapel in Ticehurst, and putting young Hoadley in it as Pastor and Spiritual Guide to half the small Farmers and country labourers of Ticehurst.*

*There are many Hoadleys in Sussex; In Ticehurst Churchyard, you can see at least a dozen Tombstones dedicated to defunct Hoadleys, going back for two centuries.*

*All these Hoadleys claim to be descended from the celebrated Benjamin Hoadley, who was a Bishop of Winchester at the time of George the Second. He was that Bishop whom Carlyle calls: "The Fighting Bishop", because of his vigorous pamphlets written in support of the Hanoverian dynasty, and against the claims of the Stuarts. Thackery mentions him in "The Four Georges", but in no Complimentary manner.*

*You can see a big painting of this ancestor of my wife in the National portrait Gallery, Trafalgar Square. We are so proud of it that we want to put a wreath under the picture every time we go to London.*

*If he was a "Fighting" Bishop, my wife's Grandfather Hoadley, might well be descended from him, for he, (the Grandfather) was a violent tempered brutal man.*

*He was tall, with a powerful physique, His wife presented him with six daughters, who all grew up to be big muscular girls, but big as they were he did not hesitate to seize them by the hair and shake them about, when he was in a rage, which was pretty often; For he was a disillusioned man.*

*His little good-looking wife he found was not intellectual, (Though intelligent). Her lack of accomplishments, and her poor education, made her anything but a congenial companion. Also! Both were ostracised by his own class.*

*Hoadley got to be a morose character, who hated his environment. Eventually! He was rascal enough to bolt and disappear into the unknown, leaving his deserted wife with a houseful of furniture, and nothing else to keep her.*

*Her girls being already married, she sold up, and came to live in London, with Aunt Emma and Uncle Tom, who kindly took care of her until she died.*

*I frequently saw her, and tried to talk with her; but her broad Sussex dialect, and her limited range of ideas, made conversation difficult. However! She was still a pretty little woman at the age of seventy.*

*Uncle Tom and Aunt Emma had a large family. The children were respectively: Rhoda, Emma, Tom, Alfred, Hannah, (who became the wife of the Writer) Walter and Henry.*

*Rhoda died early, Emma and Tom were a big and handsome pair. All the others were smaller; but they were all good looking, healthy, boisterous and noisy, but without vice. In fact they were anepitomy of their parents, an open-minded, self-willed lot; all generous to a fault, nevertheless, a terrible crew for Aunt Emma to bring up, especially on the small money that Uncle Tom earned.*

*Uncle Tom started life as "A Gold Wire Drawer". He had gone into (A firm that is now defunct.)*

*He had risen at Corneys to be Foreman of the Wire drawing department. This was a position of confidential trust; as many pounds weight sterling of gold was under his control.*

*While there; he took in his younger brother, Alexander, as Apprentice, and a very able apprentice he was.*

*Uncle Tom was unusually clever at making the "Jewel Plates". These were steel plates; with a diamond cleverly let into the middle. Either in the diamond itself or at the side of the diamond a hole of special size had to be created. Through this hole the gold wire had to be "Drawn". Thus! A "drum" of thick wire on one side of the Jewel Plate, was converted into a "drum" of thinner wire on the other side of the plate.*

*No other workman in Corneys shop could make these plates.*

*One day, Uncle Tom was "approached" by the firm of CLAUDIUS ASH & SONS of Soho; The biggest firm of Artificial Teeth Makers in the world. "ASH's" wanted to "Draw" their own gold and platinum wire, instead of buying it. They had started a workshop of Wire Drawers (But no one could make the jewel plates. They offered Uncle Tom a much better salary than he was getting; and an engagement for three years.*

*He accepted the offer and never regretted it.*

*Uncle Tom remained with C. Ash & Sons till his dear wife (AUNT EMMA) died (aged 72).*

*Uncle Tom then seemed so "Broken up", and so helpless, that we persuaded him to give up his business. His son Tom, now married and doing well, offered to take him into his own house.*

*The Writer went to Claudius Ash & Sons, and asked Mr. Claudius Ash if his firm would kindly give Uncle Tom a small pension, in consideration of his forty years honourable service. It had been a service of trust; For Uncle Tom always locked up the stock of Gold and Platinum in his special safe; and brought the keys home with him.*

*Messrs: Ash & Sons very liberally gave him a pension of fourteen shillings per week, and a message of appreciation of his long service.*

*With this pension and an allowance from his daughter Hannah, Uncle Tom was in a position to pay for his cost of living to Tom, (who would willingly have borne it all), and then have a few shillings to spend or save, (which all old people find is almost their greatest pleasure.)*

*But tragedy came! His son Tom, fine, handsome, gallant Tom, suddenly fell dead, while walking up Barnet Hill.*

*His widow, with a big family, found the old Gentleman too much of a tie.*

*So we had to shift him on to his other son, Walter, who had married my deceased Brother's wife, Eleanor. She was a soft hearted, pretty woman, whom we all really liked very much. But! After a year or two, unfortunately and sadly, Eleanor developed dropsy, became very ill, then died.*

*So poor old Uncle Tom had to be shifted again.*

*Eventually, (Uncle Alick coming in with a generous sum every week towards expense), we put the "Old Man" into & very good Nursing Home. He had become by this time, a poor feeble old invalid, with no memory, and childish habits. His rosy cheeks and good pleasant manners endeared him to his nurses, who called him "Daddy" and treated him well. He faded away and died at the age of 78.*

*And so ended Uncle Tom, after a life of plodding peaceful domestic comfort.*

*Uncle Tom and Aunt Emma were types of "The Old School". They were both religious, they had imbibed the Methodist tenets with their childhood. Dogmatic without knowing it; believing in a Devil and a Hell of fire awaiting sinners. Having "sat under" a Calvinist preacher, they believed in the future of all being preordained; and Uncle Tom, with his cheery Sanguine nature, believed that he was one of "The Elect", and sure of salvation, while his wife Emma, inheriting a pessimistic temperament, (although a woman absolutely incapable of sinning with knowledge) believed she was possibly one of "The Lost". They tried to bring up their children in these ideas. A pack of cards was not allowed in the house, a good elevating game of cribbage might incur Eternal Damnation. And even to read Shakespeare's, or any one's plays, was looked upon with reprobation.*

*But the children had fallen: on other times. They were an unruly lot, and anxious to catch pleasure on the wing at every opportunity. However they turned out none the worse for that.*

*TOM grew into a giant and could "down" successfully any opponent at a boxing club, where I introduced him with great pride at having such a cousin.*

*He married HELEN POVEY, and had a big family. His Father had got him into "ASH's", but he left, and went to the "Dental Company" at Barnet, to better himself.*

*At Barnet, Tom became a manager; and had fine prospects, but a tragic and untimely death cut him off.*

*As I have said: He was returning from a Sunday morning walk        with a friend; and they had almost reached the top of Barnet Hill, when poor Tom "staggered" and fell! And when they picked him up he was DEAD! His funeral was attended by scores of Barnet people who knew him and much respected him.*

*EMMA, A fine handsome girl, had married William Barrett, who was a tall good-looking blatant Irishman. A "Demagogue", who could, and did, talk down anybody, and any number, of people, but whose intellectual development had been retarded by his immensely developed self satisfaction.*

*They had ten children, and then Emma died in childbirth, through a drunken Doctor. She was one of the dearest and nicest women I have ever known.*

*Barrett became the most valuable salesman in "ASH's" show rooms. For by the mere power of his tongue, he could make any unfortunate Dentist purchase an American Dental chair for ninety pounds, when the proper price was about fifty-five.*

*He was punished however. In that he married again. A widow with five children, so that he had a houseful to keep (ten children and his wife), and plenty of exercise for his tongue.*

*Uncle Tom's second son, ALFRED, married a Miss Curd while he was eighteen and a CabinetMaker's apprentice. She however, made it up to him by "coming into money" in middle life. And I understand he is now "A Gentleman".*

*HANNAH came next, she who became my wife. She was dark and thin, delicate rather than robust, but inheriting a good constitution and a temperament that was a happy blend of the Father's good nature, and the Mother's austere integrity. She developed into the proper wife for a man like myself; who was impulsive, casual, and anything but practical. If a lock or a clock wanted mending, or a window blind wanted restoring to its original perfection; Hannah was the one to do it, not her husband. Aunt EMMA was against the marriage. "I don't think cousins ought to marry," she said! "But you have made your bed, and will have to lie on it, I daresay you will have to nurse him, half his lifetime, etc. etc." I don't blame the old lady; I was not much to look at, having been ill, and also having lived a life of ungoverned freedom; with too much tobacco, alcohol, billiard saloons, and late hours. However! It turned out all right. We became chums as well as lovers, and I have not spent half a dozen evenings away from my wife all the forty years we have been married. Aunt Emma got to have great affection for me; as I for her.*

*WALTER came next. He was "The bad boy of the family". Not that he was very bad, but mischievous and self-willed and the most daring of the lot. I have spoken of all the children being boisterous, rough, and full of "animal spirits". Consequently they all became "Holy Terrors" for getting into trouble, through their quite innocent little jokes. For instance, Walter climbing and shutting a kitten into the gas-lamp-post. Or! All of them throwing roadside missiles into the Area window of the Baker's Shop at the corner, on to the nice new dough the baker's man was kneading. A thing like that to them was only harmless fun. I will give one special instance of the sort of sport that was constantly occurring.*

*Uncle Tom and Aunt Emma had migrated from Peckham to the Salubrious locality of Kentish Town. They had, with their numerous progeny, taken possession of a very old house, which was really part of a Jacobaean mansion. A relic of the ancient aristocracy which had long ago fled; from what had become a grimy maze of cheap Jerry built houses.*

*As a boy, my soul"! Was filled with delight at this old house. It had a big stone Portico over the doorway, and inside the house were numerous panelled rooms, creaky panelled staircases, and dark panelled cupboards. Also a labyrinthine cellar containing numerous rats and mice. Then there was one haunted chamber, and a great big Tomcat with green eyes.*

*The garden had been entirely built over, and absorbed by a row of sordid*

*Shops facing a squalid back street. The roofs of these shops were flat and sheeted with zinc. This zinc roof was easily reached by us boys from our bedroom window, by using a romantic improvised ladder.*

*It happened that, from these leaden roof flats, we could just see at times, the head of a gentleman who was writing at the wide open window of the next door house. This Gentleman's name was "Onions", and he wrote for a living. (Heaven help all such.) He may have been the Father of the present Celebrated Oliver Onions, whose tales are well worth reading.*

*Now it occurred that one afternoon, when the boys had only just gone back to school; Aunt Emma, although she was deaf, heard a tremendous "rattatt" at the front door. She opened it; and found facing her, a scowling gentleman, who was holding a bunch of dilapidated spring onions in his hand.*

*Aunt Emma stared with the uncompromising impassivity of a deaf person, while he said: "My name is Onions! Your young scoundrels are always shouting "Onions" at me! And now I find all these onions thrown on my writing desk!"*

*Aunt Emma said, "Yes! I see they are onions; but I don't understand what you have been talking about! I am deaf! Perhaps if you will begin all over again, and explain."*

*"Oh!" shouted the Gentleman: "Damn your explanations!" And thrusting the offending vegetables into the astonished hands of Aunt Emma, he retreated! A beaten man.*

*Eventually Walter went to sea. It was this way. Young Truscoat, who was a distant cousin, happened to come on a visit. He was mate on an "Ocean Tramp". Truscoat told wondrous tales of voyages round the world; and offered to take young Walter, as a cabin boy to start with. They were just going to sail to New Zealand, and it was a fine trip. The matter was soon settled.*

*I went with the boys to London Docks to see the ship. A sailing vessel about the size of a small back garden, we found it to be. With three towering masts, each decorated with narrow rope ladders. These ladders made me shudder, and much as I wanted to see other countries, nothing would have persuaded me to risk my precious self on a ship like that.*

*However! Walter stuck to the sea and came back periodically, each time much coarser and rougher; and with a pronounced predilection for beer.*

*But after many adventures extending over many years, he came home to "roost". That is: He wanted to make a living and home, on land.*

*But though hard working, and of perfect integrity, a few glasses of beer, or bad whisky, would lead to frequent changes of masters. So he remained a "Rolling Stone". He wandered all over England in search of "Jobs".*

*Walter, home from a voyage came to visit us at Station Road, Finchley, and there met and fascinated my brother Christopher's widow. We all four went to Barnet Fair and this so stimulated their courtship that they married in a fortnight, and her brother-in-law (Frome) found him a job, and so he stayed on land and had children, till his wife, Eleanor, died.*

*He is still alive; and comes at times suddenly into view, like a comet, appearing to the consternation of his relatives one night, to disappear again next day; into the everlasting nowhere.*

*The next and last of the family is HENRY.*

*HENRY is a very steady plodder; with all the good qualities and temperament of his Father. And through that same temperament, he has been content to keep in the one situation for forty years. He considers a small but certain income better than the uncertainty appertaining to "launching out".*

*His great adventure occurred during "The War". He is a clerk for the Worshipful Company of Ironmongers, the Offices of which were at Ironnmongers Hall, then in Fenchurch Street.*

*One Saturday morning, some Zeppelins came swiftly out of the East; Low down as they reached London and began dropping their beastly and enormous bombs round about Lime Street and Fenchurch Street.*

*Cousin Harry and his bevy of girl clerks had hurried down into the cellar at the first alarm.   Frightful explosions were coming nearer and nearer. The doorbell rang! Harry left his cellar, went up and opened the door. "For Gawd's sake! let me in!." said an old woman. Harry dragged her in and down into the cellar. A*

*minute after; a huge bomb came plunging through the roof! And then! Irongers Hall was a smoking wreck, with a band of shrieking moaning women pouring from the side cellar; within a yard of which the bomb had carved a hole in the ground twenty feet wide and ten deep, which hole filled with the broken masonry, ironwork and glass of what had been Ironongers Hall.*

*How they all escaped death was "The" wonderful thing."*

*Harry says little about the matter now;  but I think he will always show evidence of that bit of German Devilry.*

*This completed the family roll of Uncle Tom and Aunt Emma. It is marvelous how these two should bring up such a family, with the little money they had at their command. Yet they brought them up, comfortably and happily. Of course simply, and without luxury.*

*I think they were both just as happy in their lives, as relations who had very much more money. They had probably both read the Sermon on the Mount, understood it, and tried to live up to it.*

*It was temperament. Uncle Tom, with his contentment with simple certainty, and Aunt Emma with her grim earnest resolution to do what she had to do, with all the might of her wonderful endurance.*

*She became deaf. And also, for some reason, could not walk much in later life, so she really was a solitary minded woman. But I believe she obtained contentment, by making others comfortable and happy.Aunt Emma died at the age of 72. Worn out with the functioning, and continuous work of a large family.*

*She has at least one memorial, in the great love and sympathy, that my wife and myself will always have for her, and the memory of her.*

*We now come to HENRY BARTHOLEMEW MULLORD. (Born about 1823).*

*HENRY was the son that followed Thomas Hodges, and was my Father.*

*When my Father died; we put upon his Tombstone:-*

*Shakespeare's words:-*

*"Take him for all in all; we ne`er shall*

*look upon his like again."*

*These words seemed to best express our feeling about him. For he was different to most men, in that he was modest as a child, and never self asserting, though possessing superlative qualities.*

*It was quite true! He stood alone in his gentle unassuming temperament. And everybody loved him for himself, more than they loved other men.*

*I mentioned before; An old fashioned photograph which hangs on my wall. It is a glass "Daguerrotype" Portrait, commonly taken by itinerant photographers. This one shows a party of five men, who are grouped artistically on a wayside grass bank. They are all sitting. The highest at the back being John Ward, with his long spreading white beard and his general appearance of being like some religious picture of Moses, (but in 19th Century garb). The other four are: Uncle Alick, who is a little apart and sitting rather upright, Old Mr. Potter, who is on the left. Bill Harris, sitting against Uncle Ward, and my Father in the centre, reclining with the substantial support of Harris's knees.*

*They all have pipes, and all look very comfortable. My Father being the most comfortable of all, having made a couch for his back, against the legs of his companion.*

*It was evidently one of their Sunday morning excursions. These Sunday excursions were very frequent during summer time. They would assemble at nine a.m. at London Bridge, sandwich and flask in pocket. Then go off to Oxted; Leith Hill; Knockholt Beeches; Brasted Chart or Arundel, and thus would visit all the beauty spots in turn. This gave them all much walking during the spring and summer days, with much benefit to mind and body, and they would return home at night very tired; also with a handkerchief full of fern roots. (My Father was a fern enthusiast, and in his conservatory grotto at Oakley Road, grew and seeded most English ferns, from the delicate "Blechnums" and "Adiantums" to the great "Osmunda Regalis".*

*Now this photograph is splendidly characteristic of them all. They all have big slouch hats, and big beards, black frock coats, grey trousers and gold chains across black satin vests. And they look exactly like a group of well dressed prosperous Boers from the Cape. The keynote is: comfortable prosperity, with a grave cheerfulness, induced by the fact that they all had sloughed off the comfortless poverty of their early lives.*

*My Father, when his Father died, was sent by his Mother to go and find "a Place".*

*He was enquiring of a shopkeeper; "Did he want an errand boy" etc. when a Gentleman standing near, attracted by his good looks and open simplicity of manner, began to ask him questions. Eventually the gentleman accompanied the boy home to his Mother, and offered to take her son as an indoor apprentice.*

*The offer was accepted, and my Father became a "Silver Smith's Die Sinker", in the home of this gentleman, whose name was Newport; and whose house was opposite the British Museum. For the first year or two; the art of "Die Sinking", appears to have been acquired by my Father, chiefly by getting up at six a.m. lighting all the fires, cleaning all the household boots, all the knives and forks, and occasionally the windows. However! In between; he must have picked up the use of the file, the chisel, the gauger, the hammer and punch, and the various gravers. For he had been at least four years at his trade; when his Master, failing more and more to get orders for his dies in London, decided to leave this ungrateful city London, and go back to Birmingham. Before doing so, he managed to transfer the burden of my Father, by making a "turnover apprentice" of him; to Joseph Mansell, of Red Lion Square.*

*Joseph Mansell was at that time, personally cutting lace paper dies for Valentine making. What we call "The Fancy Stationery Trade" was in its earliest stages. But Fourdriniers introduction of the paper making machine, by which paper could be made in continuous rolls, had given a wonderful development to the uses of paper. Paper mills sprung up all over the country where there was found a river of pure water. The production of cheaper paper in great quantities enormously stimulated the Printing Trade. There were a great many Printers, PRINTING MACHINES being almost unknown. Hand press printing was slow enough to employ a great many people, to meet the demand for printed matter. Not only Newspapers, Ballads; Lampoons, Caricatures, etc;*

*But Fine Art Printing in Black and White had reached its apogee.*

*There were many Steel Plate and Copper Plate Engravers, and Etchers in all these methods. Nothing modern indeed, can equal the perfection of the Steel Plate Engraving of the Time of George the Fourth. Sharpe, the great line engraver, was courted by Reynolds, Gainsborough, Hogarth, Raeburn and all that wonderful galaxy of genius which bloomed like a tropical flower garden at that time. Sharpe sat in his studio; Dressed in a Crimson Velvet Coat, with Lace Ruffles and Diamond Buckle Shoes. And he offered fine wines amid luxurious furniture; to the Prince Regent and many other distinguished guests, who strolled in to look through their eye glasses at his dexterous gravers working on the great steel plate in front of him. He engraved with both hands, starting a line with the right hand to the middle of the plate, then starting a line with the left hand until over the line began with the right hand.*

*He was a wonderful artist. He earned fortunes, (and spent them) and his wonderful line engravings now fetch scores of pounds for a single copy, when Christies get them occasionally from some old mansion.*

*Wood engraving too, was to have its finest development fostered by the increasing demand for pictorial adornment of popular literature.*

*Albert Durer, three centuries previously, had invented wood engraving; and the crude bold "cross-hatch" work in his "Dance of Death", remains to this day a unique and perfect example of the forceful individuality of Mediaeval Art. Albert Durer had cut his blocks with lance like knives, out of a smoothed plank of some ordinary wood. Printing letters were at that time cut from ordinary wood (probably pear tree) which was first planed type high. And from Albert Durer to the Georgian period, illustrations were cut with coarse tools in a course fashion, (see Old Ballads in Museums) on coarse woods which had the grain running long ways. But with the inrush of cheap paper, the supply and demand for any kind of books became great, and naturally, every enterprising printer started the printing of a book.*

*One of these, William Bewick, a lover of nature and an artist as well as a printer, conceived the idea of printing a book of English Birds. It was a small book. Therefore the illustrations must be small. Bewicks drawings of birds, were too fine and delicate to be engraved on soft wood. Therefore! Bewick searched for harder wood, and at last found a stump of Box wood. He cut a slice segment ways, he polished it and drew his exquisite birds on it, and then engraved them with self made fine gravers. Bewick set up his type and blocks and printed the little book, with the love a woman has for a baby, or a Della Robia for his Bambinos, without care for the money it might bring.*

*(I bought at a second hand bookshop many years ago, a copy for a shilling. It is now worth a pound.)*

*With engraving on box wood, there began a race of enthusiastic artists and engravers, who raised wood engraving to a great art. Examples of the work of such men as Sir John Gilbert the artist, and W.J.. Linton, the Engraver, will be framed and sold at Christies before long, and will be valued, as Baxter's work is valued now.*

*For the art of wood engraving has been killed by a mechanical process of illustration, which is cheaper and quicker; but in which all "individuality" has been lost.*

*The same stimulating force of cheap paper meaning "art for the million" also produced Baxter.*

*Baxter, a Printer in Red Lion Square, had conceived the idea of Printing in Colours.*

*About the year 1850, Baxter took out a Patent for "Pictures printed in more than one colour". And he actually held, and defended his patent, for fourteen years. He seems to have used combinations of all methods of Printing. Steel and Copper Plates for his dark and definite lines; wood engraving and etched Zinc Plates for sky tints, etc. His work for Messrs: Nelson & Co. The Scotch Publishers of illustrated books, also his "Child's Companion" pictures, together with the Transfers for Tops of Pomaturn pots, etc. etc. are now all collected, highly valued and sold at big prices. They are all good, and show great personality. One can recognise "A Baxter" immediately one sees it, even a dozen yards away.*

*Baxter used Lithography only a little. The principle of Printing from a stone surface had only recently been discovered, and a most interesting discovery it was. It seems, a Quarryman at Solenhofen in Bavaria, had found by accident, that a piece of paper being rubbed on the side slabs of the stone that formed his fireplace, became printed in black with a reproduction of his wife's greasy finger marks. And further; though she tried to wash the finger marks away, they still worked up again, and became black again, with the smoke.*

*The Peasant told a printer! And the printer begged a flat piece of "the" stone. On this he made a drawing in black grease, wetted the stone; then dabbed oily black ink on the stone; which being wet, only "took" where the greasy drawing was. He than rubbed a sheet of paper over the whole! And "there you were". A new art. Pictures on paper without engraving! So Munich became the centre, from which was poured all over the world, cheap Lithographic copies of all the most beautiful steel engravings. Also multi-coloured copies of all the most beautiful paintings of the world. And Baxter`s monopoly made England late to compete with Bavaria, because no English printer could print in colours while Baxters patent was running (fourteen years.)*

*I have made the preceding digression to point out the stimulating effort of Fourdriners Cheap Machine made paper.*

*And this stimulation was equally evident in various writing papers.*

*When postage was very dear, few letters were written, and writing paper, being "hand made", was very expensive; limited in kinds, and only on sale at one or two places. Dobbs Kidd & Co. of Fleet Street was one of these places. The firm Dobbs Kidd & Co. was an enterprising firm. They cut up into convenient sizes, note papers and envelopes of any kind of good paper that came along.*

*More than that, they employed Seal Engravers to cut aristocratic*

*Crests on steel dies, and this produced your "Crested Relief" stamped Note Paper.*

*They also introduced pretty neat goffered edge "Note", and Coloured edge "Note", and "Note" with a Little Flower embossed on the top in the place of a crest.*

*All these fancy novelties had-a big sale, and it became "The Fashion" among ladies to use an Ornamental note paper.*

*A man named John Attenborough was Manager to Dobbs Kidd. He was a natural artist, a seal engraver, a die sinker, and a "genius" for devising various developments of ornamental paper. Among other ideas, he had a "True Lover's Knot", embossed as a crest on a Note paper with a narrow lace border, "which was just a series of holes).*

*This, he put-on the market as "Billets a deux", for use at St. Valentines season. It was a great success. Each sheet was retailed for 6d. And the great sale induced Dobbs Kidd to retail larger and more elaborate-designs, next season. And it also induced several competing stationers to publish imitations. Silver Smiths Die Sinkers- abounded; and big screw presses were plentiful. Not only Birmingham Metal Works used them, but Bookbinders. Every Bible, Prayer Book and Hymn Book, at that period had an-embossed Cover. ("Engine turned" design.)*

*And among all these rival printers who had a screw press, and who could see the coming custom of sending laced-paper on St. Valentine's Day, as a suggestion of Love was Joseph Mansell. And to Joseph Mansell, Henry Mullord (my Father) was apprenticed.*

*Joseph Mansell was a Die Sinker as well as a Printer;*

*In fact he was one of those quiet ingenious thinking men, so often found in the ranks of Printers, Witness: the American Franklin; Guttenburg, Caxton, and a score of others I could mentioned who attained fame.           John Heywood was a Printer whom I should like to mention. He started a newspaper about 1830, wrote and printed in it strong "leaders" against the Government stamp on literature sent by post; Was elected member of Parliament by Manchester on the strength of his opinions. Soon after he defied the House of Commons,and said he should obstruct the business of the House until a bill was passed for cheap postage of Newspapers. He did so, and was locked up in the Clock Tower for doing it; A row in the House ensued. A strong radical party at last defeated the Government over the question, and the Tax on "The dissemination of knowledge" was repealed. So John Heywood came out of prison, A national hero.*

*And this other printer:       Joseph Mansell, had the "Flair" of knowing what was going to be "A good trade".*

*He cut "lace edge" dies, and sold laced papers to Printers and Stationers; Who decorated them with flowers and amatory verses, and sold them to the shops as "Valentines". So the great "Valentine" business was started. The lace paper Valentines became more elaborate each year. One paper being super-imposed on the other by "Springs", made a box a necessity. And the cost of a Valentine became so intensified by satin, perfume, and artificial flowers; that "Fiimmels", in the Strand, sold scores at a guinea each, and J.T. Wood of the Strand, sold £300 to £400 worth per week, (wholesale and retail) for a month before St. Valenitine's Day. And naturally Valentine Makers sprang up everywhere, and Valentines were sold all over the land.*

*Joseph Mansell and my Father, working at the same bench, could not cut the dies fast enough, the demand for new designs being so great.*

*At last! My Father "came out of his time". His apprenticeship ended; He was offered by Joseph Mansell, and accepted, Thirty Shillings per week, as a full fledged Die Sinker.*

*This munificient wage enabled him to get married. He married SARAH BLACKBOROUGH. (MY MOTHER). She was a finely developed woman, tall but not stout. She had an immense amount of dark brown hair which she wore in curls a foot long. She had hazel brown eyes, red lips, and a rich complexion. Mother was the daughter of a Typical Volatile irishman, who probably came from the Blackboroughs of County Clare. He had been in the Navy. He told me he had taken part in the Battle of Copenhagen.*

*Her Mother was black haired and dark, and so much like a gipsy, that I think she might have been one. My Mother inherited the temperament of both. From her Father, his high spirit, with impulsive, enterprising and energetic moods; but also an Irish lack of persistance. That is: she would slacken off old "things", to take up new.*

*She was passionately affectionate, and made of my Father and her children, everything that to her was worth thinking of, or troubling about.*

*From her Gipsy like Mother, she inherited a Romantic nature, and an irresistible love of a wild unconventional life. To rush away from Cities now and again, was a necessity for her. A necessity which she gave to me, her first born, and this in myself, is intensified by a students knowledge, which her limited education*

*denied her.*

*My Parents were married at Islington Church, 1848. They took first floor apartments in Bridport Place (next door to Gibbings the Bakers). I was born there. My Mother used to tell me how she loved sitting on the little balcony with fancy railings, at the front window; and looking across at the Great Flower and Vegetable Gardens. For at that time (1849) only the West side of Bridport*

*Place was built.*

*From Hoxton Church on the south, to the North side of Hyde Road; and on the east: St. Johns Road; was a vast open space devoted to Market gardening. Charles Lamb speaks of it in the "Essays of Elia".He says: "How sad yet how beautiful it was to walk, hand in hand with his sister, through the sweet flower plantations to "Hoggesdon House". This was the Mad house which is still at the top of Hoxton. It is a forbidding looking structure now, but I recollect it as a fine old Queen Anne Mansion.*

*Lamb used to take his sister there periodically. For she had fits of Homicidal Mania. And I believe, had thrown a Carving Knife at their parent (Maternal parent) which had killed her.*

*Very soon after I was born, my Mother's energy desired an outlet and she persuaded Father to invest his savings in a little "General" Shop which she could manage.*

*She did manage it, and they made money, but it was hard work. Open Sunday mornings, as well up to 12 p.m. on Saturday nights.*

*I have frequently seen this little shop since. A shop where you could buy a Pennyworth of anything, and farthings were not despised. It was in Bowling Green Lane, (I believe the "Bowling Green Tavern" is still there, but the Bowling Green at the back of it was only a memory, for my Father to talk about, when I was a child), at the back of what is now Clerkenwell Road. And my only personal recollection of living there, is the occasion on which I was severely reprimanded, for pushing up the "squeeze off" handle of the huge gold decorated Can of Treacle, which stood just where a precocious child could touch the fascinating outlet with his finger. As a steady thin thread of treacle had been running all night, early morning customers had walked in it, and made footprints everywhere; before the crime was discovered and the criminal punished.*

*While we were living at the shop with Aunt Kate as our "lady help" and housekeeper, an important thing happened. It occurred that Father had been asked to sing at a Smoking Benefit Concert. He had a pleasant Tenor voice (all the Mullords had good voices). So at this concert, Father sang his simple sentimental song, which I well remember. It ran thus:*

*"Oh fly with me dear Lady,*

*It is thine own one calls,*

*Oh fly with me across the blue lake,*

*Oh fly with me across the blue lake,*

*It is thy Tru-u-ue love calls,*

*It is etc etc etc etc etc.*

*Father; flushed with excitement and applause stepped off the platform, and was "seized hold of" by a Stout prosperous looking man who was seated in the front row. This man had a big gold chain on, an extended waistcoat ,and was smoking a big cigar. He said to Father: "Come and sit next to me!. Have a cigar!. I want to talk to you!. My name is John Windsor. Your name is Mullord. You are a Die Sinker. You are working for Joseph Mansell. My artist; Alfred Pernet, has told me of you. I am going in for making Valentine papers, I want you to come and work for me, and cut all my dies.*

*Here is a roll of Alfred Pernet's designs, take them!. I will give you a written order to cut them all for me. There is about a hundred and fifty pounds worth of work to start with.*

*If you will take it on, you can charge me at the rate of four pounds per week, and I like your style, and will trust you to deal honourably with me."*

*Says Father; "I must ask my wife first."*

*Says Windsor: "Quite so! I will come round home now with you."*

*\* An interview with Mother, who! you may guess, bridled under the compliments and admiring glances of bluff John Windsor; soon settled the matter, and it was arranged that Father was to look for a workshop at once and begin as soon as possible. And further; John Windsor had a boy just home from Boarding School; this son he offered to apprentice to my Father, for seven years service as out door apprentice. He did it and gave Father a premium of twenty-five pounds.*

*As a new baby was coming, my Parents at once sold the shop at a good profit, and went to live at Canal Terrace, Peter Street, Islington, which had a workshop at the back, and a beautiful view in front. I remember there was a green bank which ran down to the towpath of the Canal. On the right, was a little old grey bridge, on the left, a perspective of water and bridges, a towpath, a green bank with an Elderberry bush here and there, and in front, the canal branched out into what was called: "A Basin", on each side of which, towered up huge piles of timber, resembling the modern sky scrapers of New York. And all day!. Along the Canal, passed a pageant of gaily painted sailing barges and fly boats. These fly boats were long narrow swift barges, painted red and green and yellow, and steered by country women with flapping hoods on their heads and babies in their arms. It was all just a bit of Holland in Summer weather. And the romance of it, filled my little soul, though I was only four years old.*

*Alas! this picturesque Canal traffic has passed away, one sees only a melancholy coal barge at intervals. By a criminal conspiracy of Railway Directors, it was arranged that the Railway Companies should buy all the canals in the Kingdom, and then divert the traffic on to the Railways. So the Canal Traffic, once so charming, so useful and so quiet, has almost ceased, and the nation is dependant on to the Railways for carrying. A matter which in future will cause much*

*wailing and gnashing of teeth.*

*We remained some year or two, living at Canal Terrace ,but Father was making money, there-fore: Ambition and My Mother, urged improvements in social appearances.*

*So! We moved to Hanover Street. Our house was opposite the "Island Queen", a tavern which at that period, kept open till three in the morning. This was exciting; because my parents could wake and hear John Ward and various other gentlemen, come out noisily after their bibulous game of Cribbage or Loo, at two o`clock in the morning, which was not then regarded as unusually late, for a man to think of going back to the bosom of his wife and family.*

*Soon! a further step up the social ladder, took us to No. 29 Essex Street, Peter Street, where the Landlord had built a nice workshop for Father, who had now taken two other apprentices. For William Windsor was a "go-ahead" man, and he loaded Father with orders. And also took him to the Derby in fine style, and likewise invited myself and my Sister Emma to his daughter Nellie's Christmas Party at Coppice Row. In fact; he made pets of us, and everything was blooming, and flourishing and well cut Lace Paper Dies designed by Pernet and engraved by my Father, filled Windsor's soul with pride, and his pockets with gold.*

*One Sunday! My Father and his Brother Alick took their morning walk in the direction of the Rosemary Branch Tavern, where there was talk of building. (When we lived in Hanover Street our littleservant took myself and little sister for a walk on the towpath of the canal as far as the "Rosemary Branch Tavern", to see a Baloon "go up", from the gardens at the back in very extensive grounds, where now the White Lead Factory is. There was to be a clown on a donkey "slung" to the baloon. This affair only "came off" in the sense that the rope broke - but I remember it being a real country walk.)*

*They inspected the first two houses in Penn Street, which were just erected, but not finished. A persuasive builder, who, spider-like, had hidden in one of the rooms, pointed out the great advantages these houses possessed over all other houses, and offered to sell them at the low price of £300 each, (with £5 ground rent.)*

*Father returned greatly excited. Mother was taken to look. She approved! And in the course of a week or two, and by the aid of the Planet Building Society, Uncle Alick had purchased No. 1, and my Father was-the proud possessor of Nos. 2 and 3. Number 3 being only half built, and the remainder of Penn Street not built at all.*

*Workshops at the back of 1 and 2 were built quickly. "The present press-shop at Penn Street consists of these two workshops, with the dividing wall pulled down.) And Henry and Alexander Mullord, with their respective families, were installed in their own houses. Nos. 1 and 2 (No. 3 being let to a Mr. Brown.) This was probably the most momentous step in both their lives, and not only affected them, but hundreds of other people.*

*For! Between the two workshops a little door was cut, and through this door, the two brothers would be continually talking to each other in between their work, and thus! A community of thought and interests arose, which insensibly established a partnership.*

*A partnership which afterwards materialised, and from which developed the business known as "H. & A. Mullord Ltd." A business which has been in existence now some sixty years, and which, incidentally, during that time, has paid wages to its surrounding neighbours, of about a quarter of a million pounds.*

*The actual partnership arose this way:*

*When Henry Mullord moved into Penn Street, he had two apprentices to feed and keep and find work for, besides his own family, and this he found difficult, because, his wave of prosperity had suddenly received a great shock.*

*John Windsor, his great and generous patron, who had showered work upon him, was suddenly a Bankrupt.*

*He had lived beyond his means, as large hearted men will do. Then followed a dreadful time, everybody was sorry for good old John Windsor. But now! He being done, nobody wanted my Father to cut dies. Meek, of Crane Court, Fleet Street, and other lace paper makers, said! "Oh! we now will use our old dies, and Valentine makers must put up with the same designs year after year.*

*However! Father got an order or two from various people; And becoming full of ideas, he bought an embossing machine cheap, and had it fixed at the end of the workshop. (A fortnight after it was fixed, I, then an investigating school-boy, managed to get my hand crushed by this press.)*

*This same press was afterwards taken up, and fixed on the bench on right hand of the present press shop. It is the end one.*

*When the press was originally fixed, my Father embossed on it, and laced a "scrap" die, and taking the first lace paper from it to Goode, of Clerkenwell Green; asked if he would buy some.*

*Goode said:  "Fifty reams at six and six, and the same with as many new dies as you can produce."*

*Uncle Alick was as excited as Father over this; for they both tried and found that all Valentine makers would give them many orders for anything "new" in lace papers.*

*Here was an opportunity for two men, who were "at their wits end" as to how to carry on. (Uncle Alick's Wire Drawing business had also failed, as I shall tell later on.)*

*A partnership was now ratified. They joined their capital and their heads together, and laboured like men whose lives hang in the balance.*

*It was quite dramatic!.*

*Not only every Valentine Maker offered them big orders for any new design they could produce, but offered untold inducements for speed.*

*To discern THE EXTENT of your opportunity, when it arrives, is the faculty of your earnest worker, and your man of imagination.*

*So! Here was Alexander, with his practical way of knowing how to go about "anything", and Henry, with his technical knowledge of Die Sinking, embossing, and the requirements of the Valentine trade. It naturally followed; that these two enthusiastic workers, made a Phenomenal business, out of what appeared to be nothing.*

*As partners, they were happily mated; not so much because Alexander was a strong minded man, made to command and get the utmost possible out of every man he employed; But more because Henry was of a gentle nature, and so allowed himself to play "Second Fiddle", with cheerful acquiescence. An admiring confidence in his brother's ability to overcome all difficulties, made Henry throw all his fine imagination into his part of the work, and gave to Alexander just that backing that he required, to put all his best energies into play, in a business he had not been familiar with.*

*Many partnerships fail through jealousy But this was an ideal Partnership between two Brothers who had much respect and affection each for the other. And although there were differences of opinion at times, there was never a quarrel.*

*Going back to the start, let us see what they had to do.*

*For assets, they had three houses and workshops, (only partly paid for) a little capital, an embossing press, two or three dies, considerable brains, and promise of many orders.*

*They held a Council of War every evening at seven, and this must have been the outcome of their resolutions at the beginning.*

*1.         George and Flood (the Die Sinker`s apprentices) shall emboss at the press for half of each day, to keep two lacers going.*

*(There were lacers. to be had, who had worked for Windsor; A lacer   was a man who fitted the embossed paper into the die, and scrubbed the back of the embossed paper till it was covered with exquisite perforated work.)*

*2.         Henry gets two lacers; Old Gibbs from Mansells and COX, from Meeks of Crane Court.*

*3.         Get a top and bottom press for cutting embossed "back" papers into ornamental shaped.*

*4.         Make more "dies" and "top and bottom" tools.*

*5.         Get an engineer (Bell) to come part time to fix various second-hand presses which kept "turning up" in the market, (rusty and bought at Old Iron prices).*

*6.         Bell -(The drunken clever old engineer) suggests "Ships-timbers" for benches on which to put presses. Goes with Alexander to Chelsea Ship Yard and buys a lot cheap. (Still there, near the Tate Gallery!).*

*Much carting work and trolley work for Lockyer, the Greengrocer at the corner of Poole Street, at Contract price of 1/- per hour, for a man, two horses and van.*

*(Lockyer: "My Lor! a mighty, Wherever do you put it all, when you gets it through that front kitchen winder").*

*(The Front Kitchen window, of No. 2 had been converted into two folding doors.)*

*The Financial struggle came at the beginning.*

*First the "Deeds" were redeemed from the Planet, and lodged at L. & C. Bank, Islington, with powers to draw up to value.*

*A ream of paper was bought for cash and carried home personally, pending the opening of a paper account to £20. "Bookbinders long and short knives", were bought and used two years before they could venture on buying a guilotine. And one by one, Boys and Men were taken on for Embossing, Cutting, etc. All the family on both sides contributed labour. My Mother did some relief stamping in the back kitchen, and incidentally, much good paper was spoilt by a nice rich violet ink, which had a singular capacity for turning up everywhere, and in the wrong spots.*

*Aunt Annie did some folding, till her daughter Annie could do it quicker. This daughter Annie became very expert at colour bordering; counting; folding and sorting, and a valuable help in all ways.*

*And so! The business grew. Amazingly it grew!.*

*Many dies were cut; A printing press was added, and lace papers were printed in gold and silver. And Alexander and Henry worked hard,            with a great increase in money profit. And they met all difficulties as they cropped up. So! In ten years; were employing fifty people. And probably were making a profit of more than a thousand a year. But most of this went back into the business to increase it. An increasing business absorbs increasing capital, and this business was being increased all the time.*

*Not only the two workshops at the back were joined into one, and "lofts" superimposed; but all the rooms in the three dwelling houses became gradually "taken on" by "The Business".*

*Henry Mullord was first to live somewhere else.*

*While the business was in its chaotic infancy, the uncertainty of the future, and his wife's confidence in the enterprise, led him to buy the goodwill of a Grocery Shop, together with the leasehold house, of which the shop was part. This was at the corner of Benyon Road, Southgate Road. This house and shop with a little half shop at the side, he bought for £400. £6. g.r.*

*The idea was: that his wife, (my Mother) should manage the shop with the aid of the growing family, viz: myself as errand boy, and my sister Emma as "counter and packing".*

*Then! As it was a growing family, we could consume a good deal of the stock. And from Friends and Relations could be "wangled" order's, so! with the customers from the neighbourhood, it was easy to see a prosperous and lucrative business arise.*

*Alas! These forecasts were not fulfilled. It is true, a great deal was taken from stock and consumed by the Family; But relations and friends, with one accord, after a few weeks, declared that they bought better stuff at lower prices elsewhere, and gave practical demonstrations of these facts.*

*Also! The neighbours, who ought to have flocked in to purchase goods because of their proximity, preferred to go to Hoxton to buy the bulk, and only purchased small lots at the Benyon Road shop when they wanted credit.*

*Naturally, the first fervour and rosy glamour of successful shopkeeping faded like a dream, disappointment and boredom succeeding.*

*Moreover! after a while, the necessity of having a "second string to his bow" no longer existed.*

*The firm of H. & A~ Mullord had established itself as a solid lucrative concern.*

*So Henry Mullord sold the "goodwill" of the business; (and possibly the purchaser) then abandoned the thorny path of grocery, and also the house as a residence.*

*Prompted by the social aspirations of his wife, he bought Nos. 48 and 49 Oakleigh Road, Southgate Road. And to 48 they all moved, with great inflation of dignity and pride. And there Henry Mullord and his family lived for many happy years. Until at last, his dear wife died suddenly!. At the age of (about) 50.*

*It is difficult for me; Her eldest son, to express what I still feel as to the immense and unusual amount of affection that she poured out; not only on her husband, whom she adored, but on myself and the rest of her children.*

*This especially sad event; the death of Sarah, Henry Mullord's wife, marks the time of a very great change.*

*Previously, for about twelve years, Henry and Sarah Mullord and their numerous family lived happily at Oakleigh Road. There was plenty of money, the business had gone on prospering, and Henry was now above all financial anxieties and was getting rich. He had long ago left the workshop, and was now leisurely and as a gentleman, representing the firm by calling on City Customers in the morning only.*

*His wife Sarah, besides keeping a well furnished house and a good table, gave open hospitality to all and sundry Relatives and Friends.*

*And this included friends of all the children*

*But there came, alas! a time when she sickened, was ill for months, and then died. And from that sad time, a tragical breaking up of the even flow of prosperous family life ensued.*

*Besides Father and Mother, there had been nine of us. Myself, Henry, born 1849. Then Emma, a year intervening. George followed, but died as a baby. Christopher came next, then Kate, followed by Alfred, Alick, Albert and Amy. All of these brothers and sisters of mine, gave me more than the usual family affection, which affection I returned to the full extent that my rather selfish and self centred nature admitted. (There are now only two living. Myself and Albert.)*

*Emma married William Villiers, a bookbinder by trade, a rather small man, good looking, pleasant mannered, and he had a very generous, pleasant disposition. They had two children, Harry and Amy; who are living and are much esteemed by me. Amy is one of my particular "pals". Emma and her husband were fond of each other and had a fairly happy life, but both died at about the age of 45 years.*

*Christopher came next. Christopher was the saint of the family. He was very delicate in constitution; was very gentle in manner, and although he became a man; I think he knew he would die at an early age. He was sincerely religious, in a quiet reticent way, and I think he never intentionally committed a sin, or indulged in a vice; during the whole of his life.*

*He married when about twenty four years of age; a friend of the family, a Miss Eleanor Young. She was a dark eyed, vivacious and pretty girl. They went to live at East End Finchley, and Chris would travel to Penn Street once a week with the dies he cut. For he had been put to the "Die Sinking" as a business that would suit him, and he became an expert and most artistic "Die Sinker". The child sleeping at the foot of a Cross was one of his dies. One of the most perfect bits of Steel Die Sinking the world has ever seen.*

*But unfortunately, at Finchley they lived next door to a Butchers shop. At last poor Chris got ill. They consulted a Doctor who advised the young couple to leave the house and smell at once. They hurriedly moved up to London and took rooms at 20 Hyde Road. Here Chris got worse, developed Typhoid Fever; and. in a fortnight was dead. (Dying on the same day as John Ward.)*

*Eleanor, the young widow; had a child two months after her dear husband's death. There was much consultation as to what she could do with the baby, if she had to earn her living, with the result that Hannah (my wife) agreed to adopt the child as she had none of her own, and this child became our daughter for the time she grew up, and we had much affection for her, which affection seemed to be laid aside for a time by me, when she got married, for which I hope she will forgive me.*

*I must now return to Henry Mullord, (My Father).*

*When his wife Sarah died, the management of the house at Oakleigh Road fell upon his daughter Kate. Kate was an earnest, good hearted girl. She did her best, but the home was not the same to her Father. He was now a lonely man, living in a house with a family of self-willed and unruly offspring of various ages. My Father, at this time of life, was a stout handsome, healthy, lusty man, to whom a wife was an absolute necessity. Therefore!*

*It was not surprising, that some months after his first wife's death, he should astonish his numerous family by bringing home "A new Mother".*

*He had been introduced to a lady named Hannah Turpin. He found her good looking and amiable. He had proposed, been accepted, and was married, all in a very short space of time.*

*One change brings others. Emma was already married. The eldest son (myself) decided to marry at once. So did Chris. Our Father, always generous, gave each of us sixty pounds towards furniture, and we both settled at Finchley.*

*This still left a boisterous family for the new stepmother to tackle which task she took on cheerfully but with a requisite firmness.*

*Soon Kate; who had been housekeeper, got married to Charles Gregory.*

*Kate was a dear, affectionate, good looking girl. She had two boys, and then sad to say; Died! giving birth to another child.*

*Alfred, Alick and Albert, in the course of a year or so, also left the parental nest, and each married and began to quickly have children, that is: except Alick, who however, is worth more than a few words. Alick was a bit of a genius in his way. He was like the Poet Shelley in features, and was a Poet by nature; and wrote many verses, because he could not help it. He was impulsive, ardent, very pleasant in manner, and made friends everywhere. He was full of intelligence, and consequently at first was successful in all he took in hand. He had been a commercial traveller in his Father's firm of H. & A. Mullord Ltd. from the age of sixteen. The firm did not pay him a fair and living wage. This was apparent when he got married, so he started for himself, as manufacturer of Indoor Games, and established a business which is still prosperous. It is known as The Multum in Parvo Co.*

*Alick had taken in with him in this business, his younger brother Albert, who is now part owner and sole manager of the present "Firm".*

*Alick, when a boy, had a severe illness. He had bathed too often and too long in the sea at Hastings. He caught cold in the lungs and had developed Pleurisy.*

*He was two or three months in bed at Hastings, was "tapped" and patched up by the Doctors and turned out as cured, as far as they knew.*

*But he was not really cured. And afterwards, his business of "travelling", was not the sort of life to lead to a cure. He at last developed rapid consumption and died at the age of thirty-five. A much loved and regretted man.*

*Another son, Alfred, had started as an Artist and Engraver. His business in Farringdon Street is still being carried on as "A. Mullord & Co." The lady he married (Maggie) had given him four children, and they all lived fairly happily, with some ups and downs, for about sixteen years, and then Maggie died.*

*Alfred married again; and had a child, (Muriel). Then! He became ill, and was ailing for a year or two. The Great War broke out; and at once his business dropped seventy five per cent. His illness developed into a bad affection of the throat. The worry of money affairs made him worse, and at last, the poor chap died in hospital at the age of 54.*

*His second wife; Annie was most devoted to him, and as best she could, carried on the business for him. She was, and is, a very brave woman, and she still "carries on" as A. Mullord & Co. and makes a good living.*

*After reciting the sad memoirs inscribed in the last few pages, we must go back to Henry Mullord, the Father of all these children.*

*When Henry (myself), Christopher; and soon after, Alfred, Kate, Alick and Albert, all left home and got married, each taking with them their Father's cordial blessing, best wishes, and a substantial sum of money; it left Henry Mullord Senior alone with his second wife, and one child from his first marriage. This child was Amy, then about twelve years of age.*

*This trio started a new life. The Oakleigh Road house was too large and was full of sad memories, so they all went to live at Dagmar Hoad, Finsbury Park, and the new wife made it a very happy home for all.*

*Henry Mullord was now a "well off" man and there was no need, and no opportunity for him to work. His place at the Penn Street Factory was taken by his eldest son Henry, (myself) and his outdoor occupation by his son Alick.*

*They made a happy little family. He cultivated a garden, and his wife Hannah kept house in a meticulously perfect manner, and Amy attended school; unfortunately with the poorest results. For it soon became apparent that her brain was undeveloped. "Take her out and about!" and the Doctor, and Father, being a great lover of the quiet country took them both on numerous trips.*

*In my cottage dining room hangs an old glass Daguerrotype Portrait of the Trio. They are sitting on the grass of some country roadside. They look fine. Father is plump and round and good looking; His wife Hannah, looks both stylish and rakish in her Dolly Varden hat and many flounced mid-victorian muslin dress, and Amy looks just the artless big limbed fat child she was.*

*Sad to say, Amy was not cured by being taken about, she became more childish; then developed a mania for going away and astray. Slipping out of the house in an artful manner, and being found and brought home by strangers. This mania took her by night, and she climbed out of her bedroom window and over various garden walls in scanty night attire.*

*So she became worse, and a continual anxiety, until at last she was placed in a Private Asylum. The one known as "Lion House, Green Lanes". She did not stay long, for she wandered about in the middle of the night, her bare feet on the tesselated tiled floor. Of course, this gave her a bad cold, which turned to Pneumonia, and she died in a week.*

*We went to see her two days before; she looked wondrously beautiful. Her eyes brilliant, and her cheeks flushed with fever. (She was always a beautiful girl.) "Oh, take me home" she said, "Oh take me home"! It is in times such as this, that we realise how impotent we are; and how unavailing is the mere shedding of tears. If we cannot blame ourselves for "leaving undone those things we might have done" we are lucky.*

*Father, and his wife Hannah, now moved away from Finsbury Park and went to Wimbledon. Father bought a house at the top of Park Road. It was a sunny house, in quite beautiful part of Wimbledon, and they have both been very happy, and would have been, but that the health of both began to go wrong.*

*Father complained of headaches and tiredness, and one day he had a collapse. A sort of fainting fit. The Doctor was called in. He was advised to rest etc.*

*Then another day, walking together on the Common, Hannah was struck by a golf ball on the breast. Though not appearing serious at first; after a year, it was found an operation was needful.*

*This sadly upset their lives, and then Father was again troubled with a serious fainting fit.*

*He became uncertain in his health; And suddenly one morning; we received a telegram from Hannah to say Father was dying. He had proceeded to take his morning bath; whistling in return to the Canary's salute, when suddenly she heard him fall, went to him, and found him lying unconscious. He never recovered. The Certificate stated that the Cause of Death was "Sclerosis".*

*So ended HENRY MULLORD. I think he was the most loving and most beloved, of all the Mullords. An amiable and pleasant, most intelligent but artless man.*

*Who! being the reverse of strong-minded, attracted the goodwill and affection of all who met him.*

*He died at the premature age of 58, was buried at Abney Park, and was followed to the grave by crowds of relatives and workpeople.*

*His wife mourned him for only about two years, when she also died. (The blow on the breast had caused Cancer.)*

*She was a good woman; with a keen sense of justice. Thus: when she helped her husband to make his will, she insisted on the bulk of his fortune going to his children. She only to have a very small income. He would have given her much more, but she refused with a smile.*

*Now we come to the youngest of this family of Mullords:-*

*ALEXANDER MULLORD.*

*Alexander was the most Important Mullord of the lot.*

*He was a great man; Not physically:      for he was small, thin and frail in the body, but mentally, and by force of a strong personality; he was a giant among men.*

*All men have their faults. Therefore Alexander Mullord had his faults, but we shall not discuss them, (De Mortuis nil nisi bonum). For one thing! Apart from anything else, he had fewer faults and more virtues than has the average of mankind. And I have come to the conclusion that he was possessed with greater wisdom than is given to most men.*

*I have said he was a "great man"! I think we may define a "great" man, as one who has the ability and succeeds in doing, a thing or things, in excess and above, those that the average man has the ability to do; or having the ability, does not do.*

*Alexander, (whom in future I will call "Uncle Alick") was born, at the lowest ebb of the Mullords fortune. His Mother reared him in direst poverty. This was the hard discipline his keen mind was most conscious of, and his firm resolute nature concentrated itself on the one purpose; The determination not to be poor. His environment was such, and the mental atmosphere of the times was such, that his way upward was confined to a simple narrow path, namely that money saved; "Capital", however small, was necessary before you could round and choose which way you should go. In those early Victorian days there were no "get rich quick" paths open for brainy people as there are now. Simple economic saving was the long and narrow way. But, Uncle Alick was one of those few who have a man's head on a boy's shoulders, therefore he had his purpose in view at an age. The purpose was that of "getting on". And from the first time of his going to work, his savings began. The Sekford Street Savings Bank took deposits as low as a shilling, and so he was owner of a Bank book, even when a young boy at "Wooleys".*

*His work at Wooleys (which was his first "place") was mingling the sheets with the zinc plates at the rolling machine. A monotonous job, that soon palled on his active mind. To be a mere pawn in the game; to be a mere one of the lot of factory boys employed there, was not individualistic enough for him, so he soon left, but his eyes had drank in much, and the knowledge gained of cardboard making and stationery manufacturing, was to be of great value to him in later life. At the age of thirteen he persuaded his elder brother Thomas to take him as an apprentice to the gold wire drawing. So he then went to work under his brother; who was Foreman at "Corneys" in Little Britain, as I have previously stated.*

*"Corneys" were gold lace and gold braid makers. There were several of these firms in Little Britain at that time. They were the survival of a splendid Colony of Huguenots who were Weavers of Silk and thin gold wire into wonderful tapestrys so well known in the West of France. These Huguenots had fled from Bretagne across the channel to England after the Massacre of St. Bartholomews Day. They were welcomed in England, and settled like a swarm of bees; in the locality bounded by Newgate Street, Aldersgate Street, Long Lane and St. Bartholomews Hospital. There they flourished; kept going by the Army and Navy, and also by ecclesiastical work. And probably for a hundred years, French was the only language to be heard in that part of London. Many of these Huguenots became rich merchants selling their wares in Wood Street, and Newgate Street.*

*In my time; fifty years ago, there were many French names to be seen on the shops. The only one now left in Newgate Street is "Faudels". The French Protestant Church they built is still existing, and you can hear a Sermon in French there on certain Sundays. In many of the back courts and alleys about Cloth Fair, you may still see the weavers windows, stretching right across the first floor. But these relics of the past will soon all disappear. And alas! nothing remains of the old French Colony, except the name of "Little Bretagne" (Little Britain.)*

*Uncle Alick had the nick name among his fellow workmen of "old Brownie". This was because he wore a long brown overcoat through all the winters of his apprenticeship. Coats were very expensive then, but they were of cloth of such durability that cannot now be bought. You may be sure however; that although the coat was old, it was neatly mended. He said to me once, "Patches are good, and indicate economy, but ragged clothing is abominable. It speaks of a careless lazy disposition."*

*However! Brownie was much respected by his fellow workmen, for although a youngster, he was a strong disputant and would argue with them; till they fled in dumb despair, to the Rose and Lily, or the Black Bull, for consolation. But he was the more respected for the fact, that he always had money to lend. They said to each other "Brownie's a puffic wonder, e's always got arf a quid up `is sleeve!.!."*

*I have no doubt he made sure of getting his money back, and none would blame him if he charged an interest. But this reputation of "having money" stuck to him all his life. For all people in trouble and distress seemed to naturally turn to him for help. And I personally know, (or believe}, that he never refused that money help which he was being asked for, though he knew quite well that the "loan" had little prospect of being repaid to him.*

*His economy was methodical. He said to me: "I am often tempted! I often see something I should like to buy; but I have made it a rule in such cases, to leave the matter over for a fortnight, and I have usually found that the article has been sold, or that I no longer craved for it, at the end of that period."*

*On the other hand, if it was a means to an end, he would spend his money freely. And I well remember a great pile of about twelve immense Books standing in his kitchen. It was "Chanbers Encyclopedia". A noble work; full of illustrations, and an endless gold mine to an intelligent mind. Uncle Alick had seen it when a boy, at a second hand shop. It was 25/-. He had paid his half crown deposit, and regularly paid his shilling once a week, till that inexhaustible store of information became his own.*

*Uncle Alick came of age, about the year 1848. It was a time of early marriages, and also a time when an Artisan took a wife as a helpmate, in every sense of the word.*

*While yet in his "teens", Uncle Alick had passed every day, the girl who was to be his wife. This girl was the daughter of Mrs. Evans, a widow. Mrs. Evans had formerly lived in the country, about a mile the other side of the pretty Hertfordshire village called "St. Margarets" which is situated on the river Lea, some distance beyond Broxbourne. The row of cottages, in one of which she lived; stands rather high on the left, just before you come to the source of the "New River". (I have seen it.)*

*When her husband died, after a long illness poor Mrs. Evans brought her little savings and furniture up to London, as a likely place in which to get a living. She got work of a sort, and her daughter Annie had to carry home that work each morning. In doing so, Uncle Alick and she met each day, became acquainted; then courted; and soon after he was "out of his time" , married.*

*Uncle Alick had found a mate who suited him exactly. Aunt Annie was passive and negative in temperament. Had she have been self assertive and felt her own importance too acutely, as so many wives do, there would probably have been a continual combat of wills, and such "ructions" as would have made a very pleasant topic of conversation for the rest of the family. But no! with the wisdom of a Solomon!. Uncle Alick had chosen a mate who was amiable; and ready to accept his decision in anything as final. At the same time; willing to work for him, and live on frugal fare, and be satisfied in her heart that her man was going to "make good".*

*As for economy, they both knew the value of saving. I will give a characteristic examply.*

*At the period in which they were courting, say, the year 1847, the small coin called "a farthing", (or fourth thing") now almost unknown; was then in the same position as our present halfpenny; a useful, but much despised piece of money. Just as we had penny bazaars before the War; So then! there were "Farthing Shops" in poor neighbourhoods, shops at which you could buy a "farthing worth" of anything; Tea, Treacle, Pickles or even Coals. Consequently farthings, were in frequent circulation, and came as "change" to everyone. Uncle Alick found that Aunt Annie had instituted a small savings box in which she made a point of dropping in every farthing she found in her pocket each morning. He did the same thing. As they both said, "You cannot feel the loss of a farthing, but the sound of a hundred of them rattled in a box, warmed your heart, and braced your energies for the "Daily Toil" and the "Common Task".*

*When the time came to be married. They emptied the two boxes into a common fund, which they consecrated to the purpose of paying all the marriage fees. Which it did nicely.*

*Having served his apprenticeship, Uncle Alick was offered thirty shillings a week as Journeyman.*

*"No!" he said. "I am for independance!" "What my brains and hands can do, shall be for my own benefit. Some are born to be masters, and some are born to be journeymen. I fear, but I will venture on the unknown road. For I feel I am born to be A Master." He rented a small house on the right hand of Brittania Street,*

*Murray Street. He took his young wife there, together with his Mother, who was now dependant on him. It was arranged that She (Grandmother) should help in the housework, and that would give Aunt Annie time to help Uncle Alick in his business.*

*As I have said: He had determined to be a master, even if only a "garret master" and doing "work for the Trade". That is, doing "wire drawing" for "Corneys" and various other gold lace makers, who were then established in Little Britain and Spitalfields.*

*So he converted the Back Kitchen into a workshop, put up his own benches, on which he erected his "drums", while in the little back garden, he put his forge and "scheming" vice; and right at the end; a little fowl run, with two or three climbing nasturtiums brilliantly lighting up the side wall.*

*And thus he started, with enthusiasm and hope, in Brittania Street. Having A. Mullord engraved on a brass plate and fixed on the street door. This brass plate, 60 years old was "knocking about Penn Street Factory in 1905. I saw it. It may be there now.*

*"Gold Wire Drawing" must have been one of the primitive arts; It was co-eval with decorative weaving. When Trogloditic man was cutting and scraping with a sharp flint those wonderful drawings of the woolly mammoth on the walls of his cave, (Still to be seen in France. Dordogne Caves, I think) his wife was weaving some kind of basket or garment, with a mixture of Mammoth hair and various tough grasses which had been gathered and dried. Repetition is the essence of all ornament. Weaving essentially repeats a pattern. Two            different coloured threads elaborated the pattern, a gold thread introduced enhanced immensely the effect. Gold was probably the first metal used by primitive men in any part of the world. Surface gold in auriferous gravels was probably as common in Europe, Asia, and Africa, twenty thousand years ago, as it was in Central America a thousand years ago; or in Australia. and California fifty years ago.*

*Think of the solid gold statues of Rome two thousand years ago, and others in Athens three thousand years ago. Think of Solomon's temple with its golden treasures which are calculated to have been worth a half million pounds sterling. Think of the Legendary golden treasures of Abyssinia, Nubia, Ethiopia, Egypt, Carthage, Assyria, Persia and India.*

*Go farther back!. In a very dry season (about 1860) Lake Neuckatel shrunk, and exposed the foundation of some old Lake dwellings. (These Old "Goidel" Lake dwellings are exposed again this dry year of 1921.)*

*They had been erected there on piles, by a race long since passed away. A small people who were only about four feet high, but of an advanced order of intelligence. Buried in the mud, in a midden of broken bones and mussel shells, damaged arrow heads and flint clippings, they found the fossilised parts of a human body. Just a part of a skull. A part of an arm bone, etc. Not much but enough for learned professors to: pronounce it to be the remains of a female, probably twenty-five thousand years old. and that she was a person of importance, for the arm bone had an armlet of pure gold which was a flat ring two inches wide, waved at the edges, and having on it, a zig-zag ornamental I pattern marked with a flint.*

*In most of the Neolithic flint spear heads; and many arrow heads in our Museums, you will find: two holes at the base, which have been drilled with sharp pointed flints, for the purpose of tying them securely with ligatures to the staff or handle.*

*A primitive people who could drill a hole in a flint, would probably force through that hole, a paste made of their ground millet, and so make a sort of Maccarofli.*

*They would not stop at that, they would force clay through the hole, to make a decorative ornament for their crude pottery. And further, gold being easily melted, it would be quite natural to pour it through a hole, and even to sharpen the end and pull it again through a smaller hole. To do this with copper would supply a long felt want for binding their weapons more securely. To do it with gold until it became a weaving thread would be a delight to women who had the weaving instinct.*

*And this is an instinct that is found in all nature!. It is the "protective" and "covering up" instinct. Whether in insects with their cocoons, Birds with their nests, or "Pithicus Erectus" in Borneo, who! When up in his own particular tree interlaces the branches round about, to keep the privacy of his home, and also keep out the night cold.*

*Uncle Alick's wire drawing was a little more advanced and complicated than that of the Ancient "Goidels", in fact, that gold being more scarce, economy demanded that wire should be made of copper or silver, which should have a thin coating of gold on the outside. And which wire was then to all intents and purposes, as good as an "all gold" wire.*

*His whole process and method, from beginning to end, was as follows: -*

*Taking half a pound of Copper, he would forge and file it into a smooth thin "Finger" about six inches long. He would then take an ounce of pure gold, melt it and beat it, into a flat plate about 6 inches by two. He would now heat both the copper and the gold and then skilfully and carefully hammer the gold all round the copper, welding it with heat; and hammering it perfectly smooth and round. It was now in appearance a gold "finger".*

*One end of this "finger" was secured on a "scheming drum" and the other end secured in the jaws of a powerful vice.*

*Now!. By the united strength of Aunt Annie and Uncle Alick; and the leverage of a heavy "fly wheel", the "scheming drum" was made to turn. It did not turn much, but enough to stretch considerably the finger of gold, at the same time curving it round the "drum".*

*The now elongated finger would be taken off the drum, and beaten straight. The vice and the drum be put farther apart, and the "finger" fastened in again; to be again stretched and curved. This process would be repeated, with occasional heating, until what was once a finger of gold, had now become: A coil of wire 1/16" thick, neatly wound on an iron drum, and looking like a giant wheel of gold string.*

*I have before spoken of the Jewel Plates. They were steel plates with a range of holes in them, these holes being graduated in size. And each hole having jewels (usually "off coloured" diamonds) fixed in, leaving enough room for a wire to pass through a small interstice.*

*This jewel plate stood on a bench in front of the workman. The "hank" or "drum" of thick gold wire on his left, and on the right a huge flat "drum" about six inches thick and about three feet across. The edge of this had two flanges, just as a railway wheel has one flange.*

*The workman took one end of the wire on his left; threaded it through the largest hole in the Jewel Plate in front of him; and pulled it carefully till he could fasten it on to the outer edge of the big drum on the right, and then proceeded with the "wire drawing".*

*This big flat drum on the right was poised on a central pivot fixed in the bench underneath, and from the ceiling came a suspended broomstick, at the bottom end of which was a "point" which fitted into a hole in the surface of the big winding drum, about three inches from the centre.*

*The workman began by taking hold of the broomstick and very slowly turning the drum, with his right hand. With his left hand he held and guided the wire that was being drawn through the Jewelled hole. He had between his thumb and fingers some wool and beeswax, so that the wire should not be dimmed with perspiration from the hand.*

*And that, is roughly, a description of the whole process. For by repeatedly winding from one drum to another, and the wire passing through a smaller hole each time, it became longer and finer, until, at last; the six inch finger of metal, had become a thousand yards of very fine gold wire. And because the workman had so carefully welded the gold on to the copper that the two surfaces amalgamated; The malleability of both metals had enabled the gold to remain on the outer surface of the copper, without a break.*

*(Sometimes silver was used instead of copper.)*

*Now for a year or two, everything went well. And Aunt Annie helped in "scheming" and Uncle Alick also schemed in his head; and had great dreams, and bought a second hand "Cornish Boiler", and resolved to fix an engine and do what had never been done before, that is "Gold Wire Drawing" by power. And he experimented, and saved money, and bought No.1 Penn Street, through the Planet Building Society, and moved there because he wanted more room for the great scheme of wire drawing on a large scale, by power, at half the usual cost. And then! "The Bottom Dropped Out"! Gold Wire Drawing suddenly ceased to be!*

*This is what occurred. The old musket ball and smooth bore, had given way to the rifled barrel and long bullet, and this had doubled the range of a soldier's killing power. The Battle of Koniggratz had taken place; and the Prussians in netral green uniform, without a gleam of gold wire ornament, had shot and killed easily all the Austrian Officers who were dressed in white and were highly decorated with gold lace epaulettes, etc. which made fine targets of them even a mile away.*

*The new repeating "Schneider" rifle had made it an easy thing for the Prussians to kill twenty-five thousand Austrians almost before the Austrians could see them, as they wore no gold lace, nor brilliant colours,*

*Just think of the commotion in all the War Offices of Europe. Naturally!!! Orders soon were passed to abolish gold lace. All that beautiful gold lace which every soldier for thousands of years, had adorned himself with; was "to go". Except for a few ceremonial occasions. Corneys and Kennings could do all the, wire drawing that they wanted for Freemasonry and such like demands, "Indoors".*

*They did not want wire done by power, from Uncle Alick or anyone else. No thank you!*

*And so!. Uncle Alick found himself faced with ruin. So far as the business to which he had devoted his energies was concered.*

*His workshop, his apprentices, his machinery, with his imposing looking "Cornish Boiler" all standing idle.*

*Now what I have written seems rather dramatic! Like the swift changing scenes of a play that is only presenting the obviously striking features of the subject. But life is not so swift! It appears to pursue a placid, and even monotonous course.*

*Before this stoppage in wire drawing occurred, Uncle Alick and Aunt Annie had been married six or seven years. Babies had been born with almost annual regularity. I think one died, but there seemed to me to be a continuous procession of girls. Annie, Alice, Jane, Kate, Emma and Nellie, (some of these were born after the wire drawing "Debacle") and much later, the boys; Alick, Charles and John.*

*During those first few years, life did not seem monotonous to Uncle Alick and Aunt Annie. They worked hard, and saw the savings growing. He ruled his life by method; and knew they must have recreation: So there was a Saturday night visit to the Theatre; Sadlers Wells or "The Eagle". And the Sunday evening walk up "The Green Lanes" or to the "Pleasure Gardens" of Highbury Barn, Hornsey Wood Tavern; Highbury Park Tavern or "The Manor House".*

*The suburbs of London had many of these "Pleasure Gardens" in middle Victoria times. They were attended by hundreds of quite respectable folk. Such people as now go to Quenns Hall, or Albert Hall, or Hampton Court on Sundays, or that throng the Parks after Church. Finsbury Park and Alexandra Park had no existence then.*

*In these Pleasure Gardens there was always a very large Green lawn; And all round this lawn werw little Summer houses, and tables and chairs. And you had your tea; and afterwards, had your glass of Bitter Ale, and smoked your pipe, and nodded to your acquaintances as they promenaded round the lawn on the broad gravel path.*

*These gardens were not only crowded on Sunday, but were well attended on weekdays, when bowls and skittles were played; and children toddled on the grass, and Lovers "huddled" in pairs in the Summer Houses.*

*These things are no more!. Fashions change, and the glories of the world pass away.*

*Besides these simple recreations, there was the annual fortnight at Margate or Ramsgate. You took "The Eagle" Steam Boat from the White Swan pier at 10 o`clock, and arrived at Margate at 5 o`clock. The good old "Eagle" was always crowded, and most of the children were sea-sick before arrival. Parents rushed off the jetty, their pallid biscuit fed progeny trailing after them, and somehow and somewhere they at last found anchorage, in furnished apartments of limited space. Ah! but the next fortnight was priceless in its sunburnt enthusiasm. Yes! Life passed quickly enough for Uncle Alick and Aunt Annie, these first few years, And then Aunt Annie developed "Fits". It may have been hereditary, or; having many children; or malnutrition when she was a child. Or she may have "overdone it" in working. She was one who would work hard at anything; uncomplainingly But anyhow! These periodic fits came in a mysterious manner, lasted some years, and stopped in a mysterious manner.*

*I mention them chiefly because as a boy, I was enlightened and impressed by the great loving tenderness that Uncle Alick revealed on these occasions. He took her to the best Doctors, and they all said, "Patience, rest, and a change of manner of living, would cure her." He was most assiduous in devising all sorts of things, and eventually they moved to the top of Oakleigh Road, when suddenly these fits ceased.*

*I have alluded to Uncle's loving tenderness with his wife. He did indeed display a wonderful kindliness of heart to other people as well. When any relative was in trouble, a large hearted sympathy resulted in him taking practical methods of helping them; when others might be saying "what a bad job", but doing nothing.*

*This:   Although, he was a man who appeared to be somewhat stern and hard, which appearance came from his resolute character.*

*But I believe that Uncle Alick had from the beginning made sure that he would never do anything wrong knowingly; and would try to guide his life that he might not do wrong unknowingly; But on the other hand, also; he felt that life was a continual battle, and therefore in any dispute, one must be top dog, or underneath dog, so he thought it wise to be "top dog", even if you had to fight for that position.*

*It was partly his nature, but it was more his method; and he lived by studied method and mistrusted doing things by impulse.*

*One of his great assets was his voice. Uncle Alick had a fine baritone voice, and when he spoke, all listened.*

*My Father and he were summoned on a jury one day. The "twelve men good and true" were all seated waiting.*

*The Coroner bustled in. He said! "Good morning, Gentlemen! The first thing you have to do, is to elect a "foreman"." There was a pause, and then Uncle Alick said: "The best plan, Mr. Coroner!." "You`ll do!" said the Coroner, "Name please?" And he was installed "foreman" at once. Now that just illustrates the magnetic effect that his voice gave him, together with a keen clear grey eye which looked fearlessly; and with great determination, at the people he was talking to.*

*With so fine a voice, it seemed natural that he should be a good singer. And an intuitive sense of music was one of his natural gifts. He cultivated it as a "hobby" and, indeed, became a professional singer in a small way.    That is: he sang at evening concerts and minor entertainments for a small fee. This was always after work time.      But he told me: that when his fees had mounted to a hundred pounds (he saved it all in a separate account,) he concluded Professional work was a mistake. It took him away from home every evening and meant drinking, smoking and late hours. Which was bad for health, and meant lonely evenings for Aunt Annie. But his singing continued to give great pleasure all his life, to himself, his family and all his friends. And his Solos, Duets, and Glees, with my Father and Uncle Tom, were really a musical treat of a very high quality.*

*To resume our history. I have told elsewhere how Uncle Tom came into partnership with my Father, and how the business of A.& A. Mullord" was started, with the purpose of supplying Valentine Makers with new designs in Lace Papers. But the business did not stop at that.*

*There was a customer named Robert Canton, of Aldersgate Street, who was a Printer as well as a Valentine Maker. Robert Canton had designed and published a card at Christmas time, to be sent as a Christmas Greeting to your friends.*

*This card was "Relief Stamped" in colours just on top, with a small robin holding in his beak a sprig of holly. That was the beginning of the Christmas Card Trade. Canton being a Lithographic Printer, next year produced a card printed all over with a very fine robin. It was done in litho, in ten colours, and had a greeting on the bottom. It also had a narrow ¼" white margin. This margin Canton got Mullords to cut a die for, and emboss and Lace all round the border. Mullords have the die now. They charged Canton Forty shillings, I think, a thousand for "Lacing" the cards and £17 for cutting the die. The cards sold well, and then Canton produced many designs and had many dies cut, which all tended to make Mullords more busy.*

*Robert Canton was also making "Scent Sachets" and Mullords, therefore, cut lace envelope dies. The sachet trade began with a very small "all lace" envelope, but a man named Bollans of Leamington, wanted larger envelopes of a more tasteful design. Our designs pleased him, and his orders became very important. Then Stevens of Coventry, a silk ribbon weaver, put his silk woven mottoes on the face of the "Scent Sachets", and gave Mullords very big orders. I recollect one order for eight dies and a million sachet envelopes.*

*I say "I" and "We", because I, having entered the business as draughtsman, had gradually assumed the post of Manager.*

*All these things and tradeshad their day, and then passed away. The last time, I saw old Robert Canton (who was always a Gentleman, and kept his own carriage,) he was in a little room in his son-in-law's factory, playing with papers like a child. His memory gone! Bankrupt in everything.*

*But while these trades were still flourishing, a Wood Street Agent, named Luckmann, had brought to Penn Street, a real Leghorn "Chip" Hat, which was then all the Fashion, and which was sold in real fine straw plait for guineas each. Luckmann asked Mullords if they could cut dies and imitate this fine Leghorn plait, using thin straw colored cardboard. They did so. It led to many dies and many shapes. Luckmann's account one Quarter - was about £800. These cardboard hats had an enormous sale. Several imitators took up the idea, until common "butter coloured" "Trash" was sold by all the very cheap drapers. For every mother found that she could decorate her children's heads with an imitation Leghorn hat; already trimmed, for one and sevenpence three farthings. Consequently! Southgate Road, on a fine Sunday afternoon when children came out of Sunday School, looked like a bank of primroses.*

*But of course, that ended it!. Our chief customer was bankrupt and the yellow cardboard Leghorn Trade vanished.*

*About this time, began our very important Stove Ornament Trade.*

*Father had become the City representative of the Firm, and as such; got ideas of what was selling. Among other things, he noticed in shops, a paper packet about five inches in diameter and about ½" thick, on which was printed a crude picture of a Stove Ornament.*

*Hawkers had for years, cut out in coloured tissue paper, a "rough and ready" stove ornament, and had hawked them from door to door for money or a pair of old trousers. But this that Father saw and bought, was a much more finished article. It consisted of 300 "ringlets" made of waxed tissue paper. The paper had been waxed first on large double Crown Sheets. Then 300 of these sheets were laid one on another, and put under a "Shoe cutting press." A spiral steel tool was put on top, and cut through the 300 thicknesses in one blow. The article was now complete. The Customer bought it in the block of 5 inches diameter, and at home, carefully separated each single spiral, then hung it by one end on a wire. When 300 of these "Spirals" hung down from the wire, you had a very beautiful "Ringlet Stove Ornament. We copied it. The retail price had been 2/-. We sold one the same size, but all white to retail for 1/-. But before we could do that, Uncle Alick's wonderful genius for doing things properly and thoroughly came into action. He bought hot ovens, with shelves, and gas jets at the bottom. We sprinkled powdered wax on every quire of paper, and put a ream on each hot shelf. When the wax melted into the paper, we laid the ream on a table and ripped off each sheet, flinging it into the air to cool. It was a hot and very*

*dangerous process. And for fear of fire, it was carried on in a small shed away from the workshops.*

*Uncle Alick invented all this. He then invented a machine for waxing paper on the roll. It was like a paper making machine. It reduced the total cost by a third. But it meant buying tissue paper by the ton at 8d. per pound.*

*He and Father travelled to "Stoneclough" paper mill in Lancashire, and persuaded Fletchers to supply them direct with eight tons of thick tissue. (8½ lbs. to the ream 30 x 20"). They chanced selling it all. However! We sold it right enough; for my sister Emma, who was forewoman, one day, folded some wax paper, and got our James Hollis to fringe it with some "plaiting paper blades". When "opened", it was a strip 40 inches long with a plain band in the middle and a 3 inch fringe sticking out each side. We at once called it the Favourite. It was absolutely lovely. Father took the first one to "Faudels". Who bought £400 worth in the first two months. We could not make them fast enough. The price was half a crown retail, and every Stationers in the land bought them. Next year we had the "Silken Floss", and other designs, and this was the maximum year of the fortunes of H. & A. Mullord. The week before Whitsuntide our week's business was £560. It has never reached that since. Of course, it was not all satin stove ornaments. There were cut tissue lace papers, memorial cards, and various side lines. Memorial cards were beginning to boom. I had introduced an Oxford border memorial card single embossed, and a double card Oxford border with "In Memoriam" on front page. This was the first double memorial card ever sold. They had all been single previously, and customers had complained to me of the lack of room for printing. Besides these, there were several other Branches or Trades.*

*And the three houses were now like a rabbit warren, with apparently the business always growing. So Uncle Alick and Father and myself kept talking about building a factory. The firm had previously bought five houses on the Hyde Road and at the back. Therefore! when it was settled, we turned the tenants out of these houses and moved all the stock and hands round to Hyde Road in a week. The three Penn Street houses were then pulled down in two days, and the new Factory was built in about two months. All the business was moved back as fast as benches etc. could be installed.*

*Then Uncle Alick said to Father: "I propose that the business of H. & A. Mullord should pay a rental for the Factory". Father agreed, Uncle Alick then suggested that the rent should be £300. per annum. And as the five houses in Hyde Road, were supposed to let at about the same total rental of £300, he further suggested that one of the Partners should have the deeds and possession of the Factory and the other partner the possession of the five houses in Hyde Road. Father agreed, and I suggested the choice should be settled by tossing up a coin. My Father won the toss: and chose the Factory. A year after came catastrophe.*

*In all languages, and especially in Biblical Language, you will find it stated as a certainty that: Nothing lasts!*

*This business of H. & A. Mullord had been flourishing for about twenty years, and the Factory had been built on the assumption that everything was always "going forward".           But it wasn`t a correct assumption. One year we were facing prosperity, a year after we were facing disaster.*

*Disaster always comes at the wrong time, and troubles never come alone.*

*The big business had developed into several businesses, and suddenly these all slowed down to almost nothing.*

*This requires the following explanation.*

*The growing Christmas Card Trade killed the Valentine Trade. It had been dying slowly, because competition had swamped the market with cheap trash. A development that kills most artistic trades.*

*The wax paper Stove Ornament trade was killed by the Newspapers. A sub-editor wrote that "these tawdry decorations are a public danger, any man lighting his pipe, and throwing the match into the grate, might, could, and sometimes did, cause the Stove Ornament, the house, and the whole family to perish by a rapid conflagration."*

*The wholesale trade dropped the Wax Ornament, and took up the cardboard screen.*

*Also! The McKinley tariff robbed us of our trade to the United 5tates of many hundreds of pounds worth, annually, of lace papers, by a prohibitive duty.*

*Then!. Gilt frame mirrors went out of fashion, and so we lost the Cut Tissue trade of about a thousand pounds per annum.*

*All! Everything seemed to go at once, but really leaving a large connection of customers with odds and ends of various trades, though not enough to pay for the rather extensive staff of Die Sinkers, Engravers, Clerks etc.*

*When stock taking was finished about a year after we were in the new Factory, it was found we had lost eight hundred pounds on the year's business, instead of showing a profit (as it always had done.) The shock was terrible. A council of War was held, Uncle Alick suggested that the only thing to do was to dismiss all the staff, die sinkers, clerks etc. cut down every expense, run only such business as could be worked piecework, and at the end of the year; sell the business "lock, stock and barrel" for what- it would fetch.*

*This policy was adopted, and it was a very sad thing; that sending away of people who had been friendly with one for years, and who would have to find different work in a different place.*

*However, this drastic combing out of everything just saved the firm from coming to an end.*

*At the stock taking a year after, it was found there was a profit of £800, but it was concluded that a page of the "stock taking" had been lost and omitted in the previous year, and this was the page with some tons of tissue paper on the reel, which had made the situation look worse than it was.*

*Anyhow!. We had made a profit and had turned the corner. Uncle Alick had invented a weekly account, which showed week by week how we were getting on. This enabled us to keep expenditure down to its proper proportion, by sledge hammer methods if necessary. There was no more talk of selling the business. Economy, hard work, and inventive imagination brought the Firm into prosperity again.*

*It was about this time of recovery that Messrs: Catty & Dobson, for whom we were embossing Dish papers in their paper on our dies, persuaded Joseph Mansell to join them by creating a limited Company. They came to us, also, and offered to buy all our dish paper dies and trade; for cash, to the amount of a year of our business in those goods only (about £1000 at that time.) I think Father and Uncle were inclined to take the offer, but I so strongly opposed it, that they came to my opinion.*

*The subsequent increase of our table stationery trade justified the retention.*

*To resume our Chronicles; Uncle Alick's inventive genius continued to help the business, but he to a large extent gradually ceased to take an active part in the management, though he always was in his office at Penn Street, as head of the Firm, and regulated the Ledgers and financial work.*

*But he had a good private income, and had bought and moved into a nice house at Finabury Park where he lived a happy life with Aunt Annie and his growing family. He had for many years taken an active part in Freemasonry and was very popular and greatly respected by many brothers of the craft.*

*I think it was when he was about sixty-five that his interest in religious matters quickened. He had always been more or less inclined to religion, but the evidence had not been very apparent. His intense interest and enjoyment of the every day battle of life, had seemed to keep dormant his spiritual awakening.*

*But then it came suddenly to him; that to devote himself to spiritual work was imperative, and from that time, till his death at the age of 89, his mind was fixed on serving God to the utmost of his ability. Aunt Annie died at the age of 70.*

*The poor dear lady was very uncomplaining; but I think she must have been seriously ill for a year or two. Her death seemed to me to sever the last link of real interest Uncle Alick had in his own earthly life, though he was still always ready to help others, and had a great affection for the young ones. He had always been a great smoker, but he now gave up the use of tobacco, and there were other abstinences. One could see he had taken to heart the words:*

*"Give up all and follow me." And with all the concentrated earnestness of his nature he did so to the whole of his power.*

*I think the loneliness of old age, in his case, was mitigated by his social instincts. True! He lived alone with his daughter Nellie; who devoted herself to his care, and was a tender and loving woman. But his sons and daughters with their spouses were often with him. They were all married, except Alice and Nellie. It was a large family. After the first regiment of daughters, there had come three sons, Alick, Charles and John, and Uncle was very proud of them all. As they are all living I shall say nothing about them, except that they are a credit to the Mullord Family and will carry on its traditions worthily.*

*Uncle Alick spent much of his money, and most of his time in these last years among the people of his chapel, and indeed he had become a most important man in his particular group. He had lent money to build a church, and had Personally directed the work of this particular religious organisation.*

*He died of old age at 89, and was buried at Abney Park Cemetryry. He was attended to the grave by a large body of relatives, workpeople and delegates from his Church.*

*So lived and died, The greatest member of the Mullord Family. His "greatness" chiefly lay in three qualities - his powerful strong determined personality. His unswerving probity, and thirdly, His mental alertness. The faculty of penetrating all coverings and getting to and understanding, the Heart and Reason of things, and the Heart and Motives of men.*

*It is fit that I terminate here my chronicles of the Mullord Family. Uncle Alick was the last of the very fine progeny of that Big, highly intelligent man, who came from Scotland a hundred years ago, who was named George A1exander Mullord. And who married Anne Scantlebury. Having given a history of all the children of*

*this worthy couple, I must leave it to some one of their great grandchildren, or great, great grandchildren to carry on the Chronicles.*

*I might have written more of the many younger members of the Mullord Family who are now living and flourishing, but some one of them must "carry on the torch". I have given such Chronicles of their predecessors, that would otherwise have fallen into oblivion.*

*MY WISH*

*I should like a copy of these Chronicles to be in the hands of any Male Mullord who has a son, and that he should hand the Chronicles to that son as a family relic, which may not always be considered important, but might come into the possession of some member of the family who would attach some value to it.*

*HENRY MULLORD.*
