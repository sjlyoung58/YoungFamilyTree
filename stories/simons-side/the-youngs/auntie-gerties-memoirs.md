M E M O R I E S

The shelves of libraries are full of Autobiographies, some very interesting, some very dull, but they are mostly written by people who have something to write about - either in the world of Music, Art, Politics, Adventure, etc.  My memories will perhaps only be interesting to me, but our family increases so rapidly that perhaps one day a little girl or boy may be interested to read about life in the twentieth century before the First World War changed everything and everybody. As for the title, I could call it the Diary of a Nobody, A Person of no Importance or perhaps the story of a Lady of Leisure, but these have all been used before, so I just call my efforts "Memories".

As I am a real Old Maid, these pages will never be read by my grandchildren, but nevertheless at the time of writing I am rich in having 10 nieces and nephews and 14 great nieces and nephews. In my possession is a wonderful letter written by one of our ancestors to his son. This is of real historic interest, but although my memories are negligible in comparison (my life having been very quiet and unadventurous), it has amused me to look back over the years and. write these few pages in the hope (as I said before) that someone in years to come may read them with interest.

Now that I am in my 72nd year, I often think back to the days when I was a little girl - the eldest of five brothers. We were all born in a house in the town of Gravesend with no modern conveniences and the housewife of to-day can have no idea of the work involved. Our mother was a gentle soul, I never heard her complain although she had to work terribly hard and was at times I know, very worried over our financial position. She must have been very strong, for besides the housework, washing, mending, ironing, cooking, etc. for the family, she at times answered the door and attended people calling at our father's office after hours if he was out. During 15 years she brought up six children -another - to her great sorrow - having died in infancy. For many years she made her own and our clothes, and they were beautifully made and finished. She had been apprenticed to the dressmaking, her wages after 4 years when she was a skilled worker, being 9/- per week. She was one of nine children, a very happy and handsome family, and her father was one of the jolliest men I have ever known. Her mother was beautiful in every respect. Grandpa told me once that however small his pay packet, she never grumbled. Grandpa was a moulder and from what I have heard, a very skilled man. He came, I believe, from the Brierley Hill district to the Isle of Dogs to work there in an iron works. How many children came up with him I don't know, but our mother - the sixth child - was born in Deptford. When mother was about five years old the family moved to Northfleet, where Grandpa had taken a new job, and from what mother told us it was like being transported into Paradise. The road they lived in led into the open country, and apart from one or two cottages and a farm, there were no other houses. They lived in a picturesque house called 'Gothic Cottage' – two cottages which had been converted into one, and as Grandpa's hobby was gardening it was soon such a picture that passers by stopped to admire it.

Of our father's beginnings we know little, except that he too was one of nine children and that he was born in Newton Abbott, Devon. They were a very delicate and unfortunate family. Their father was a strange and impecunious man, who had money at times from rich relations, but soon spent it. He was a "literary" man and had some connection with Mrs. Beeton and her publishers, and for this reason he came from Devon to Greenhithe, where I understand Mrs. Beeton was living. Five of his children died from tuberculosis at an early age, one lived to 35, and of the three remaining sons - one died in Australia in middle age, while the twins, my father and Uncle Julian - lived to the ages of 90 and 82 respectively. All father's brothers and sisters who attained the age of 21 inherited £100 from some source unknown to me. My father, who was then working in a builder's office, then started an Estate Agency - the foundation of the present business. He worked all hours in an effort to build up the business and, to use his own words, would take on anything to earn a shilling. He had a first rate knowledge of building, which was of course very useful to him in business. He was very fond of music and had a good baritone voice, and for many years he, and his two brothers, were choristers in St. Botolphs Church, Northfleet. Among the congregation was a very pretty girl, in charge of a younger sister, and I feel sure when father looked at her he knew she was the one girl for him. She was then only 17, and he was 14 years older, but somehow they met and became engaged. From what I have heard the course of true love didn't altogether run smoothly, owing I imagine to my father's health and family worries, but anyway after four years they were married. Father had, at one time, lived with his twin brother, but the wife was a very unpleasant woman and for some reason told father to go. He then lived in a queer little bungalow, looking after himself. Mother has told me that he sometimes went to supper at her house and it worried her because funds were low and the joint was small. But Grandpa had a wonderful garden where he produced all kinds of vegetables, fruit, etc. including eggs and at one time he even kept pigs. To a lonely and rather pessimistic man this happy home must have seemed like heaven, and I believe he was quite a favourite with them all. Several of mother's brothers were musical and I know they had many happy evenings singing and playing their various instruments. Grandpa knew Gilbert & Sullivan almost by heart, and he also belonged to the local Christy Minstrels where he had great fun.

Our parents were married on a lovely April day in 1896 and after a short honeymoon at Ascot they came back to live in the house where father conducted his business. He used the front room on the ground floor for his office, and a wooden gate separated it from our private apartments. As I have said earlier, it was a very inconvenient house, but it did have a small secluded garden where we used to play and have our pets. We had rabbits, guinea pigs, white mice, pigeons, etc. at one time or another, and one brother even went in for chickens and ducks. Also, our front room upstairs was a wonderful place to see the world go by, and in those days there were carnivals, processions of various kinds, not to mention visits by Royalty and perhaps the most exciting for us, Lord Sanger's Circus. Our school friends were sometimes invited to see these sights, and as there was only one window we must have been packed very closely.

We were not always able to have a summer holiday, but if Dad had a bit of luck we went to Margate or Herne Bay for a week or two. Christmas was always a great occasion, and I don't remember a single year when we didn't have a tree. Dad and mother would go to London (it was only 1/6d. return then) and come home with lots of pretty little toys bought from the pavement vendors - most of them not more than 1d. each. You could buy a little doll with long flaxen hair for a penny, and mother would dress them very prettily. Dad used to do all sorts of tricks at Christmas to amuse us and I know we were all very sad when it was over. On Boxing Day Grandma and Grandpa would come for the day, and I remember Grandma always had the job of slicing the beetroot. After dinner we all played a favourite card game called Speculation, and Grandpa was a very exciting person to play with, sending us all into fits of laughter. For the winners Grandma would produce little prizes, and I'm sure modern children with all their expensive toys don't enjoy themselves half as much as we did.

We lived near a sweet shop kept by a funny old man who always wore a smoking cap, and at Christmas time his shop was besieged by admiring youngsters admiring the tempting things in his window. Pink and white sugar mice were 2 and 4 for a penny, and there were fancy sugar shapes of all kinds as cheap as 8 for a penny. They were very pure, all sugar, and would be in the shape of drums, crowns, little animals, etc. We always had a few pennyworth to deck our tree. This old man also sold ice cream, which was little known to the general public in those days, and for a halfpenny we could get a cornet biscuit with a large blob of ice cream on she top. The only other ice cream shop was in High Street, kept by one Valentini, and their ice cream (which we very rarely had) was served in a glass and was really super. Also a pretty little cart sometimes came round selling Hokey Pokey. Some say this was ice cream, but it was not. I should describe it as a sort of nougat and it was sold by the "lump". Comic papers could be bought for a halfpenny, and we revelled in the adventures of Dreamy Daniel, Butterball, Tail Thomas, etc. If funds allowed we sometimes went to visiting fairs and circuses and I can recall the first "animated pictures". They were very shaky and flickery, and quite alarming as the motor cars (just coming in) or trains, or other vehicles, seemed to be about to leap off the screen and come straight for us. Another treat was Poole's Mirriorama which were coloured lantern slides with dialogue and musical effects. I remember lovely snow scenes and views of other lands, which were a great thrill.

Money was of course worth far more in those days, and £3 a week would be considered good wages. A little house could be rented for 5/- a week, and quite a nice place for 10/-. Bread was rarely more than 3d. a loaf, and one baker I remember made a lovely Coburg shaped loaf for 2**¾**d. Our Sunday joint cost on an average 2/6d. and would be a nice piece of topside or shoulder of lamb. Delicious sausages cost 8d. per lb. and a favourite dinner was sausages in batter, it was really rich and luscious and a delicious flavour. There was little or no margarine in my young days. Butter was about 1/- a lb. and best dairy butter 1/4d. at most. Tea was 1/- a lb., rice and sugar 2d. A nice cooked beetroot could be had for ld. and a large smoked haddock for 1/-. Shrimps were caught locally and sold for 2d. or 3d. a pint. There were several shops in the town with open fronts and great piles of shrimps on a marble slab. They were measured in wooden beakers. The shopman would bale out a measureful and slide his hand over the top to level it so that we didn’t get too many.

In the summer we sometimes had a picnic tea on Windmill Hill, which was a pleasant spot commanding a nice view of the river and ships. Mother would go in advance with us carrying the eatables and Dad came later on his bicycle with a bottle of tea wrapped in a sock to keep it hot. It always went down well and he must have ridden fast to keep it so hot. I remember other children hanging round, much to mother's annoyance, but I suppose they were a bit envious.

In 1907 we had a patch of bad luck, beginning in February with the death of our baby sister, aged 13 months. This was a great blow to our parents. After that we had continuous illness in the house and in September our youngest brother, aged 4, caught scarlet fever. It was arranged that he and mother would be isolated on the top floor, but after a few days mother caught the fever and they were both taken off to the Isolation Hospital. Poor father was left to look after his business and three young children, but he managed to get a woman to come and do a bit in the house. He bought us a season ticket to Rosherville Gardens, and we went there every day. It was a wonderful place, and hundreds of people came down from London by boat to spend the day there. From my recollection it was made from a disused chalk pit and there were entrances at the top and the bottom. The top entrance, marked by a small tower, was in the London Road. One walked through glass houses with exotic birds, etc. then down a winding rustic path to the bottom. The lower entrance in Burch Road led to a broad promenade lit in the evening by hundreds of coloured fairy lights. I suppose someone had to go round to light them and replenish the candles. On this broad walk was a bear pit, bandstand, and many arbours and seats. Further in was an open air stage and ballroom, and a cafe where one could eat inside and out. Tables outside provided tea with shrimps or watercress and bread and butter for 6d. The dance hall was known as the Baronial Hall, and King Edward VII is said to have danced there when he was visiting the Royal Yacht Club near by. There was also a piece of rough ground where on special occasions would be roundabouts, swings and all the fun of the fair. The Gardens were run by a Limited Company, but some of the Managers were dishonest or incompetent and eventually the Company was wound up and the land sold to factories.

Little girl's clothes were very different when I was small. We always wore a wool vest, then a cotton chemise or shift, a pair of stays (the earliest form of Liberty bodice) in a sort of grey twill with buttons and loops to keep up stockings and knickers. The knickers were of white calico trimmed with lace. We used to make these at school and the calico was hard to little fingers. We also had to stroke the gathers with a pin - why I don't know. Then there was usually a flannel or knitted wool short petticoat and over that a fancy cotton one. Dresses were often made on a yoke with gathered skirt and some had little dangling pockets for our hankies. We also wore a pretty pinafore to keep our frocks clean. Right up to my teens I wore black ribbed wool stockings, which we bought at a shop in High St. for 1/- a pair. They used to wear into holes and after frequent washings were inclined to go a bit green. We wore black or brown shoes, usually buttoned but occasionally laced. For best or party wear little girls had pretty bronze kid shoes with buckles. Winter coats were warmly lined, often with velvet collar and cuffs and once I had a little moleskin cap which was the pride of my life. Our school hats were straw sailors with brims of varying width, and for some reason or other most of us favoured a wide brim.

I remember the first electric tram coming along the road with a crowd of cheering people running beside it. The trams were a cheap form of transport and the trip from King Street to the top of Windmill Street only cost a halfpenny. They were very noisy and spoilt the roads with their rails, but they were very useful. The passengers sat opposite one another in two long rows, that is on the lower deck, and a friend of mine said she always wanted to laugh when she looked across at the long row of serious faces. In those days many people walked to work, and were no doubt better for it. Grandpa used to start very early in the morning and take his breakfast with him. It was a long day, and before mother went to work she would, in school holidays, run all the way to his works with a hot dinner tied up in a red handkerchief. Grandma thought nothing of walking to Gravesend and back to do her shopping (nearly 2 miles each way). As I have said before, she was a very beautiful woman and to the end of her life was upright as a dart.

At Christmas time there were lots of parties, and we always had one. Some of the well to do would hire a hall, and this was a great occasion if we were invited. At these times a fire was lit in my bedroom and there we dressed in our party finery. Once when Cyril and I were going to a party at our Aunts in Northfleet, he climbed on to the sofa, just as we were ready to go, fell off and broke his collarbone. He was about 4 or 5 at the time. Our second brother, Percy, was a fat boy and had the nickname of Chub or Chubbles, after a fat boy in the comic papers. We all had a magazine delivered from a local shop - I had the Girls Own Paper, Cyril the Boys Own Paper, and Percy "Tiny Tots". They came out once a month and were much enjoyed and looked forward to. Of course there was no radio or television, but we amused ourselves playing in the garden or on the "Prom" in the summer, and in winter reading or playing games. On Sunday evenings mother would play the piano and Dad would kneel at her side playing the air on his little concertina. We had a little book of music published by Beechams, the pill firm, containing many pretty tunes which they played, and also children's hymns. A great favourite with Dad was "Now the Day is over". The first song I remember mother singing to us was "Ring the Bell Watchman" and Dad, too, used to sing some of the old ballads. We used to like to play with "transfers" and had little pieces of perforated card on which we worked designs in coloured silks. There were also little cardboard models which Dad would sometimes bring back from a shop in Grays when he went there. They only cost ld. or 2d. and were I imagine made in Germany. They were brightly coloured and had to be cut out and fitted together by means of slots in the card. The one I particularly remember was a gypsy van, with brushes and cans to hang on the outside.

On Guy Fawkes Day we usually had a bonfire and a few fireworks, the favourite being golden rain, which was more successful than rockets or Catherine wheels. Mother was always worried, lest her few chrysanthemums should be scorched by the fire, as our garden was very narrow. Dad was a great gardener, and at one time had three allotments. How he did it all I can't imagine, but I know he used to get up early and go off on his bike before breakfast, and of course at week ends. He used to work till very late at night and go down to the Post Office at midnight just in time to catch the mail. I have often heard it rumble by as I lay in bed, but to my great regret I never get out to see it go by.

Gravesend was quite a pleasant residential town in my young days. The big factories on the river front were only just starting, the population was smaller and life more leisurely. There were several very good private schools, and many good class families lived in the bigger houses in the town. I'm afraid there was also a lot of poverty, and I can remember people begging and singing in the streets, processions of unemployed, and always a crowd of poor ragged people waiting in the market place for tickets to the casual ward for a night's shelter. There were very few multiple shops, and I think the first of them must have been Liptons and the Maypole. I don't know how the butter was kept cool, as there were no refrigerators, but it always seemed to be firm, and the Maypole always parcelled their goods up in shiny brown paper tied with string, which the assistant broke very smartly with his hand. When David Grieg came there was great excitement - especially at Christmas time with poultry, etc. hanging outside, gas jets flaring, and the Manager shouting at the top of his voice. He used to stand outside with a long hooked pole, with which he took down the birds and joints as the buyers chose them. Mother often went out late on Saturday to do her shopping, and I used to beg her to take me for a treat. We had several good drapery shops where one could buy anything in that line without walking from one department to another. The assistants were polite and anxious to serve - sad to say they are quite the opposite nowadays - and there were always chairs for the ladies to rest while making their purchases. Our chief drapers was so grand that I hated to go there, being a very timid child. The lady shopwalkers looked like Duchesses in black gowns with long trains. They kept the assistants in order and saw that customers were not kept waiting. Nowadays one hardly gets a please or thank you from shop assistants, though one usually finds good manners in the case of older assistants. The class of girls in shops nowadays would not have been tolerated in years gone by, as many of them are only fit to be in factories. Some of our shops were quite unusual, and I shall never forget Missings Bazaar, which sold practically everything in the china, drapery and fancy goods line, and consisted of two or three big shops in the main road. The proprietress was a little hunch-backed woman, who could sometimes be seen sitting in the shop with an assistant who was shouting into an ear trumpet, for she was very deaf. Then there was Deans, the Chelsea Bun shop, a tiny shop which couldn't have been very hygienic, but the buns were so delicious that nobody thought about the shop. They sold at 7 for 3d. and the folds of the bun were filled with a lovely creamy mixture. The Chelsea buns today are not worthy of the name, as they are usually dry and tasteless with no creamy filling. Yet another little shop made raspberry tarts about the size of a tea plate for 2d., the puff pastry melted in the mouth. But the most popular shop of all was "Uttings" the Yorkshire Cake Shop.

I remember a nice old lady with a white Pom coming to father's office looking for a shop and living accommodation. We always thought there had been some domestic trouble, as they came all the way from Yorkshire. Anyway, father found them a shop in Parrock Street where they stayed for many years until the business warranted a larger place. There were two or three sisters (not all in the business) and they made the most delicious cakes, tarts, bread, etc. The small cakes were **½**d each, iced in different flavours or covered with coconut. Blackberry jam tarts were 3**½**d teacakes ld. Each and a special delicacy was their vanilla slice. I never remember tasting anything so delicious - the modern pastries can't compete with them. The layers of flaky pastry were sandwiched with jam and a beautiful creamy custard, and icing on top of all, and they sold for 1**½** or 2d. each. My school friend's father was a baker. He didn't go in for "smalls" but every Saturday he baked lemon scones, a large round of 4 for 3**½**d. They didn't come out of the oven until about 10 p.m. as he could only make them when the bread was out of the oven, but there were always many customers waiting. Our Grandma used to come into the town for her shopping, call for scones and get the bus or tram home. They were well worth waiting for, and according to my friend the profit was small as her father was very free with fats and sugar. They also made good Christmas cakes, which were sold by weight, hot cross buns on Good Friday and miniature lemon loaves which cost ld. each. Next door to us was a corner drapery shop, very old fashioned even for those days, and on busy nights a young lad stood on the corner of the pavement guarding the rolls of lino, etc. displayed outside. He was said to be subject to fits, and I'm afraid we used to watch from our window in the hope that we might see him in this sad state, but we never did. One of the local bakers used to make a giant cake at Christmas time in the shape of a famous building such as St. Pauls or Windsor Castle, and once in the shape of a battleship. It filled the shop window and caused a great deal of excitement. Just before Christmas it was cut up and sold. for about 1/6d. per lb. Another shop raffled a huge stocking filled with chocolates and sweets.

One or two incidents in my early life remain in my memory, one being the poor bear which was led about on a rope and made to perform at street corners. He was a poor shabby old animal and really a very sad figure. Also I vividly remember a fight near our house between two men, who were afterwards led off by the police half naked, as their clothes had been nearly ripped off in the fight. On Bank Holidays hundreds of Londoners would come down for the day, some just for the river trip, others to enjoy the riverfront. I have a clear recollection of Harrys and Harriets in big feathered hats dancing round the Clock Tower singing the popular songs of the day. There used to be a street vendor of buttonholes at holiday times. They were pinned on to a large black velvet-covered boards and cost ld. each. They were mostly carnations with a bit of fern, and I used to wish I could have one. There was also a brown wrinkled old woman with a shawl over her head carrying a cage containing two "lovebirds". For ld. they would pick out a card which was supposed to tell your fortune. As I remember them, the cards were very old and dirty.

Another excitement was "The Siege of Sidney Street" and the escape of the criminal known as "Peter the Painter". We never knew where he might be - in fact I don't think he ever was found - and any strange looking man was eyed with fear and suspicion. Also one night as I went with father to my music lesson we heard the newspaper boys shouting the loss of the Titanic. This was the most terrible disaster we had ever heard of, and the papers were full of it for weeks. There was of course no radio then, but the first time we took notice of radio was when Dr. Crippen was arrested on the high seas for the murder of his wife. As soon as one horrible deed had been worn out there seemed to be another to fill the papers and keep up the sales.

We always made a point of going to Church the Sunday before Christmas to witness the distribution of David Varchell's Charity. I believe Varchell died in the 17th century and left money to buy so many loaves for old people and so many suits of clothes for poor boys. The old and young folks receiving the gifts occupied the front pews. Varchell also gave the church a large and handsome candelabra or chandelier, and the candles were lit on the occasion of the distribution.  While the recipients were being given their gifts the choir sang carols. The one I liked best was "When Christ was born of Mary free" and I thought the harmonising was beautiful. When father was a boy he was forced to attend Church three times on Sundays and he never pressed us to go.  However, we did go and I attended our Parish Church for many years, my eldest brother being in the choir for a long time. We had the most wonderful Rector, Canon Gedge, who was blind. His sermons were so good that there was no fidgeting or coughing and the Church was always packed. We also had a very fine organist and choir. The Rector took Holy Communion on his own - the communicant at the end of the row would just touch him and he knew he had to go back and start again. He used to take out his Braille watch towards the end of his sermon and I never knew any hitch in the service. He was so well loved that when he retired I'm afraid the attendance gradually fell off as we couldn't bear to see anyone else in his place. Moreover, none of his successors could come up to him, they seemed so poor and third rate compared to him. He was a bearded man with a saintly face and his blind eyes were closed. He walked about very quickly, very often with a small boy holding his arm and trying to keep pace with him, and sometimes his elder daughter.

A favourite walk for Gravesenders on Sundays or Bank Holidays was to Springhead Watercress Gardens. It was a pleasant walk, and one could get a cup of tea and light refreshments at the Gardens. There were also cage birds, and sometimes monkeys to see. The cress gatherers waded knee deep in the stream, wearing high boots, and for ld or 2d. a large bunch of fresh cress could be bought. On Bank Holidays, if weather permitted, we always went off for a picnic - to Southfleet or Shorne Mill as a rule. There was usually a baby in the push chair and our luggage was stowed away around the baby. There was no thought of taking a bus, we always walked. I've seen Dad with a child on his back and another in his arms when their legs gave out. We invariably took ham sandwiches and apple turnovers for our lunch, and Dad was very fussy over the ham. It had to be cut into small pieces, he hated to get the ham out in one piece and be left with the bread. Our grandparents often walked out from Northfleet to join us and sometimes our Uncle from Rochester would arrive with his wife and several boys. Also two girl cousins with their parents often came from Northfleet and we all walked home happily together. I suppose Mother got a cup of tea from the public house near by - she must have needed one.

We had an occasional trip to London to such places as the Zoo, the British and other museums, Hampton Court, the Tower, etc. and of course to the great Exhibitions at Earls Court and the White City. The latter must have been produced in conjunction with France, as I believe the correct name was the Franco-British Exhibition. It was called the White City as all the buildings were white and the general effect was most beautiful, especially when lighted up at night. There were cascades, lakes and lovely gardens all lit up with coloured lights like a rainbow, and I'm sure if anything like it were produced now, the public would flock to it in thousands.

We all started our education at private schools and later at the Technical School, which was a mixed school and at that time a very excellent school. I well remember my very first day at school and being surprised to see my cousin Sidney there in a black velvet suit. There was another boy there who always intrigued me - he was very lively and jolly though not very good at lessons, and he always wore a red fez (not in school of course). I met him again many years after - he had grown very big and was I fear rather addicted to drink[[1]](#_ftn1) - but he had the same jolly manner. I am sure the grounding at this school was very sound for when I left at 10 to go to the Technical School I was very advanced in many subjects as compared with the other children there. I can still remember some of the songs - after more than 60 years - and often sing them to myself. The tuition at the Tech was good too, our masters and mistresses were gentlefolk, and all fully qualified. The only bad one was the Headmaster, a nasty sneaky man who earned himself the nickname of Treaclefeet. I often wonder how the others bore with him, as he was a really nasty type with nasty manners.

In those days women always wore hats, and very pretty some of them were, Mother often went to a milliner and had hers made. I remember the milliner as a very sweet ladylike little woman who always looked as though she could do with a good square meal. Her charges were probably hardly enough to keep her. Her little front room was full of hatboxes containing her shapes, etc. The shapes were usually made of white buckram and she would cut and adapt these to meet her customers requirements and build up the hat on it - perhaps with velvet, chiffon or flowers. Ribbon and ribbon velvet were very much used and bundles of straw in various colours could be obtained to make up the hats. I was very proud when I too had a hat made by this good lady. It was a pretty deep blue velvet with loose crown and stiff brim - the kind known as a motoring hat. When motor cars first came in they were, of course, open and ladies wore this type of hat tied on with a scarf, with a long coat known as a dust coat. I remember my first ride in a car - an open Citroen - from Gravesend to Strood, quite an experience, tho' I don't expect we did more than 20 miles per hour.

Dress materials were of much better quality in my young days and of course there was no man-made stuff. It would be a treat to be able to buy once more the lovely pure silks, crepe de chine and satins. Our summer dresses were made of cotton and gingham, and for best white jap silk was cheap and popular and washed well. For winter we had serge for school wear and for best, nuns veiling, delaine or cashmere, all fine woollen materials. When my baby sister died mother made me a dove grey cashmere with a tucked front and bands of lace insertion, and my confirmation dress was of cream cashmere similarly trimmed. Our winter coats were of tweed or face cloth, warmly lined. They sometimes had a highwayman effect with shoulder cape or perhaps two capes, and always a generous wrap in front - today they hardly meet. In my early teens I wore blouses with a high neck band kept in position by small bones let into the material and one of my friends had a dress with a medici type collar standing up high at the back. Mother's best blouse would be of french silk, a narrow material only about 18" wide, sometimes striped or checked and made in a fancy style with tucks or ruchings. These blouses were quite complicated to make and were always mounted on a "lining". My first mackintosh was made of a harsh dark grey material called cravenette, and afterwards came rainproof coats, but I don't remember when the rubberised macks came in, though sailors and workmen had the sou'wester and oilskin coats. I suppose we carried umbrellas when it rained and they were always black, but parasols for the summer were very dainty. Mother had a pretty pale grey silk one with a china knob, which I have still.

There was none of the modern table ware for ordinary folks. Knives were steel and had to be rubbed on a board sprinkled with brick dust to clean them. Kettles and pans got very black underneath through sitting on open stoves and fires and - before the advent of enamel ware - were of tin or iron. I don't remember having a casserole - stews were made in a large saucepan. The kitchen range had a high fender in front which was useful for aring and drying; washing was done by hand and boiled in a copper which took up half the scullery. Our scullery had an uneven brick floor, no ceiling, just the rafters overhead. When we were very small Dad fixed up a swing in the doorway, which we greatly enjoyed. We rarely had heat in our bedrooms unless we were ill, never hot water bottles or eiderdowns. The beds were covered by a honeycomb or marcella counterpane. Later we did have an oil stove, which was removed when we were in bed.

At the first sign of spring out would come tops, skipping ropes and hoops. There was practically no motor traffic and children used to play on the pavements, though of course we were never allowed to play outside. The boys were very dexterous with their pretty little wooden tops and kept them spinning with a flick of their whips for quite a long time. Girl's hoops were made of wood - boy's of iron. The girls rolled theirs along by hitting them with a short wooden stick known as a hoopstick; the boys used a "skid", which was a piece of iron hooked at the end. Hoops were made in all sizes and the bigger the child, the bigger the hoop. These pastimes seem to have gone for good, although girls will always skip in a quiet corner.

I still remember some of the songs I learned at school, one in particular, and as I walk down the lane in the morning I often think of the "Farmer's Song" –

 Away in the morning light I go

The good brown earth to till and hoe

And it matters not to me if the birds should silent be

For the music of the plough is the song for me,

Turn, turn, good brown earth

You're dear to every farmer for he knows your worth.

Out there in the fields it seems to me

Are sights enough for folk to see

Each furrow straight and true and the corn that's spring through

Are a picture that's as fine as I want to view

Turn, turn, good -brown earth, etc.

Some folk have a taste for posies fine

For roses red and columbine

But although they may be right, yet to me a finer sight

Is a harvest field all gleaming with its gold and white

Turn, turn, good brown earth, etc.

We never see the grand teams of horses now, their place is taken by tractors which do the job so much more quickly. The gulls don't seem to mind, for they follow the plough just as they always did, and it is still quite a pretty sight to see the red tractor with the white gulls overhead.

I can remember many other old customs gone for ever. The muffin man would come round with his basket balanced on his head, ringing his bell to bring the housewives out to buy muffins for tea. On Good Friday the hot cross bun man would appear calling his wares. Mother used to say a little rhyme about the hot cross buns –

One a penny poker, two a penny tongs,

Three a penny fire shovel, hot cross buns.

If your daughters don't like them give them to your sons,

One a penny, two a penny, hot cross buns.

They were worth eating, too, not like the dry, tasteless buns sold nowadays for 4d. or 5d each - they were fresh and sweet and flavoured with spice, and we went in for them in a big way as we all enjoyed them so. Then there was the cat's meat man with his little barrow, calling "Meat, meat," selling little cubes of horsemeat impaled on a little bit of stick. The cats always seemed to know when he was about. The meat cubes only cost a **½**d each, so the man couldn't have made much of a living.

At Christmas time I remember Father calling to me as I lay in bed that the "Waits" were outside. This was a band of men playing in the streets all the Christmas hymns and carols and to me they sounded lovely.

Soon after my 14th birthday I left school, and for the next three years I helped mother in the house and father in the office.

At this point I end my narrative, though I may write of the years that followed later on. I lived through two awful wars and have since suffered the loss of my parents and my beloved youngest brother. No words can tell my sorrow at this shattering blow, and the shadow of this tragic and unexpected loss will never pass from me. Old Omar spoke truly when he wrote –

The Moving Finger writes and having writ moves on

Nor all your piety and wit shall lure it back to cancel half a line

Nor all your tears wash out a word of it

- but it is hard to understand why one so young and good should have been so cruelly and unfairly treated.

I have now lived many years alone and often think of the happy days when I had a "family" to come home to However, I am very thankful for all I have and the many members of my family now living bring me great happiness and much to think about. Our parents were wonderful people, and to them I owe much. Nowadays it seems to me there is not the family atmosphere such as we experienced. In these days pleasures come too easily to young people and I don't think they are enjoyed half so much as when they only came occasionally.

I end with two little verses which have pleased me –

When little things would irk me and I grow

Impatient with my dear ones make me know

How in a moment joy can take its flight

And happiness be quenched in endless night

Keep this thought with me all the livelong day

That I may guard the harsh words I might say

When I would fret and grumble fiery hot

At trifles which to-morrow are forgot

Let me remember, Lord, how it would be

If these, my loved ones, were not here with me.

The Mother

She is the hearth fire that makes four walls home

No room is cold or dark when she is near

She is the lamp lit when the shadows come

The answering voice that robs the night of fear.

She is the lost thing found, the healing kiss

Balm for youth's griefs, the heart that understands

All comfort and all light are where she is

And all of heaven she holds in her two hands.

I left school at the age of 14. I was glad to leave as I had no liking for school. In spite of that I gained first prize in each of the four years I was at my last school, I don't remember working hard as at that time of my life I had a good memory and learned easily. In fact, I think I did pretty well in most subjects.

After leaving school I helped Mother a bit in the house and by taking out my two small brothers, shopping etc. and also helped Dad in the office. He taught me shorthand and I went to evening class to learn typing. The class was held by two sisters and I doubt whether either of them could type. They taught us nothing, just left us to ourselves with some old machines, and consequently I was never a good typist - only the two finger kind. Shorthand was different. Father was an expert and he made me write good shorthand. I can still write it easily after nearly 60 years, though not at speed as I am out of practice. My characters no doubt are out of date too. In my experience most of the present day shorthand writers make characters which would make Pitman turn in his grave and how they transcribe it I can't imagine. I suppose they know their own signs but it is probably the reason for so many errors.

I was still at home when the First World War broke out in 1914. My Father had been down to the Post Office and coming back, he noticed a crowd outside a Club. Someone came out and announced that we were at War. It was a lovely summer day and a Bank Holiday. Many were away on holiday on the Continent and elsewhere, and rushed back to their homes. A day or two after there was a long procession through the town of horses of all kinds, on their way to France, poor things.

For the first few months very little happened, and in the autumn of 1914 I went to Windsor for a week or so to stay with an Uncle and Aunt. It was the first time I had been away on my own and I had a wonderful time visiting all the lovely places in the neighbourhood, including a never to be forgotten service in St. George's Chapel. At that time people thought the War would be over in a few months, but how wrong they were.

In 1915, when I was 17, I thought I would like to get a job, and answered an advertisement in the local paper. I had a reply from the Borough Treasurer asking me to call and see him. This I did, but I was rather disappointed, as he wanted someone older and with more experience. However, it was just as well, as soon after I was asked to go and see his brother, who was a Chartered Accountant with a big practice in the town and surrounding districts. His young male clerks were leaving to join up and he was obliged to start engaging girls. He then had only one clerk (who I always disliked intensely) and a few boys. Mr. King was then in the 40s - rather a delicate man with some kind of ear trouble. He was a very clever man and a jolly one too. He taught me all I know about book-keeping and I owe him a lot. He had never had a shorthand-typist before and accounts were always hand written and tied up with green ribbon. It used to fascinate me to see him sort out figures dotted down on odd pieces of paper and what he called Washing Books, and like a wizard would produce clear accounts and Balance Sheets, but I soon got the hang of it and by following his golden rule and getting out a cash account first, I was able to try my hand at it. It was a great thrill when I began to understand it. Then I began to go out with Mr. King or the other man on the staff to assist on audits - mostly in the town but also in London and the Medway towns.

One of our audits was a weekly one at the Empire Theatre, Chatham, which was a very fine theatre owned by the "Stoll" group, whose headquarters was the London Coliseum. I used to go down on Tuesdays and get the figures ready for Mr. King to sign when he came later in the day. The office staff there was very incompetent and I gradually began to go down on other evenings to prepare the returns which had to be sent every day to the Coliseum. After a while I went down every evening for a small recompense. I found the staff very nice and friendly and very well behaved. I never heard bad language there in spite of the fact that Music Halls were referred to as "Sinks of iniquity". The staff were poorly paid even for those days, but most of them were glad to earn a few extra shillings by evening work. The box office girls were paid 12/- a week, which was only 2/- a night for 2 performances. The programme girls got 9/- a week, but they were able to make a little more by the sale of chocolates. I liked them all very much and often think what good workers they were compared with present day youngsters. I saw a good many of the performances, and they were good.  The first turn on the programme was usually not of much account, but compared with the rubbish shewn on Television to-day they were excellent. Audiences of that day would have hissed 50% of the television acts off the stage. No vulgarity or double meanings were tolerated by the management and I saw many enjoyable and clever acts. One of my favourites was the clown Grock, who in additon to his clowning was a very clever musician. He used to play a very favourite extract from La Traviata on his violin, but sad to say he never reached the end - from memory I think he fell off the piano, or something equally ridiculous. I was there during the latter part of World War 1 and there were packed houses at each of the two nightly shows. Service men had a small reduction, end how they used to flock in. There was a very good orchestra - mostly service men - and they were paid their Union rate of £3 per week. When the house turned out you could have walked on the heads of the crowd filling the narrow street.

I left the accountancy firm in 1920 and went full time to Chatham. It was a mistake, as I soon found out. After the Armistice, business gradually fell off and the Managing Director was soon trying to get me into the Box Office as well as my usual work. I stuck it for two years and then left for good. They tried to make me stay and were quite nasty when I persisted. The Manager was a very nice man and good for everything but office work. His salary was £7 per week. He could play many instruments and was well liked and respected by the staff. His wife was a nice woman who had been a dancer in her youth, and it was a great grief to them to have no children. I often used to go to tea with her, and often wonder what became of them.

During the time I worked in the Theatre a Cinema was built at the side, and sometimes I went down in the afternoon if there was a good film. Now both these buildings are demolished and the site turned into a car park. It seems a pity, as the Theatre was a very fine building on the lines of the London Coliseum, with a dome which slid off during the interval to air the theatre. It was a very interesting experience, but sometimes I think it would have been better to have stayed in accountancy - although nowadays it becomes increasingly difficult with all the problems of Income Tax, etc.

After leaving there I took a job at Tilbury in a large shipping laundry. Father knew the Manager slightly and was of opinion that he was a gentleman. I think he was right. He was a fine looking man, an Irishman, but his failing was drink. At that time numbers of large liners came up the river and into the Dock, and it is sad to think that the dockers themselves have killed the trade with their strikes, etc. Now very few big ships are seen on the river. Coming home in the dark evenings they were a picture, blazing with Nights from stem to stern. These liners sent hundreds of "pieces" to be washed or cleaned.  The Manager went round getting orders and was, of course, asked to have a drink at every stopping place. This was his undoing.

I used to enjoy the trips across the river, but several times, if there was a think fog, the ferry boat couldn't run. There was no Radar then. However, the watermen then came into their own, and I have been across in all kinds of small craft. The river men were wonderful. There we sat, in a blanket of thick fog, yet in a very short time we drew up safely at the Tilbury or Gravesend landing stage.

There was quite a large staff of girls at the laundry, earning rates of pay laid down by the Board of Trade. From memory the 14 year olds got 2**½**d per hour and 18 or over 30/- a week, which was the maximum. There were three or four in the office with me.  The forewoman would hand in the pay sheets and I used to make up the packets and pay out at 12 on Saturday morning. I used to take my lunch, and at that time the Argentine meat shops were beginning to appear. There was one quite near the ferry, and I could get a nice little chop for 2**½**d.  There was a cheerful girl at the laundry who presided over the kitchen, and would warm up the dinners and do a little cooking.  If I had a chop she would cook it with a few chips. The forewoman and I used to eat our lunch in a little private room. She was a very pleasant person. She had an egg custard made every day and my good mother made me a jam or fruit pasty. We used to share our sweet every day. I stayed there I think about 18 months, but one day the Manager came in in a very nasty mood and annoyed me by complaining about my work. So, at the end of that week I gave in my notice, as the work really wasn't good enough for me, and I felt I was wasting my time. He had by then forgotten his complaints and thought I was leaving because of the journey. He kindly asked me to look in any time I was passing and have a cup of tea with him, but needless to say I never did.

I then went into the drapery trade, in the office of what was then the chief local drapery. I knew the Secretary, as I used to go there to check the books, and she had asked me several times if I would join their staff. She was a very fine looking woman, always beautifully dressed, and knew her job. Many of the staff "lived in", and how they ever escaped being burned to death I can't imagine. The old building was a maze of narrow passages and staircases. The rooms were very small and only the buyers had a room to themselves, yet they seemed happy enough and for the most part good friends. Most of these girls came from broken homes or had a step or no parents. The work was easy, but still I wasn't satisfied and felt I should be doing something better. It was an experience, I suppose.

When the January sale was over, out came the new merchandise and the buyers came out in their new frocks. It made quite a bit of excitement admiring or criticising Miss A. or Miss B. in their new finery. We wore navy blue in the office, and I had some very nice dresses myself. I remember one in particular, a fine navy crepe with a, waistband beautifully embroidered in jade, cream and crimson. I felt really smart in it, but alas it wore all too quickly at the bend of the arm.

When fixing my wages the Manager wanted me to have my meals in, but I was living so near that I declined. However, on Fridays and Saturdays and other late closing nights I had tea there. We had slabs of Bread and margarine, jam or watercress served in vegetable dishes and cups of what was called tea - altogether a most unappetising meal. If the other meals were similar, I'm glad I didn't have to partake of them. I know the seniors used to supplement their diet with whatever they could afford.

In my young days the annual fortnight holiday was planned months ahead. I usually went alone and stayed in a small guest house, paying about £3 per week. I don't remember ever being unlucky - the food and beds were good and I met some very nice people. On our small wages we weren't able to save much, and usually had little more than our three weeks money, I frequently had just enough to get home, and many of my friends said the same. However we bathed and walked, and generally the weather was fair.

Later I spent some holidays with one of Dad's cousins who lived in a pretty thatched house in Lyme Regis. They had a beach hut, and with another cousin also staying there I usually spent the days there. It was a lovely part and sometimes there were blackberrying expeditions. I often think of the view from the garden, with Golden Cap in the distance. Another memorable holiday was spent in Cheltenham with a cousin who lived there and was a district nurse. She had a small car, an Austin Seven, and we visited many of the lovely villages in the Cotswolds. Later when she lived near Malvern she took me round that beautiful part of the country. Now I am old, holidays have no appeal, but I can go back and remember those happy days and the kindness of the people - now long deceased - who made them possible. Our Father hailed from Devon which is perhaps why the West Country has such an appeal. My favourite St. Ives is the best remembered - it was quiet and unspoilt when I first went there, but I'm told it is so commercialised now that I should hate to go back.

I'm sure people were happier in those days with simple pleasures, now the greed for more end more seems to show in their faces and they never seem to be satisfied. Our Mother used to cook every day and provide delicious and nourishing meals, but nowadays all is different. Crops are sprayed, and fruit and vegetables don't taste nearly the same.

At this time we lived in a little house in Darnley Road. It was much nicer and more convenient than our birthplace, but rather small. However, here we had a bathroom of sorts and just one flight of stairs instead of two or three in the previous house. My eldest brother married soon after we moved there and afterwards moved to Cambridge with his wife and small son. My second brother, who was in the Essex Police Force, also got married. My third brother took me to Ipswich for the wedding, he then having a motor bike and side car. This helped the accommodation a lot, and the three boys left at home shared the large front bedroom.

After about four years in the drapery trade my eldest brother, who was with a London firm of Chartered Accountants and resident Manager of their Cambridge Office, suggested that I should apply for a job there. I was very hesitant about this as my shorthand had become rusty and, as I said before, typing was never my strong point. However, he obtained an interview for me and I went up to see the Partner for whom my brother worked. He was a very pleasant man and I felt quite at home with him, and as his typist was on the point of leaving, I was engaged in her place.  Then began 12 very happy years. I was very nervous at first, but soon realised that although my typing wasn't good I was far more intelligent in other ways than the other typists and very anxious to get on.

At first our offices were in Fenchurch Avenue, a turning off Lime Street. In our lunch hour it was very interesting to roam around Leadenhall Market and the little courts and, alleys in the City. We didn't get very big salaries in those days, but the season ticket was of course much less and a good lunch could be had for 1/- to 1/6. There were also shops known as the Guinea shops, where a very good dress could be bought for one guinea.

![Scan](AllanCharlesworthCo.jpg)

I was always able to manage, but some of the other girls I know had to borrow from their parents when their season ticket had to be renewed.

The typists sat in one corner of the big General Office, and the clatter of typewriters all day long must have been very distracting to the other people working in the same office. Our Manager, who was a cousin of the Partner I worked for, was a very genial man and knew London from end to end. If there was a special letter or parcel to be delivered he would ask if we were busy, and if not one of us would go out and deliver it. At the same time he would tell us of any interesting places in the direction we were going, and in this way I saw many places I should otherwise not have seen.

At that time the Firm only engaged young men who had been to Public Schools or University, and they were a very nice lot. Of course I was much older than these boys and I was very fond of them. It was a very busy firm and there was usually plenty of work. A lot of investigation was done in connection with the kitchens of the Cambridge Colleges, and my chief did the Reports with my brother assisting and getting out all the particulars. There were about 16 of these lengthy Reports - full of statistics - and I did a lot of dictation and typing in this connection. Once I went to Cambridge and stayed with my brother and the Reports were dictated in the Cambridge office. It was winter time, and for the first time in many years the river was frozen over. It was a pretty sight to see all the young men skating with their coloured scarves flying – more like a Breughel picture. In our spare time my brother took me round the College gardens, end very lovely they were with lawns like velvet, hundreds of years old. In one of them was a small herd of tame deer.

During the time I worked in London we moved from our small house in the town to a nice new house on the outskirts of the town. There was a much larger garden in a very rough state, but we soon got it laid out and it was a great joy to us all. My brothers laid a little terrace about half way down with coloured paving, and Mother and I laid the crazy path down the side. We also had a little greenhouse where we raised all sorts of flowering plants and at one time we grew a large quantity of tomatoes and even cucumbers. In time we made a very nice garden and I know that Mother, especially, had a great deal of pleasure from it.

Every Saturday afternoon Mother and I went into the town by bus, looking round the shops, and it was a weekly treat we both enjoyed, although Dad used to say he couldn't understand why we had to go every week. In 1938 I had a bad arm through being vaccinated, but in the summer my brother, who was going on to Cornwall, left me with a cousin at Lyme Regis, where I had a lovely holiday.

This was Munich time, and we came back to find trenches being due in our road and all sorts of rumours going round. However, the War was postponed end we were able to enjoy the wedding of my fourth brother, which was a very enjoyable and happy occasion. We were all very relieved and thought there would be no war, but it was only putting off the evil day. The following summer my youngest brother and I went to Cornwall for our holiday, taking with us a little boy my brother knew and was fond of. We had a grand time in spite of the disquieting news in the papers. But one morning I was walking in the High Street when I overheard someone say that Hitler had invaded Poland. A few hours later we were heading for home, as my brother was in the Fire Service and all members were recalled. We drove back through a most terrible storm, and as we were only allowed a very feeble light we lost our way two or three times. However, we safely reached home in the early hours of the morning to find Mother still up anxiously awaiting us, as the little boy was to be evacuated and had to report at his school at 6 a.m. These children left the Pier soon after and were taken by sea to a town on the East coast. It seemed a strange place to take them and many of the mothers were in great distress, but they all arrived in safety and stayed for some time in various East Anglian towns.

About the middle of that morning the siren sounded for the first time, end there was great excitement with everyone wondering what would happen next. Wardens were rushing to their posts, some people were shouting "Close the windows" while others said we shouldn't, but the all clear sounded very soon and apparently it had been a false alarm.

Life went on much the same as usual for some months. My three brothers were all in the Fire Service and running the business. Towards the end of 1940 I decided that I ought to leave London and come and help in the business, as my two young brothers had voluntarily joined up and it was very hard for the other one to run the business as well as being in the Fire Service and on sudden call. His family was evacuated to the country and the staff was very depleted. I did not want to leave my job, as I was very happy there but I left at the end of 1940. I was not very happy at first, but the work was not difficult and as things got worse regarding the staff, I felt I had done the right thing. My parents I know were glad I was not travelling as we were getting more and more raids. During the last few months travelling had become very uncertain and with damage to the railways our journey often had to be partly taken in buses.

But enough has been written about the Second World War. As usual we made the best of things but it was a very trying and anxious time to most of us. The brightest time was when the boys came home on leave, but how quickly the time went and they were gone again.

At long last the War ended and what a relief it was to go to bed and know we would not be disturbed by the siren or the guns.

The food situation did not improve for a very long time and we were on poor rations, also clothing was rationed and we had to make our things last. It was worse for men, as their clothes needed so many coupons, but we managed and I don't think we ever looked shabby. I cannot write about the years after the War and the time when both our parents were ill. My youngest brother lived at home with us and was a tower of strength to me. It was a very sad time, and in 1949 our dear patient mother passed away. Her end was no doubt hastened by the very trying time she had nursing our Father, who had become bedridden and  needed a lot of attention. Nothing was the same without her, but with the aid of a good housekeeper and various other helpers we carried on, and 14 months later our father died in his 91st year.

My brother and I were then left on our own and we were able to get about a bit more. We bought a large plot of orchard land about four miles out of the town and many a happy hour we spent there. We planned to have a house built there for me, and if my brother married he would also build there. We were very happy and spent many hours planning our new home, but just as it seemed in our grasp after all the delays my dear brother fell ill, and in no time he was gone for ever. As I have said before, it was the most terrible blow I could have had, and life has never been the same to me since. I have never ceased to ask why it had to be, but there is no answer and what little faith I ever had is gone. People ask me why I don't do this or that, or travel to other parts of the world, but I have no inclination and travelling doesn't interest me at all now.  Visits to my relations are as far as I want to go.

Since then we have lost our second brother. He was a fine strong man and never ill, but he suffered much at the end, He was not very good at school, as were the others, but he was a kindly cheerful man and devoted to his family, and well liked by his fellow workers.

I am nearly at the end now. My eldest brother with whom I had much in common, was married for the second time to a foreign women. She has been so much against our family from the first that I do not want to have anything to do with her, and as they now live in Italy I don't suppose I shall ever see them again. I have two splendid brothers left and so many young nieces and nephews to brighten my life. I cannot express how much they mean to me, they provide unending    interest, and I treasure their kindness to their old aunt. In addition I have many good friends, but alas their ranks keep thinning. Although the old friends are best I do meet new ones and enjoy their company. I do not want to retire but realise it must come soon. There is nothing like work and I am very thankful for my good health, especially when I see some of my contemporaries.

My home and garden give me great pleasure and I am a great reader. There is much to be deplored at the state of the world and it looks as though there will be little or no improvement in my time. We know that in the old days there was bitter poverty and much that could have been altered, now things have swung full circle and the majority are greedy all the time for more and are never contented. Our parents never had much money and had to work very hard for it, but there are few like them left now. My only hope is that when my end comes it will be quick and that I shall not be a burden or nuisance to my family. I am fortunate that my lot has been cast in such a beautiful place. I never mind how quiet it is and am glad to get away from the noise and rush in the town and come home to peace and quiet.

My love to you all, and especially to all my dear young people who are so much in my thoughts. I hope they will all have very happy lives and that soon the world will become a better place and that there will never be the sad and terrible years that I had when I was young.

War Time Travelling

To go back to the time I was working in London and travelling became so hazardous. After I left, many of my friends continued to travel up and down till the War ended, and it is remarkable that of all the people I knew on the train, not one was ever injured. The only person I knew of to be hurt was a young man who worked in our office and was sitting in a shelter while a raid was on. A piece of shrapnel was flung into the shelter and injured one of his arms, which had to be amputated.

My first recollection of raids upsetting our journey was being halted one morning at Sidcup and we had to get out of the train and queue up for buses to take us to our destinations. One of our party spied something in a shop - I think it was tinned salmon – and several of us went in to get a tin - a great prize. It was worse coming home in the dark, but I had a good friend and we always tried to travel together. I only remember one occasion when I didn't get to the office. We had to leave the train at Woolwich and get into an underground shelter. We came out once or twice but as it seemed impossible to continue our journey we found there was a train back about mid-day and we came home. That was a day of continuous raids. A little girl living near us was standing at the door of the shelter with her father and a friend when a piece of shrapnel hit and killed her. My father never seemed to fear, and usually insisted on going out. If we were called up in the night he always dressed completely with collar and tie. For some time we three slept in an underground shelter in the garden, but Dad caught a bad cold and the Doctor said we shouldn't sleep there. So whenever there was an alert I slept in the cupboard under the stairs and the parents under the dining room table. We had guns very near us and our doors and windows rattled, but only once was something shaken off a shelf in the pantry, so we were very lucky. Our old house in the town was quite badly damaged, but no one was hurt.

THE FIRST WORLD WAR

The years of the First World War were very busy ones, and in time two or three more girls came on to the staff. I was promoted to a nice little private office with a carpet on the floor and everything Very comfortable. How these rooms were heated I can't remember but I don't remember ever feeling cold.

When the boys came on leave we usually managed a little tea party if the "governor" was out, although he too always welcomed a visit from the boys.

I shall never forget the first Zeppelin raid on the town. I was just getting into bed when loud reports were heard and my parents rushed to the front door. They soon shut it, and calling to me we all rushed upstairs to the top floor and brought the three little boys down to the cellar where we wrapped ourselves in blankets and sat on the sacks of potatoes. Several houses were destroyed but no one was hurt, though many had narrow escapes. After this I became very nervous and trembly whenever the alarm was sounded, but after a while we settled down to as normal a life as was possible.

Father kept his allotments going and sometimes we took a picnic tea to an especially pleasant one in the neighbourhood of Shears Green and enjoyed the peace of the country.

I remember going to stay with friends in the Leyton district for the purpose of attending attending wedding. The night before the wedding there was a raid and the bride and bridegroom had a very disturbed night. However, although they were rather late we all turned up and it went off all right after all. It seems now a rather foolish thing to go and stay in London.

With rationing our diet was a bit limited, but Dad was always trying out new dishes and we had puddings made from maize flour, savoury rice, etc. which were quite nice besides being filling. Mother's oaten scones and potato cakes were delicious and she made good suet puddings with meat or fruit and good Irish stews. I never remember going hungry and the only dish I wasn't keen on was rice boiled in a basin. It was white and solid enough to be cut out in slices and needed sugar or a spoonful of syrup to get it down.

PARTIES

The little house in Darnley Road witnessed a good many parties and family gatherings. I can remember many happy occasions sitting in the front room, with a. cousin playing the piano, my eldest brother singing - songs from Chu Chin Chow were great favourites with the rest of us joining in the chorus. When my eldest brother married the bride elected to be married in the Register Office, but Mother prepared a fine spread and wedding cake and all the guests came. We opened the doors and  sat round a long table doing justice to the meal. My My two young brothers and a friend used to amuse as by acting plays and I remember them doing "White Cargo" with enamel basins on their heads, but what the basins represented I can't recollect. Another time several of us did a playlet "Five birds in a cage", an adventure with five people trapped in a lift. Yes, we had some happy times when we were all young.

On the occasion of my fourth brother's marriage - when we had moved to Orchard Avenue - we had a marquee in the garden and most of the guests came to tea, including some from a distance who had not been to the Church and reception. It was a lovely day and we sat in the garden long after the bride and bridegroom had left for their honeymoon. Even in war time with rationing we managed to have our little parties, and on one occasion Mother made a batch of her famous potato cakes, which we called Woolton's Special after the Minister of Food.

After the War three of my friends and myself used to gather together each week, calling ourselves the Forty Club, and this continued until it was nearly the Sixty Club. On these occasions we used to work for a Charity in which we were interested, and once a year in October we hired a Hall and had a little Bazaar. We got a great deal of pleasure out of this and our many friends rallied round, and after a few years we had raised £1,000, for the Charity. By this time we were all getting old and decided that it was too much for us to continue, much to our regret. Our little group continued to work for the Charity, and even to this day one loyal worker still raises several pounds each year in various ways. At one time we had perhaps twenty kind friends who helped us in various ways, but alas through the intervening years many have passed away.

For many years we had a roast beef supper on Christmas Eve, to which members of the family came. It was a good start to the Christmas holidays but a lot of work for Mother at an already busy time. At long last she decided it must be the last one, and sad to say she was right, as by the next Christmas she had become very tired and frail and quite unable to make the effort.

Before that we at one time had a Friday gathering, and the favourite dish was Sausages in Batter. This was really delicious, as the sausages at that time were rich and flavoursome and the batter mouth watering; now a sausage is hardly worth eating.

In those days we sat at home in the evenings and all were busy at something. There was of course no Television and Radio was in its infancy, but we were never bored and were always busy at something.
