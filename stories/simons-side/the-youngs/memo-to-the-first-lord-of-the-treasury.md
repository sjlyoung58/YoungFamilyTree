# Memo to the First Lord of the Treasury

*Extracted from Confluence FamilyTree wiki (exported 2 November 2012)*

---

It seems that at the end of his life, FCY fell upon hard times and attempted to obtain a pension - at that time there was no general pension provision, so applications were made on merit directly to the First Lord of the Treasury to be granted a pension from the Civil List.

This was around the last time that the post of [First Lord of the Treasury and Prime Minister](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Lord_of_the_Treasury) were held separately. Arthur Balfour held the post at the time of this application in 1897. There was some doubt as to whether the pension was actually awarded, but in the obituary to Julian Young it is stated that the pension was awarded.

This document also contains a Bibliography pf FCY's principal works.

The Actual Word document below is laid out fairly faithfully to the original, but the text version provided in the page below loses most of the formatting.

## The text of the Application

### A Memorial

### TO THE

### FIRST LORD OF THE TREASURY,

*PRAYING, that in**view* *of* *the great and lasting services* *rendered during the past Thirty-five Years to Scholastic, Practical, Technical, and General Literature, by* FRANCIS CHILTON-YOUNG*, and of the necessitous circumstances in which he is placed, HER MAJESTY will be graciously pleased to award to him a Pension from the Civil List.*

LIST OF FRANCIS CHILTON YOUNG'S PRINCIPAL WORKS.

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| QUESTIONS ON WHITE'S "LANDMARKS OF ENGLISH HISTORY," with Map. | THE BIBLE STUDENT'S HANDBOOK. |
| ROUTLEDGE'S EDUCATIONAL MANUALS | THE STUDENT'S COMPREHENSIVE ETYMOLOGICAL DICTIONARY. |
| BEETON'S DICTIONARY OF GEOGRAPHY. | EVERY MAN HIS OWN MECHANIC. |
| LIFE AND TIMES OF GARIBALDI | HOME CARPENTRY FOR HANDY MEN |
| THE EXCELSIOR READERS | EVERYBODY’S GUIDE TO POULTRY KEEPING |
| THE MANCHESTER READERS |  |
| THE HOUSE AND ITS FURNITURE |  |

Mr. Francis Chilton-Young came to London in 1862, at the pressing solicitation of the late Mr. S. O. Beeton, to enter on a purely literary career.  Prior to this he had been engaged for eight years in School-keeping, during which period he had written Questions on "Landmarks of English History," by the Rev. James White, illustrated by a map showing at one view England under the ancient Britons, Romans, Danes, and Saxons. This led on to the preparation of several School books, which appeared under the title of " Routledge's Educational Manuals," a map showing Europe at the time of Charles V. of Germany, and many maps for issue with Cassell's "Illustrated Family Paper." He was further entrusted with the preparation of several maps and plans to accompany Dr. W. H. Russell's " History of the Crimean War," with maps, geographical and historical, for Vol. I of Beeton's " Dictionary of Science and art."

On coming to London he became one of the principal contributors to the second volume of "Beeton's Dictionary of Science and Art," for which he wrote several hundred articles.  He also contributed many papers to the " Englishwoman's Domestic Magazine" and "Boys' Own Magazine," and was entrusted with the compilation of Beeton's "Dictionary of Geography." On the coming of Garibaldi to England in 1864, Mr. Young was asked to write " The Life and Times of Garibaldi," which was issued in weekly penny numbers, and put a considerable strain on his bodily powers. The production of this work at once settled his literary position, for he became forthwith. Mr. Beeton's. chief general Editor, and in addition to specially editing "The Young Englishwoman," had the supervision of all Mr. Beeton's magazines, and negotiated the purchase of various works which Mr. Beeton then acquired. He also contributed portions, such as those on Stoves and Cooking Utensils, to Mrs. Beeton's famous " Cookery Book."

This continued until Mr. Beeton's business passed into the hands of Messrs. Ward, Lock, and Tyler, when Mr. Young transferred his services, as Editor, to Messrs. Cassell, Petter, and Galpin, editing for them the last volume of Cassell's " Illustrated Family Paper," in which appeared (Mr. Young being from a boy an assiduous herald and genealogist) the Rev. Charles Boutell's " English Heraldry."

On the completion of this work, Mr. Young undertook the compilation of "The Excelsior Readers" for Mr. Thomas Murby, the revision of the Maps in Cassell's Atlas, purchased from the Weekly Dispatch Newspaper, and subsequently sold to Mr.. G. W. Bacon. He also re-wrote, revised, and brought up to date several School books and Works of Reference for various publishers, and notably Messrs. Houlston and Sons .and Messrs. T. J. Allman and Sons. To particularise every thing that then passed through and under his hands would take far more space than can be given here.

We now come to the more important work of Mr. Young’s literary life. About 1867 he re-arranged for Messrs. Cassell, Fetter, and Galpin, John Cassell's original issue of the "Popular Educator," and provided for the introduction of much new matter, and on this he was occupied for three years; after which he took charge of the production and edited the first issue of the "Technical Educator," over which another two years were passed. While engaged on these great undertakings, which were highly spoken of and praised in the House of Commons by Mr. Robert Lowe, afterwards Lord Sherbrooke, he prepared the " Manchester Readers forthe late John Heywood of Manchester, and dealt with editorially or wrote many books of an educational character.

About this time Mr. Beeton seceded from Messrs. Ward, Lock, and Tyler and commenced business with his former publisher, and Mr. Young joined them as Editor, and produced Knight's Penny books, the Daisy Books, etc., and Iooked after the literary portion Mr. Beeton's Fashion Papers. Shortly after reopening this connection Mr. Beeton advised Mr. Young to remove to Gravesend, in order to be near him when at Greenhithe, but Mr. Beeton died before he was installed once more at Greenhithe, and Mr. Young was thrown on his own resources. He was then fifty years of age. This was in 1877.

He soon, however, made arrangements with Messrs. Ward, Lock, and Co., as the firm was then styled, and wrote for them the "House and its Furniture," "The Bible Student's Handbook," and "The Student's Etymological Dictionary," of which the *Athenaeum* said, "*It contains few omissions and fewer errors, and is altogether the best book of the kind that we are acquainted within the English Language.*"

It was two or three years after this that Mr. Young, who was at this time Messrs. Houlston and Sons' Editor, wrote his famous work, "Every Man his Own Mechanic," which was received with a chorus of approval by the press, and placed him in the first rank of practical writers on Technical subjects. Encouraged by this, he projected and for seven years edited "Amateur Work, Illustrated," and he became Editor of Letts's Diaries for Messrs. Cassell and Co., who were part owners of this well known literary property. Before this he had become editor of Beeton's "New Book of Garden Management," and latterly of many other works on Gardening belonging to Messrs. Ward: Lock; and Co., which he revised, re-arranged, and, in a great measure, re-wrote. But a divergence of opinion as to the future of "Amateur Work" arose between the publishers and himself, and he left these and projected for Messrs. Cassell and Co. a similar publication in weekly numbers, called "Work," which he carried on for three years. Since that time he has written a sequel and companion to " Every Man his Own Mechanic," entitled "Home Carpentry for Handy Men," and early in 1896, the present year, produced for Messrs. Saxon and Co. "Everybody's Guide to Poultry-Keeping," under the *nom-de-plume* of Denzil Sherraton.

Despite the valuable work that he has done in his time, Mr. Young is in very reduced circumstances, having had no opportunity of saving money through having given "host to fortune" in a wife and nine children, whom he has had, with a single exception, to bring up and nurture on an income which has never ruled higher than, £300 per annum. His eyesight is failing fast, and it is with difficulty that he can see to write: his sight having been permanently injured by the small type used in " The - Student's Etymological Dictionary." Moreover, in addition to keen suffering for more than a year and a half from rheumatic gout, he was, on April 10th, 1895, knocked down and sadly hurt by being run into by a railway collector's van, sustaining serious injuries in the right leg, which keep him confined to the house almost entirely.

Mr. Young is now on the eve of entering the 70th year of his age.

Taking that which is set forth above into consideration, we, the undersigned, do most earnestly support this MEMORIAL to the First Lord Of The Treasury, praying that Her Majesty may be graciously pleased to award Mr. Chilton-Young a Pension from the Civil List.
