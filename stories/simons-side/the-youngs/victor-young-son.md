## A brief History - by Alan Young

Having read my Aunt Gertrude's memoirs recently, I felt I should put on record a brief history of my grandfather's firm, which has been indirectly the source for several members of his family's incomes and ultimately the source of what limited wealth my own family has enjoyed.

Victor Young started off by using the front room of a small terrace house in Berkeley Road which lay just off the main street in the centre of Gravesend. The house incidentally was also the family home of his wife and six children, my Aunt Gert being the oldest (and only girl).

Victor Young had a limited income, derived principally from his secretary-ship of a local building society (in those days a group of investors putting funds in, and in turn drawing out by lot a sufficient sum to buy/build a house) and his meagre earnings from teaching shorthand. He was encouraged to put up a plate saying "ESTATE AGENT" by one wealthy acquaintance who promised him some income from the collection of the rents. I always understood that this promise was never kept but this was hearsay. This was 1887.

Evidently the business did prosper slowly, and in due course Percy, the eldest boy, was offered the chance to come in with his father, but never liked the experience and left to join the Police.  
The next eldest, Cyril, was then given the chance and he too felt unable to make much of it and he left to study accountancy and eventually qualified in that profession.  
Next in line was my father, Donald, who was then given his opportunity, which he eagerly took and the business never looked back. He once told me that he had had a job working for a grocer and told his boss that a cat had peed in the sugar bag — his instructions were to mix it all in! after which he left. Anyhow, as the business grew he managed to persuade his dad to move in to bigger and better premises at 25 Windmill Street and I can remember being taken in to see him and my grandfather both sitting at desks in the same room. This must have been lust before the war. when I was about 7 and also must have been the time when the business became VICTOR YOUNG & SON. The two next boys, now of course grown men, Hubert and Bernard, both had to enlist in the army, and my father was given dispensation as the sole owner of a business which would have had to close. He served in the National Fire Service and then the Home Guard. In the years leading up to the war, they acquired the adjoining 26 Windmill Street and this was the firm's main office — up to and past the Centenary.

There was very little buying and selling during the war, but the now substantial rent collection and maintenance kept the firm just about above water, and once the war had ended Hubert and Bernard were taken into the firm, Hubert being taken into partnership but Bernard tragically died at a very young age.

At around this time I was just about leaving school, and it had always been understood that I would join the firm, very much with my approval, and I was articled to a London Chartered Surveyor at the age of 17 — I was glad to leave school having been a boarder since the age of 8. A premium of several hundred pounds was paid which was to be repaid to me in lieu of wages.

My father was a Chartered Estate Agent (in common with many other local agents) and this qualification was one which could be purchased by subscription in those days. He was very anxious that I should qualify as a Chartered Surveyor because this would push the firm in to the top bracket and generate a great deal more income.

I duly commenced training enjoying the experience of commuting to London and also started a correspondence course with the Royal Institute of Chartered Surveyors. After my articles expired I stayed on fora year or two, and after a prolonged battle with the RIGS they eventually allowed me to join the family firm, which was not at first deemed to be sufficiently professional experience fora budding young Chartered Surveyor.

I joined the firm in about 1950 with a "princely" salary of £8 per week, which my father was generous enough to continue throughout my 2 year National Service and this was still my salary when I was married. I eventually qualified, having completed the last year or so of my correspondence course as a married man, not quite what my mother wanted, but one can work much better at home not having to meet up with one's intended at frequent intervals!

Having completed National Service, I at last started work in earnest. Like all future bosses I started at the bottom, learning all the rent rounds (about 8 of them) some of which I knew vaguely having accompanied my mother when on school holidays, a job she had to do for many years. I was given an office on the first floor and gradually eased my way into some useful work acquiring clients of my own, and so bringing in, slowly, extra income to justify my existence!

About this time my father, who had enjoyed one or two house content sales over the years, had the opportunity to buy Tivoli House which he could see would make ideal Auction Rooms. This was an inspired move, since executors disposing of houses with valuable contents would instruct us on both counts. Having 'clerked' for my father on the rostrum for several sales he, without warning, handed me the gavel and changed places halfway through the sale. Apart from losing my voice, I enjoyed the experience, and with Hubert, we shared the rostrum every third sale for many years.

It was about this time that my Aunt Gert joined the firm as bookkeeper and accountant —having had much of a similar job with the Chatham Empire Theatre.  
Tea was always served in her office at 4pm and became a short meeting time for the family & partners. She always refused more than a nominal wage in her selfless attitude to family and firm.

Shortly after this my father decided we ought to have an office in Meopham, a country village to the south of Gravesend, where a great deal of development was in progress. We decided to rent a small office on Station Approach and appointed a local Insurance Salesman as Manager. This was one of the few decisions I ever disagreed with, especially as we had a young man with real talent who had been promised the job if it ever occurred. A few years later a perfect opportunity arose for a spectacularly sited building, an old General Store, and I persuaded my partners to buy and extend it and to appoint Barry Bright, the young man who had joined us from school. Both projects have been a resounding success, and Barry was eventually taken into partnership.  
Around this time I had thoughts of extending into Rochester; we found a small one man firm between Rochester and Chatham where the existing owner wanted to retire and we bought the freehold property, converting the rear into flats and appointing a young man as manager. The position was not too good, and we eventually bought an office in High Street Rochester, in poor repair — which we renovated — and converted more flats. At this point I think I was contributing to four mortgages, including my own house but we did it at the right time and profited therefrom, and I still have the controlling interest in the three freeholds.

Shortly after this we were able to rent from a client a property in Northfleet where we established a fourth branch, benefiting from a Halifax Building Society Agency. These agencies were useful in that we could attract money into the deposit accounts and obtain mortgages for our Sales. This last arrangement gradually promoted a curious phenomenon in that Banks and Insurance Companies began to believe that they could increase the sales of their products ie Insurance, Mortgage, Endowments etc by buying up chains of Estate Agents often at inflated prices.  
Whilst this was quickening, we reached our centenary in 1987 and had a splendid banquet to which we invited most of the town's leading businessmen, including most of our rivals.  
Shortly after this, I heard that Prudential Insurance Co were leading the race for building up a chain of Agents and had already bought Ward & Co and were looking towards Sevenoaks. I wrote to the Prudential suggesting that we were a Four Branch firm ideally situated to link up the Medway Towns with Sevenoaks and so it came to pass after lengthy negotiations, including the doubling of the initial offer, we sold out — establishing market rents for the freeholds — despite some reservations from the junior partners. They were a little happier when, in a few years, the market had one of its cyclical downturns and many of the firms managed to repurchase at something like 10% of their sale figures!
