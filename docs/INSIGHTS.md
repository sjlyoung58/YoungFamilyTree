### The "Lambton" Middle Name

The recurring middle name "Lambton" traces to **Ralph Lambton** (1652–1717), whose daughter **Anne Lambton** (b. 1701) married Robert Chilton. Their daughter **Dorothy Chilton** (1730–1806, buried Iver Church, Bucks) married Robert Young (gen 8), bringing both the Chilton and Lambton names into the Young line. The Lambton connection reaches further back — Ralph Lambton's notes reference a connection to Thomas Lambton (b. c.1408) and the Hedworth family through the marriage of Margaret Lambton and Robert Hedworth in the 15th century.

### The Cary Connection

The earliest known wife in the line is a mysterious **Cary** who married Robert Young (d. 1603). A note from the "Visitation of England and Wales, Vol. 4" records her as a "maid of honour to Queen Elizabeth." The Visitation volume held in the repository (Vol. 5, pp. 140–142) may contain further details of this connection.

### The Newcastle Origins

The Young family were prominent in Newcastle-upon-Tyne in the 17th century. **Robert Young** served as **Sheriff of Newcastle-upon-Tyne in 1647** — during the Civil War. The family were merchants, and Christopher YOUNG (gen 6, 1638–1691) was possibly a physician (occupation recorded as "M.D.?"). Ralph Maddison, father of Christopher's wife Sarah, was also a Newcastle merchant.

## 2. The French Huguenot Connection: d'Abbadie & Delafosse

One of the most fascinating threads in the tree is the French Huguenot ancestry via the **d'Abbadie** family, connecting to the Youngs through marriage.

### The d'Abbadie Family in London

The d'Abbadies were Huguenot refugees who settled in London. Four d'Abbadie children were baptised at the **French Huguenot Church, Threadneedle Street, London** between 1730 and 1735:

- **François d'Abbadie** — baptised 22 Dec 1730
- **Anne Marie d'Abbadie** — baptised 18 Dec 1732
- **Daniel Augustin d'Abbadie** — baptised 25 Jul 1733 (later Captain, Hon. East India Company)
- **Pierre d'Abbadie** — baptised 4 Jun 1735

Their parents were Daniel D. d'Abbadie (b. 1670) and likely a wife from the **de la Cour** family — Jane Theresia d'Abbadie's parents are recorded as "Daniel d'Abbadie of Richmond and Theresa de la Cour."

### The Cross-Channel Marriage Web

The d'Abbadie family created a tight web of marriages connecting France, London, and the English countryside:

- **Robert Mark Delafosse** (1757–1819) married **Jane Theresia d'Abbadie** (1760–1822) — 10 children. Robert was a clergyman at St Mary Magdalene, Richmond, where a monument stands to him.
- **Thomas Chilton Lambton Young** (1769–1826, Rector of Dodbrooke, Devon) married **Margaret Louisa D'Abbadie** (1775–1859) — 13 children. This is the marriage that brought the French Huguenot blood into the Young line.
- **Joseph Daniel d'Abbadie** (1753–1834) returned to **Rochefort, France** and married **Marie Louise Grabeuil** there in 1792. They settled in **Tonnay-Charente**, where 14 events in the database are recorded — births, deaths, and marriages spanning 1797–1868.

### The Tonnay-Charente Branch

Joseph Daniel's return to France created a distinct branch of the family. His children and grandchildren were born in Tonnay-Charente, including:
- **Edouard Louis d'Abbadie (de Sireix)** (1796–1885) — the "de Sireix" suffix suggests a landed estate connection
- **Sophie d'Abbadie** (1799–1868) who married Arnaud Auguste Constantin of Bordeaux
- Several Constantin grandchildren born in Tonnay-Charente through the 1830s

### Richmond, Surrey: The Huguenot Hub

Richmond was a centre of Huguenot settlement, and the tree reflects this. Multiple Delafosse children were baptised at **St Mary Magdalen, Richmond** between 1784 and 1825. The Delafosse family ran a school there — Daniel Charles Delafosse operated the **Richmond Academy**, where (according to research notes) the young Richard Francis Burton was once a pupil.

## 3. The Mullord Dynasty of North London

The **Mullord** family — 162 individuals, the largest surname in the tree — were a sprawling Victorian and Edwardian London family centred on **Islington, Shoreditch, Hornsey, and Finsbury**. Their story is one of artisan industry, upward mobility, and remarkable longevity.

### The Patriarch: Alexander Mullord (1831–1914)

Alexander was a **Manufacturing Stationer** who by the 1871 census employed **56 men and boys, 23 women and girls**. By 1881 this had grown to **80 hands**. His factory occupied **1, 2 & 3 Penn Street, Hoxton** — a Mullord industrial compound. He was also a **Freemason**. His company, **H. & A. Mullord Ltd**, was dissolved in 1896 and the telephone number for the Penn Street premises changed over the decades (Dalston 2550 in 1913, Clerkenwell 8750 in 1925). HM Land Registry records show that by 1924, the Mullords held freehold over 114–130 Bridport Place, 2–18 Hyde Road, and 1–3 Penn Street.

Alexander married **Annie Roberts** (1833–1903) and had **12 children**. Both are buried at **Abney Park Cemetery** (plot 97221).

## 4. Remarkable Lives

### Henry George Delafosse, CB (1835–1905) — Cawnpore Massacre Survivor

Major-General Henry George Delafosse was **one of only four survivors of the Cawnpore Massacre on 27 June 1857** during the Indian Rebellion. He was the son of Henry Delafosse (1794–1845, Bengal Artillery officer who died of sunstroke at Lakki-ki-Dera) and grandson of Robert Mark Delafosse and Jane Theresia d'Abbadie. The Delafosse military connection to India spans generations — his brother **Claude Fraser Delafosse** (1868–1950) was baptised at **Futtegurh, India**.

### Hannah Dorothy Mullord — Collins Music Hall Star

Hannah Dorothy Mullord was a **London music hall star of the 1920s and 1930s** and an actress who owned her own repertory company. On 28 March 1921 at the Empire Theatre, Islington, she performed in her own play *Her Night of Sacrifice*. Her company was associated with **Collins Music Hall, 10–11 Islington Green** for over 20 years. The last play she produced at Collins, on 11 July 1938, was *A Mother Should Tell*, which she also wrote. She is buried at Streatham Cemetery (1963).

### Arthur Ernest Mullord (1910–1995) — "Arthur Mullard"

Arthur Ernest Mullord was an **actor and comedian** who wrote a book called *Oh, Yus It's Arthur Mullard*. Before showbusiness, he was a **butcher's boy** (1924), then joined the **army** (1930), and served as a **Drill & PT instructor in the Royal Artillery Light Aircraft Division** during WWII (1944). He was also a **prize fighter** (1943). He was buried at **St Paul's Church, Covent Garden** — the "Actors' Church" — in December 1995.

### Ernest Cooke (1912–1989) — Secret Beach Scout

Ernest Cooke served 1940–1946 in the **Corps of Royal Engineers** and was a member of the **COPPists — Combined Operations Pilotage Parties** — a top-secret naval unit that scouted invasion beaches ahead of D-Day landings. "They were given powder to cover their bodies to repel sharks and years later they discovered it was nothing more than chalk!" He also mined the Isle of Sheppey bridge in preparation for a possible invasion. He was awarded the 1939–1945 Star, Burma Star (Pacific), Defence Medal, War Medal, and **Military Medal**. After the war he was a pioneer of quick-frozen foods, selling to hotels like Claridges and the Grosvenor.

### Charles Henry Mullord (1874–1928) — A Tragic Boxing Day

Charles Henry met an untimely death on **Boxing Day 1928**. He was out searching for a pianist for a party when he was hit by a motor car. He was found beneath the vehicle and had to be extricated. He died two days later from shock caused by fractured ribs. A verdict of "accidental death" was recorded.

### Arthur Mullord (1877–1935) — South Africa Emigrant

Arthur was "interested in the newspaper business and went to South America looking for opportunities." He returned to Barnet, then emigrated with his family to **South Africa**, where he worked as a **Linotype operator in Johannesburg**. During WWI he joined a South African contingent and was wounded on active duty.

### Albert Henry Mullord (1890–1944) — Killed on Point Nepean Road

Albert emigrated to Australia and served in the BEF, VDC, and CMF. He was killed on Point Nepean Road, Carrum, on 29 April 1944. His wife placed an "In Memorium" notice in The Sun newspaper, Melbourne, in 1949: *"Sudden and sad was the parting / Bitterly cruel was the blow / In my garden of memories / We meet every day."*

### Dorothy Serbentat (née Mullord?) — Transatlantic Traveller

Dorothy made at least **four transatlantic crossings** between 1950 and 1954:
- 1950: Pan American Airways, London to Idlewild, New York (stayed at Mansfield Hotel, 44th Street)
- 1953: SS *United States*, Cabin Class, Southampton to New York
- 1954: RMS *Queen Elizabeth*, Cabin Class, Southampton to New York

### Henry Delafosse (1794–1845) — Bengal Artillery Officer

Henry's military career spanned 37 years across India and Indonesia. Cadet at RMA Woolwich in 1808, he served in the **3rd Mahratta War** and the **1st Afghan War** (1842), earning a CB. He died of sunstroke at camp in Lakki-ki-Dera, Ferozepur, Punjab, on 3 October 1845. A grave monument stands in Ferozepur cemetery. He married Louisa Maria Shield, eldest daughter of his second cousin George Maunsell Shield.

### Geographic Migration

The family shows a clear migration pattern:
1. **16th–17th century**: Newcastle-upon-Tyne (Young merchants, Sheriff)
2. **17th–18th century**: London establishment (via marriage to Maddison, Johnson families)
3. **18th century**: Richmond, Surrey (Huguenot hub — d'Abbadie, Delafosse families)
4. **18th–19th century**: Devon (Dodbrooke — the Rector line), Buckinghamshire (Iver — the Chilton connection)
5. **19th century**: North London concentration (Islington, Shoreditch, Hornsey, Finsbury — Mullord industrial expansion)
6. **19th–20th century**: Outward diaspora — India (military service), South Africa (Arthur Mullord), Australia (Albert Henry Mullord), New York (Dorothy Serbentat, Grace Ella Mullord, Ellen C Mullord)

## 6. Research Gaps & Opportunities

### Missing Data
- **218 people** have no recorded birth year; **491** have no recorded death year
- No marriage dates are recorded for any of the 221 families in the database (the marriage events exist but weren't linked as primary family events in all cases)
- The **Chilton** family is barely documented — only Dorothy Chilton (1730–1806) and her father Robert Chilton appear

### Promising Leads
- The **Cary connection** to Queen Elizabeth's court deserves investigation — the Visitation of England volume in the repository may hold details
- The **Lambton** connection to the medieval Hedworths and the broader Lambton family (Earls of Durham?) could be significant
- **Abney Park Cemetery** visits could yield gravestone inscriptions and confirm dates
- The **French Huguenot Church, Threadneedle Street** records may hold more d'Abbadie baptisms and marriages
- **Henry George Delafosse's** Cawnpore survival is likely documented in British India military records
- Ernest Cooke's **COPP** service may be documented in SOE/Combined Operations archives at the National Archives, Kew
- The **Richmond Academy** run by Daniel Charles Delafosse — its connection to Richard Francis Burton could be verified through Burton biographies
- **Collins Music Hall** archives may hold programmes and reviews of Hannah Dorothy Mullord's productions
- Several Mullords emigrated to **Australia** and **South Africa** — overseas records could extend those branches

### Data Quality Notes
- Some names appear in ALL CAPS (e.g., "YOUNG", "SERBENTAT") — likely imported from older GEDCOM files
- A few birth/death years appear corrupted (e.g., death year "2" or "19" for some Mullords — possibly truncated)
- Place names are inconsistently formatted (e.g., "Hoxton, middlesex" vs "Shoreditch, Middlesex")
- The three merged GEDCOM sources (from Julie Young's Family Historian) account for 767 of 836 citations — the tree's foundation is these earlier research efforts
