## Research Findings

### 1. Full Biography

**Arthur Ernest Mullard** (ne Mullord; 19 September 1910 -- 11 December 1995) was an English actor, comedian, and singer. He was born into a working-class family in **Islington, North London**, one of **five brothers**. He was known locally as "The Dook of Islington".

He stood **6 ft 2 in (1.88 m)** -- a physically imposing figure that served him well in boxing, stunt work, and character acting.

**Name change:** He changed one letter of his surname from Mullord to Mullard early in his entertainment career. The reasoning was strategic: the name "Mullard" was already familiar to the British public through **Mullard Limited**, the well-known manufacturer of radio and TV valves (vacuum tubes), founded in 1920 by Captain Stanley R. Mullard and later owned by Philips. The surname Mullard would be more recognisable and easier to remember than Mullord.

**Family:** He married **Florence Rose** in 1939. In the 1939 Register he was a general labourer living with his parents at **35 Douglas Street, Islington**. The couple had three children: **Brian**, **Barbara**, and **Johnny**. A blog post from 2012 also identifies a younger brother named **Jeremy Mullord**, whose wife was **Christiane Mullord** (living in Accrington, Lancashire at the time of writing).

### 2. Early Career: Butcher's Boy to Boxer

Arthur left school at **14** and started work as a **butcher's assistant** (the family tree records "butcher's boy" in 1924, which is consistent -- he would have been 14 in 1924).

He **joined the army at 18** (c. 1928), where he took up boxing and became **champion of his regiment**. After leaving the army, he fought professionally.

**Boxing record:** According to BoxRec and boxinghistory.org.uk, Arthur Mullard (Islington) was active as a professional boxer during **1931**, with **5 recorded professional contests**. Other biographical sources claim approximately **20 fights over three years**. The discrepancy likely reflects that BoxRec's database may be incomplete for the era, or that some of the 20 fights were unlicensed/booth bouts. His boxing career ended after a **knockout from which he temporarily lost his memory**.

The family tree dates his army entry to 1930 and lists "prize fighter" in 1943. Public sources suggest the boxing career was primarily in the early 1930s, prior to WWII, not during it. The "1943" date in the tree may reflect a different record or possibly unlicensed fighting during the war.

### 3. Military Service (WWII)

He **rejoined the army** for the Second World War, rising to **warrant officer (sergeant major)** in the **Royal Artillery**. The family tree specifies "Drill & PT Instructor, Royal Artillery Light Aircraft Division" -- this is consistent with published sources. Mullard himself later said: _"Drilling troops and just being in the Army was like acting to me."_

The family tree's detail about the "Light Aircraft Division" is more specific than any published source found. This detail may come from service records or family knowledge and adds to the public record.

### 4. From Stuntman to Actor

After demobilisation in **1945**, Mullard sought work as a **stuntman at Pinewood and Ealing Studios**. From stunt work he drifted into **uncredited bit-parts** in classic British films:

- **The Captive Heart** (1946) -- film debut, uncredited
- **Oliver Twist** (1948) -- uncredited
- **The Lavender Hill Mob** (1951) -- uncredited
- **The Ladykillers** (1955) -- uncredited

His imposing physique, distinctive face, and authentic cockney accent saw him graduate to credited character roles. He became in demand as a **straight man** to leading comedians:

- **Tony Hancock** -- multiple appearances in *Hancock's Half Hour* (1956-1960), playing roles including PC Trubshaw, Clarence, and various workmen
- **Frankie Howerd**
- **Benny Hill**
- **Spike Milligan**
- **Tommy Cooper**
- **Arthur Askey** -- *The Arthur Askey Show*

### 6. Television Career

**Major TV roles:**

- **Romany Jones** (London Weekend Television, 1973) -- played **Wally Briggs**, a crafty caravan-dweller, opposite **Queenie Watts** as his wife Lily. This was his highest-profile role and made him a household name.
- **Yus, My Dear** (LWT, 1976) -- spin-off sequel where Wally and Lily moved from their caravan into a council house. Featured **Mike Reid** (later EastEnders' Frank Butcher) as Wally's sponging younger brother Benny. The show attracted **more than 8 million viewers**.

**Other TV appearances included:**
- Hancock's Half Hour (multiple episodes, 1956-1960)
- Citizen James
- The Troubleshooters
- Till Death Us Do Part
- The Wednesday Play

### 7. Music Career

In **1978**, Mullard entered the **UK Singles Chart** with a novelty cover of **"You're the One That I Want"** (from the film *Grease*), a duet with comedy actress **Hylda Baker** (then in her seventies). The single:
- Peaked at **No. 22** in the UK charts
- Released on **Pye Records** (catalogue 7N 46121)
- B-side: "Save Your Kisses For Me"
- Taken from the album **"Band on the Trot"** (Pye PKL 5576, 1978), a 16-track LP produced by **Kenny Lynch** and **Rob Boughton**

### 8. The Autobiography: "Oh, Yus, It's Arthur Mullard"

- **Published:** 1977
- **Publisher:** Everest Books
- **Format:** 160-page paperback
- **ISBN:** 978-0905018317
- Copies are available through secondhand booksellers (AbeBooks, Amazon). This book is potentially the most valuable genealogical source, as an autobiography may contain family details, the Mullord name, Islington origins, and possibly references to Hannah Dorothy Mullord or music hall connections. **Obtaining a copy is recommended.**

### 9. Death and Burial

- **Died:** 11 December 1995, in his sleep, aged 85, in Islington
- **Funeral service:** 20 December 1995 at **St Paul's Church, Covent Garden** ("the Actors' Church") -- **confirmed by photographic evidence** (UPPA/Photoshot stock images of the funeral service, showing vicar and pallbearers)
- **Obituary:** The Times, 13 December 1995

The family tree's record of burial on 20 December 1995 at St Paul's Church is confirmed. Note: the churchyard closed to burials in 1852, so this was a funeral/memorial service rather than an interment. His actual burial or cremation location is not confirmed by online sources.

### 10. Post-Death Revelations

In May 1996, the Sunday Mirror published allegations about Mullard's private life. A planned *This Is Your Life* episode had been cancelled years earlier after producers contacted his eldest son Brian and learned of alleged domestic violence and sexual abuse of his daughter Barbara from age 13 (early 1950s). His wife Florence had committed suicide in 1961 (overdose of sleeping tablets). Her note reportedly said: _"I don't want to live any more because of what you're doing with Barbara. Please look after my Johnny."_

Mullard's youngest son Johnny, who became a comedian in Sydney, Australia, defended his father, attributing the allegations to a family dispute over money. Mullard left Barbara and one brother 5,000 pounds each, and 250,000 pounds to a children's home.

### 11. Hannah Dorothy Mullord and Collins Music Hall

**No online sources were found** connecting a "Hannah Dorothy Mullord" to Collins Music Hall or any music hall career. The searches returned nothing.

**Collins Music Hall** was located at **10/11 Islington Green**, in the same neighbourhood where Arthur Mullard grew up. It was a famous music hall that opened in 1863 (founded by Sam Collins, real name Sam Vagg) and closed in 1958 after a fire. Notable performers there included Charlie Chaplin, Gracie Fields, George Robey, and Tommy Trinder. The geographical proximity to the Mullord family in Islington makes a connection plausible, but it cannot be confirmed from online sources.

The family tree's claim that Arthur's mother was related to Hannah Dorothy Mullord, a Collins Music Hall star, remains **unverified but not contradicted**. This information likely comes from family oral history or the autobiography. **The autobiography is the most promising source for confirming this connection.**

## Cross-References from Confluence Wiki (added 2026-03-21)

The **Chronicles of the Mullord Family** (186K chars, written by Henry Mullord Jr.) in the Confluence wiki provides rich context for Arthur's background:

### The Mullord North London World

The Chronicles document the Mullord family concentrated in **Islington, Hoxton, Shoreditch, and Clerkenwell** — exactly the neighbourhood where Arthur grew up. The family's deep roots in this area (Penn Street factory, Abney Park Cemetery burials, multiple addresses across N1/N5/N7) mean Arthur's performing career emerged from a well-established local dynasty, not from nowhere.

### The Music Hall Connection

**Hannah Dorothy Mullord** (Collins Music Hall star, 1920s–30s) performed at Collins Music Hall, **10–11 Islington Green** — in the heart of Mullord territory. The Chronicles describe the family's proximity to Victorian entertainment: "The Grecian" (Eagle Tavern, City Road) is mentioned as "a Classically brilliant haunt." Arthur's path from the same neighbourhood into showbusiness follows a family pattern, not just individual ambition.

### No Direct Mention

Arthur Ernest Mullord (b. 1910) is **not mentioned by name** in the Chronicles, which were likely written before his fame. The Chronicles focus on the 19th century generations. His autobiography (1977) remains the key source for the direct family connection.
