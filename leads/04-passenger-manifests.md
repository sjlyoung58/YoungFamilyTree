## Findings

### 1. SS Philadelphia (Grace Ella Mullord, 15 Apr 1914)

**Ship Identity:** The SS Philadelphia that sailed the Southampton-New York route was the former SS City of Paris (1888), one of the most famous liners of the late 19th century. She was built by J. & G. Thomson, Clydebank, Glasgow, for the Inman Line. Key facts:

- Originally named **City of Paris**, launched 1888. She held the **Blue Riband** (fastest Atlantic crossing) in 1889-1891 and again 1892-1893.
- Renamed **Paris** in 1893 when the Inman Line merged into the **American Line** and she was transferred to US registry.
- Served as the auxiliary cruiser **USS Yale** during the Spanish-American War (1898).
- In 1899, she grounded on **The Manacles** off the British coast and was badly damaged.
- **Rebuilt at Belfast** with new engines, her three smokestacks replaced with two taller ones. Renamed **Philadelphia** and resumed North Atlantic service in **August 1901**.
- **Specifications:** 10,786 tons, 527' x 63', twin-screw, 19 knots. Capacity: 540 first class, 200 second class, 100 third class.
- Route: **Southampton - Cherbourg - New York** from 1901 onwards.

**April 1914 voyage:** According to sailing schedules issued November 1913, the Philadelphia was still operating the Southampton-Cherbourg-New York route alongside the St Paul, St Louis, and New York. She was transferred to the **Liverpool-New York** service at some point in 1914 (the next documented voyage is 17 October 1914, Liverpool to New York, under Captain A. R. Mills). The 15 April 1914 sailing from Southampton fits within the ship's known schedule on that route before the transfer.

**Important context:** 15 April 1914 was almost exactly two years after the Titanic sinking (15 April 1912). The First World War began in August 1914, after which the Philadelphia was requisitioned as the troop transport **USS Harrisburg**. She resumed commercial service in March 1920 and was scrapped in Italy in 1923.

**What the manifest might reveal:** Passenger manifests from this era typically recorded: full name, age, sex, marital status, occupation, nationality, last residence, destination address in the USA, nearest relative in country of origin, and whether the passenger had been to the US before. Finding Grace Ella Mullord's manifest would answer several key genealogical questions.

**No direct hit on the manifest itself** was found through web search. The actual manifest images are behind paywalls at Ancestry.com or available through the Ellis Island Foundation database (which covers 1892-1924 arrivals at New York). **Recommended next steps:** Search Ancestry.com or the [Ellis Island Foundation Passenger Search](https://www.statueofliberty.org/discover/passenger-ship-search/) for "Mullord" arriving April 1914 on SS Philadelphia.

### 2. SS Imperator (Ellen C Mullord, 11 Dec 1920)

**Ship Identity:** The SS Imperator was one of the most famous ocean liners ever built:

- Built for the **Hamburg America Line (HAPAG)**, launched 1912 at the Vulcan shipyard in Hamburg.
- At completion in June 1913, she was the **largest passenger ship in the world** (52,117 GRT), surpassing the White Star liner Olympic.
- 909 feet long, capacity of approximately 4,234 passengers across four classes.
- Shut up in Hamburg during World War I.

**Post-War History & 1920 Timeline:**

- After the Armistice, Imperator was turned over to the Allies. She was briefly commissioned as **USS Imperator (ID-4080)** and used to repatriate American troops.
- She was then allocated to **Cunard Line** as war reparations (compensation for the loss of RMS Lusitania).
- **11 December 1919:** First Cunard voyage, New York to Southampton.
- **21 February 1920:** First voyage from Liverpool to New York (her only Liverpool departure).
- **2 March 1920:** Left New York for Southampton; developed a severe list due to a faulty ash ejector. Cunard **withdrew her from service** for major overhaul.
- **July 1920:** Sir Arthur Rostron (famous for rescuing Titanic survivors as captain of RMS Carpathia) took command.

**The December 1920 question is critical.** Multiple sources state the ship was withdrawn from service after March 1920 for overhaul, and that she was then sent to Armstrong Whitworth shipyards for coal-to-oil conversion, resuming service in **May 1922**. She was renamed **RMS Berengaria** in February 1921.

**However**, Ellen C Mullord's record states she sailed on the SS Imperator on 11 December 1920. This presents a discrepancy with the standard narrative. Possible explanations:
1. The ship may have made some late-1920 voyages not captured in the commonly cited summaries (Rostron took command in July, suggesting some service resumed).
2. The date in the family records could be from a different source (e.g., departure vs. arrival date).
3. There may be confusion with another ship.

**Recommended next steps:** Search Ancestry.com for the actual manifest. The [GG Archives Cunard Digitization Project](https://www.ggarchives.com/OceanTravel/Passengers/Cunard/DigitizationProject2.html) lists westbound Cunard voyages 1912-1924 and may have the specific voyage. Also check the [Cunard Line timetable images](http://www.timetableimages.com/maritime/images/cun20i.htm) which show Cunard schedules from 1920.

### 4. Dorothy Serbentat -- Who Was She?

**No specific records found** for "Dorothy Serbentat" through web searches. The name does not appear in publicly indexed genealogical databases, news archives, or social media.

**The surname "Serbentat"** is extremely rare. Searches for the name's origin returned no results. It does not follow typical Serbian naming patterns (which usually end in -ic, -ov, or -ev), despite the superficial resemblance to "Serb." It may be of Central/Eastern European origin, possibly Lithuanian, Latvian, or French (the "-at" ending could be French). It could also be a phonetic anglicisation of a foreign name.

**Working hypothesis:** Given that Dorothy Serbentat appears in Mullord family records and made multiple trips between England and New York in the 1950s, she was likely a Mullord by birth who married someone named Serbentat. The passenger records listing her as travelling "Cabin Class" on the SS United States and RMS Queen Elizabeth suggest she was comfortably middle-class or above. Her consistent use of the Mansfield Hotel / 12W 44th Street address suggests she had a semi-permanent base in New York.

**Her crossings:**
- **5 May 1950:** Pan American Airways, London to Idlewild (now JFK), New York. Pan Am was operating Stratocruiser and Constellation aircraft on this route in 1950. Flight records from this era are held at the [University of Miami Pan Am Archives](https://merrick.library.miami.edu/specialCollections/asm0341/) and the National Air and Space Museum.
- **23 Jan 1953:** SS United States, Cabin Class, Southampton to New York. The SS United States was the fastest ocean liner ever built (launched 1952, held the Blue Riband). This would have been within her first year of service.
- **31 Mar 1954:** RMS Queen Elizabeth, Cabin Class, Southampton to New York. The Queen Elizabeth was Cunard's 83,673-GRT flagship, the largest passenger ship in the world at the time.

**All three crossings were westbound only in the records.** This may mean she returned by other means not captured, or the return records were not indexed in the family database.

**Recommended next steps:**
1. Search Ancestry.com for "Dorothy Serbentat" in New York passenger and crew lists 1917-1967.
2. Search for marriage records linking a Dorothy Mullord to a Serbentat (FreeBMD, GRO index).
3. The 1950s passenger manifests (available via Ancestry and National Archives through 1957) would list her age, birthplace, and UK address, which would confirm her maiden name.

### 5. Mansfield Hotel, 12 West 44th Street, New York

**The Mansfield Hotel still exists and is still operating as of 2026.**

**History:**
- **Built:** 1902, designed in the **Beaux-Arts style** by Renwick, Aspinwall & Owen.
- **Developer:** Former Vermont governor John G. McCullough and lawyer Frederick B. Jennings.
- **Original purpose:** An "apartment hotel" for well-to-do single men and childless couples, offering dining rooms, maid and laundry services, valets, and private physicians.
- **1925:** Opened to both men and women (previously a "bachelor hotel").
- **1940s:** Sold to Leonard Wolfe (1940), then to Leo Judson of the Hotel Mansfield Corporation (1941).
- **1950s context:** During the era when Dorothy Serbentat stayed there, the Mansfield was a residential-style hotel in the heart of Midtown Manhattan, between Fifth and Sixth Avenues, steps from Times Square and Grand Central. During the 1950s, **Max von Gerlach** -- believed to be the inspiration for Jay Gatsby -- was a resident. The hotel attracted theatrical and artistic personalities as well as businesspeople.
- **1994:** Renovated by Bernard Goldberg, who restored the ornate lobby decorations and the domed ceiling.
- **Landmark status:** The building is a **New York City designated landmark**.
- **Current status:** Operating as a boutique hotel. Listed on TripAdvisor, Booking.com, Yelp, and other platforms with recent reviews.

**Note on the address:** The lead file lists Dorothy's address as "12W 44th Street" -- the Mansfield Hotel is at **12 West 44th Street**, confirming this is the same location. This was clearly her regular New York accommodation. The consistency of this address across multiple years (1950, 1953, 1954) suggests either a long-term arrangement with the hotel or a personal connection to someone at that address.

### 6. Other Mullord Passenger Records

**The Mullord surname** derives from the Old English "mylen" (mill) + "weard" (guardian), essentially meaning "mill-keeper" or "miller." Variants include Milward, Millard, Millwood, Mellard, and Mullord. The name is chiefly found in the **West Midlands** of England and is rare -- not in the top 1,000 UK surnames.

**Earliest recorded Mullord:** John le Millard, 1279, Hundred Rolls of Huntingdonshire.

**No additional Mullord passenger records** were found through web searches beyond the three already documented (Grace Ella, Ellen C, and Dorothy). However, the rarity of the name means that any hit in a passenger database is very likely a genuine family member.

**Recommended next steps:**
- Search Ancestry.com broadly for all "Mullord" entries in passenger/immigration records.
- Search the [National Archives UK passenger lists](https://www.nationalarchives.gov.uk/help-with-your-research/research-guides/passengers/) (BT26/BT27 series) for outbound Mullord departures from UK ports, which would capture the other side of these journeys.
