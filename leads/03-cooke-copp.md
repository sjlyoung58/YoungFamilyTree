# Lead 03: Ernest Cooke — COPP Secret Beach Scout

## Status: Research Complete (Initial) -- 2026-03-21

## What We Know

- **Ernest Cooke** (1912–1989)
- 1940–1946: Corps of Royal Engineers, Army Number **2121422**
- Member of the **COPPists — Combined Operations Pilotage Parties**
- Top-secret unit scouting invasion beaches (D-Day preparation)
- _"Given powder to cover their bodies to repel sharks — years later discovered it was nothing more than chalk"_
- Mined the Isle of Sheppey bridge in preparation for invasion
- Awards: 1939–1945 Star, Burma Star (Pacific), Defence Medal, War Medal, **Military Medal**
- Post-war: pioneer of quick-frozen foods, sold to Claridges and the Grosvenor
- Later: Brooks Sales Manager, offered directorship
- Buried 1 Nov 1989

## Findings

### 1. ERNEST COOKE — FULL BIOGRAPHY (CONFIRMED)

**The COPP Survey website has a dedicated page for Ernest Cooke at https://www.coppsurvey.uk/ernest-cooke — this is an exceptional find.** The site was built from archival research and veteran testimony, and Ernest's granddaughter Jacky Mackenzie contributed information.

#### Personal Details (confirmed/expanded)
- **Born:** 16 March 1912, Mexborough, Yorkshire, son of a colliery engineman
- **Married:** Lucy Mary Mullord, 5 September 1936, Wood Green, Middlesex (she was known by her middle name, Mary)
- **Died:** 26 October 1989, aged 77, at Warwick Hospital
- **Buried:** 1 November 1989 (matches family records)

#### Military Service Timeline
- **12 Sep 1940:** Enlisted, given service number **2121422**
- Posted to **1 Training Battalion Royal Engineers (1 TBRE)**, then based at Shorncliffe near Coventry
- **8 Mar 1941:** Completed initial Engineers' training
- Posted to **262 Field Company** at Hastings
- **3 Aug – 28 Sep 1943:** Attended and passed the field engineer course at SME Ripon
- **31 Jul 1944:** Joined Combined Operations Pilotage Parties (COPP)
- **3 Sep 1944:** Completed navigation training; joined COPP Depot at Hayling Island
- **5 Nov 1944:** Sailed for the Far East
- **5 Dec 1944:** Arrived in Ceylon (Sri Lanka)
- Based initially at **Hammenhiel Camp** near Jaffna, Ceylon
- **Mid-Jan 1945:** Commenced operations in the Arakan (Rakhine State), Burma
- **23 Mar 1946:** Transferred to the army reserve

#### COPP 1 Operations — Ernest Cooke's War Record
Ernest served with **COPP 1** in South-East Asia. His usual canoe partner was **Captain Jack Crane, Royal Engineers** (Cooke was the paddler; Crane was the navigator/surveyor). Their operations:

| Date | Operation | Details |
|------|-----------|---------|
| 24-25 Jan 1945 | Reconnaissance north of Myebon | Immediately after arriving from Akyab |
| 25-26 Jan 1945 | Pyunshe chaung reconnaissance | Consecutive night operations |
| 2-3 Feb 1945 | Dalet chaung reconnaissance | Canoe 2 (Crane, David White, Cooke) — tasked to find channel and beach for landing craft |
| 9-10 Feb 1945 | Ruywa reconnaissance | Combined COPP 1 and COPP 4 operation |
| 11-12 Feb 1945 | Follow-up Ruywa reconnaissance | Further survey work |
| 16 Feb 1945 | Ruywa assault pilotage | COPP 1 provided pilotage for landings |
| 26-27 Feb 1945 | Letpan reconnaissance | Survey of possible main landing beaches |
| 2-3 Mar 1945 | Letpan follow-up reconnaissance | Survey of artillery landing site |
| 14 Mar 1945 | Letpan assault (Operation Turret) | Cooke and Crane led infantry to Easy Beach for the assault |

Jack Crane recorded in his diary after the Ruywa assault pilotage: **"Sgt Cooke and I now hold COPP record out here for having been on Jap soil five times and led in one assault."**

#### Military Medal — Citation Found
- **Gazetted:** 27 September 1945
- **London Gazette:** Issue 37284, Supplement, page 4781
- **PDF available:** https://www.thegazette.co.uk/London/issue/37284/supplement/4781/data.pdf
- **National Archives reference:** WO 373/98/60

**Full citation text** (signed by Paul Clark and Peter Wild):
> "Sergeant Cooke has taken part in five operations of a protracted nature in less than three weeks, and in enemy held territory. During most of these operations he was in very close proximity to the enemy but continued his reconnaissances with coolness, courage and skill. In cases where the information by this sergeant has been subsequently examined in daylight it has proved accurate. In addition he has assisted in the guiding of one assault."

#### Post-War Career (confirmed/expanded)
- After demobilisation on 23 March 1946, Cooke started working with fellow **COPP 1 member Robin Harbud** in the **frozen-foods industry**, based out of **Covent Garden in London**
- This confirms the family account of him being "a pioneer of quick-frozen foods"

### 2. WHAT WERE COPP — COMBINED OPERATIONS PILOTAGE PARTIES?

#### Formation
- Founded by **Lieutenant Commander Nigel Clogstoun-Willmott, Royal Navy**
- After the disastrous **Dieppe Raid** (August 1942), Admiral Lord Louis Mountbatten asked Clogstoun-Willmott to form a team for **Operation Torch** (invasion of French North Africa, November 1942)
- The team was initially codenamed **"Party Inhuman"** / **"Party Koodoo-Inhuman"**
- Success at Torch led to the formal establishment of **COPP in December 1942**
- Originally proposed to be called "Beach Intelligence Parties" but renamed for security

#### Organisation
- Headquarters: **Hayling Island**, Hampshire (specifically the Sandy Point beach club, now Hayling Island Sailing Club)
- Personnel drawn from: **Royal Navy**, **Royal Marines**, **Corps of Royal Engineers**, and **Special Boat Service (SBS)**
- Each COPP team was a small independent unit led by a Lieutenant RN (navigator/hydrographer) paired with a Captain RE (preferably Commando and SBS trained)
- Total unit: **fewer than 200 men** who won **over 90 medals and commendations**

#### What They Did
1. **Covert beach reconnaissance** of enemy-held beaches ahead of planned amphibious assaults
2. **Mapped enemy beach defences** and surveyed underwater approaches for uncharted hazards
3. **Took soil samples** to determine the load-bearing capacity of beaches (could tanks drive on them?)
4. **Assault pilotage** — on D-Day itself, guided the invasion fleet to the correct beaches using lights and beacons

#### How They Operated
- Deployed from **X-craft midget submarines** or **motor launches**
- Final approach by **folbot canoe** or **swimming**
- Wore rubber wet suits, carried minimal equipment (pistols, commando knives, waterproof writing tablets, compasses, soil-sample augers)
- Training at Sandy Point included: nocturnal canoe work, swimming to beaches, measuring gradients, testing beach compositions, infrared homing techniques, loading/unloading canoes through submarine torpedo hatches
- Also trained in unarmed combat, jungle warfare (at Elahara in Ceylon and Shimoga in India)

#### Major Operations
| Date | Operation | Theatre |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Nov 1942 | Operation Torch (North Africa) | Mediterranean |
| Feb-Mar 1943 | Sicily reconnaissance | Mediterranean |
| Jul-Aug 1943 | COPP 5 reconnaissance near Gioia Tauro ("toe" of Italy) | Mediterranean |
| Sep 1943 | Operation Avalanche (Salerno landings) | Mediterranean |
| Jan 1944 | **Operation Postage Able** (Omaha Beach reconnaissance) | Normandy |
| Early 1944 | Normandy beach surveys (all sectors) | Normandy |
| **6 Jun 1944** | **D-Day** — X-craft off Sword and Juno Beaches; motor launch pilotage for Anglo-Canadian beaches | **Normandy** |
| Jan-Mar 1945 | Arakan campaign (Myebon, Ruywa, Letpan) | Burma/Far East |
| 1945 | Rhine crossings | Germany |

#### Casualties
The Sicily reconnaissance in early March 1943 was devastating: **5 men killed, 6 captured** — only 4 of 16 men returned. COPP 3 was disbanded and merged into COPP 4.

#### The Normandy Reconnaissance (Operation Postage Able)
On 17 January 1944, the midget submarine **X-20** departed Gosport. **Major Logan Scott-Bowden** and **Sergeant Bruce Ogden-Smith** swam ashore at Omaha Beach, crawling in a "W" pattern to collect approximately 20 soil samples. They later returned on D-Day itself to guide landing craft and DD swimming tanks of the 741st and 743rd Tank Battalions.

### 5. ISLE OF SHEPPEY BRIDGE MINING

#### What We Found
The family account states Ernest "mined the Isle of Sheppey bridge in preparation for a possible invasion." This is **entirely consistent with documented British anti-invasion preparations** in 1940, though I could not find a specific document naming the Sheppey bridge mining.

#### Context — British Bridge Demolition Programme 1940
After Dunkirk, **all bridges on key defensive lines were prepared for demolition** by the Royal Engineers:
- Bridges were fitted with **demolition chambers filled with explosives**
- On receipt of the invasion codeword **"Cromwell"**, demolition parties would complete destruction
- The Royal Engineers drilled holes for charges in bridges across the country — "you can often still see bridges where the demolition chambers have been refilled with concrete"
- Over **50 stop lines** were constructed across Britain
- Waterways and bridge crossings were heavily defended and prepared with demolition charges

#### Sheppey Specifically
- The Isle of Sheppey was a key defensive position guarding the **Thames Estuary and London**
- **"Fortress Sheppey"** — the island bristled with gun batteries, harbour mine defences, and defensive positions
- **Defended Locations at Queenborough and Kingsferry** were established to control the crossing of the Swale
- The **Kingsferry Bridge** (a lifting bridge over the Swale) was the only road/rail connection to the mainland — mining it would isolate the island and prevent enemy use
- Nearby: the **School of Military Engineering at Brompton** (Chatham, just across the Medway) was the base for demolition parties — e.g. the documented Aylesford Bridge demolition order of 26 August 1940

#### 262 Field Company RE — Ernest's Unit
Ernest was posted to **262 Field Company RE** at Hastings after his initial training (completed 8 March 1941). This unit had extensive experience with **bridge demolition**:
- In the BEF in France (1940): 262 Fd Co cratered roads, laid anti-tank mines, and blew bridges during the retreat
- At Boulogne (22 May 1940): a party "remained out to blow the last bridge over the river before joining their unit"
- The unit was later involved in the Normandy landings and mine/bridge work in France

**Assessment:** Ernest's account of mining the Sheppey bridge is highly credible. The operation would have occurred in 1940-41 when he was with 262 Field Company or possibly during initial training. The specific bridge was almost certainly the **Kingsferry Bridge** — the only road/rail link between Sheppey and mainland Kent. This was standard Royal Engineers work during the invasion scare period.

### 6. BURMA STAR CONNECTION — CONFIRMED

Ernest Cooke's service with **COPP 1 in the Burma/Arakan theatre** (December 1944 - March 1945+) fully accounts for his **Burma Star** medal.

The Burma Star was awarded for operational service in the Burma campaign. Ernest's five reconnaissance operations and assault pilotage work in the Arakan (Myebon, Ruywa, Letpan) between January and March 1945 qualify him for this award.

COPP operated extensively in South-East Asia:
- Initially based at **Cocanada, India** (from October 1943)
- Moved to **Hammenhiel Camp, Ceylon** (from early 1944)
- Operations in the **Arakan** (western coast of Burma) from late 1944
- Final operations April-August 1945

### 7. SHARK REPELLENT / CHALK POWDER STORY

The family story: *"They were given powder to cover their bodies to repel sharks and years later they discovered it was nothing more than chalk!"*

I could not find this specific anecdote documented online. However:
- The US Navy's official shark repellent during WWII was **"Shark Chaser"** — a combination of **copper acetate and dark dye** packaged in cake form using a water-soluble wax binder, supplied to servicemen from 1943-1973
- Julia Child (before her cooking career) helped develop this at the OSS
- The substance was later found to be **largely ineffective** — which matches the spirit of the family story
- COPP swimmers in the Indian Ocean/Bay of Bengal (where sharks were a genuine concern) would have been given some form of protection
- The "chalk" detail could be a family embellishment, or it could refer to a locally-produced substitute used by COPP teams in Ceylon/Burma — this may be documented in *The Secret Invaders* or *Stealthily by Night* but is not findable in online sources

**This story is plausible and fits the pattern of wartime "reassurance measures" given to troops.**
